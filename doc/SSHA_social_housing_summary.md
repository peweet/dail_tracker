## What the SSHA is

The **Summary of Social Housing Assessments (SSHA)** is a statutory, point-in-time
count of all households that local authorities have assessed as **qualified for
social housing support but whose need is not currently being met** ("net need").
It is carried out annually by the 31 local authorities under **Section 21 of the
Housing (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2009** and the **Social Housing Assessment
(Summary) Regulations 2016**, compiled by the Local Government Management Agency
(LGMA) and analysed/published by the Housing Agency.

**Net need explicitly excludes:**
1. **Duplicate applications** — only the first application is counted where a
   household applied to more than one authority (2024: 436 duplicates removed;
   2025: 421).
2. **Households already in receipt of social housing support** — i.e. living in
   local authority or Approved Housing Body (AHB) accommodation, or supported via
   HAP, RAS or SHCEP.
3. **Households on a transfer list** — anyone applying to transfer from an
   existing support, including HAP.

SSHA homelessness figures **differ from** the DHLGH monthly homelessness reports
(different definitions, timing, household-vs-individual counts, IT anomalies).

Count history: annual since **2016** (first count Sept 2016; June 2017–2019;
November each year 2020 onward, moved to Nov due to Covid disruption and kept
since). SSHA 2024 = 9th annual; SSHA 2025 = 10th annual. Figures comparable back
to a 2013 assessment under the common legislative framework.

Policy anchor: 2024 report references **Housing for All** (90,000+ social homes by
2030, avg 9,500/yr new build to 2026). 2025 report references the successor
**Delivering Homes, Building Communities 2025–2030 Action Plan** (72,000 social
homes over the plan, avg 12,000/yr new build).

Income thresholds (since 1 Jan 2023, +€5,000): Band 1 €40,000, Band 2 €35,000,
Band 3 €30,000 (net income; +5% per extra adult up to 10%, +2.5% per child).

## Geography (local authorities)

- **Dublin City** has by far the greatest need: 12,682 households (21.2%) in 2024;
  13,002 (21.1%) in 2025.
- **Four Dublin authorities** (Dublin City, Dún Laoghaire-Rathdown, Fingal, South
  Dublin): 24,598 (41.0% of national) in 2024 → 25,684 (41.6%) in 2025 (+1,086,
  +4.4%).
- **Mid-East** (Louth, Kildare, Meath, Wicklow): 8,665 (14.5%) in 2024 → 8,865
  (14.3%) in 2025.
- **Cork** (City + County): 5,055 (8.4%) in 2024 → 4,718 (7.7%) in 2025 (−337,
  −6.7%).
- **Galway** (City + County): 3,174 (5.3%) in 2024 → 3,463 (5.6%) in 2025 (+289,
  +9.1%).
- **Limerick City & County**: 2,423 (4.0%) 2024 → 2,419 (3.9%) 2025.
- **Waterford City & County**: 1,419 (2.4%) 2024 → 1,458 (2.4%) 2025.

**Largest movers:**
- 2024 biggest **decreases**: Monaghan −16.5%, Kilkenny −15.4%, Meath −7.5%.
- 2024 biggest **increases**: Leitrim +22.7%, Donegal +19.0%, Galway City +14.6%.
- 2025 biggest **decreases**: Cork City −10.4%, Louth −9.2%, Laois −7.0%.
- 2025 biggest **increases**: Longford +39.7%, Leitrim +16.9%, Monaghan +15.5%.

(Note: 21 May 2019 Cork City/County boundary change means per-authority Cork
figures aren't comparable before 2019, though national aggregates are.)

## Year-over-year story (2024 → 2025)

- Net need up +3.0% (1,778 households) to 61,719 — second consecutive annual rise.
- Dublin's share edged up to 41.6%; Longford (+39.7%), Leitrim, and Monaghan saw
  the sharpest local increases; Cork City the sharpest drop (−10.4%).
- Homelessness rose on both measures (basis of need 16.1%; tenure 16.1%).
- Continued shift away from private renting toward living with family.
- Employment among applicants continued rising; long-term (7+ yr) waiters' share
  fell slightly.
- Sharp rise in refugee basis-of-stay within the non-EEA cohort.

## Data structure (for enrichment)

Each annual report = one national snapshot plus an **appendix breaking every
profile table down by the 31 local authorities** (Tables A1.1–A1.9), with
2-year (prior/current) comparison columns. The dimensions available per local
authority:

- Age profile of main applicant (A1.1)
- Employment status (A1.2)
- Source of household income (A1.3)
- Household size/composition (A1.4)
- Main need for social housing support (A1.5)
- Specific accommodation requirements (A1.6) + Traveller identifier (A1.6a)
- Current tenure (A1.7)
- Length of time on waiting list (A1.8)
- Citizenship (A1.9)

Machine-readable versions of the main + appendix tables for **all years** are on
the Housing Agency **Data Hub** (link above) — preferable to PDF scraping.

## Enrichment note (Dáil Tracker)

> **Flagged for enrichment — not yet built.**

Why it's relevant: SSHA is **local-authority-level civic-need data** with a clean
annual time series back to 2016. Dáil Tracker is organised around TDs and
constituencies; social-housing waiting-list need is a strong constituency-context
signal (housing is consistently the top national political issue).

Possible direction (subject to the usual pipeline rules):
- Pull the **Data Hub** machine-readable LA tables (avoid PDF scraping) into
  `pipeline_sandbox/` first (Polars), per [[project_pipeline_sandbox_rule]].
- Map **31 local authorities → Dáil constituencies** (many-to-many; needs a
  crosswalk — LAs and constituencies don't align 1:1).
- Surface as constituency/member context: waiting-list size, homelessness share,
  trend vs prior year, tenure mix.
- Note the **SHCEP / HAP / RAS exclusion** when describing "net need" so the
  figure isn't misread as total housing need.
- Privacy: SSHA is fully aggregated (no individuals) — no conflict with
  [[feedback_personal_insolvency_privacy]].

Open questions: LA→constituency crosswalk source; whether the Data Hub exposes a
stable download/API; how to present a non-1:1 geographic join honestly.

## Related programme references — SHCEP & capital schemes

> Naming caveat: the SSHA body text says "Social Housing **Capital** Expenditure
> Programme (SHCEP)" but its own glossary (and the Department) define SHCEP as the
> Social Housing **Current** Expenditure Programme — recurrent **leasing**
> payments, not capital build. There is **no programme literally named "Capital
> Expenditure Programme"**; the capital (new-build) side runs under **SHIP**
> (Social Housing Investment Programme), **CALF** (Capital Advance Leasing
> Facility) and **CAS** (Capital Assistance Scheme). Treat the SSHA wording as a
> report error.

**Census (CSO Census 2022) — population denominator / civic context**

The CSO Census is the strongest geographic fit for a constituency-organised app:
results are published down to **Small Area** level *and* aggregated by **Dáil
constituency**, so census provides the *population denominator* (and the same
tenure/Traveller/nationality dimensions as SSHA) — letting you express need
*per head of population* by constituency. Small Areas nest within constituencies,
so they can be aggregated up (solves the SSHA LA→constituency crosswalk problem).

- Census 2022 **Small Area Population Statistics (SAPS)** — 48 tables incl.
  housing/tenure, household composition, Travellers, nationality, age; **open CSV

## Potential app improvements unlocked by this data (2026-05-30)

Brainstorm, not a spec. The app is currently TD-organised; this data is mostly
**local-authority / region / Small-Area** scale. The biggest unlock is adding a
**constituency dimension** — not just "what is my TD doing" but "what does that
mean against the conditions in their constituency".

### 1. New page candidates

| Page | Data spine | What it answers | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Housing & Social Housing** (per constituency + per LA) | SSHA + HAP01 + HSA07 + Construction Status Report + Homelessness CSV | "What's the social-housing picture where I live — waiting list, supply pipeline, homelessness, HAP reliance?" | Most natural new page; pairs cleanly with member_overview |
| **Council Performance** (per LA, league-table view) | NOAC H1–H7 | "How is my council doing on vacancy turnaround, retrofit, PRS inspections, long-term homelessness vs other LAs?" | New civic-accountability angle; nothing in current app does this. Best feature in this catalogue not yet anywhere else |
| **Constituency Profile / Context** (per constituency, all dimensions) | Census SAPS + Pobal deprivation + RTB rents + above | "Who lives here, what do they rent, how deprived, what's the housing need?" | Becomes the reusable backdrop that every other page can sip from |
| **True Demand vs Stated Need** (national + per LA) | SSHA + HAP01 (PBO methodology) | Reproduce the PBO "ongoing need" figure live; show the gap between official "net need" and reality | Editorial / accountability piece; cite PBO |

### 2. Enrichments to existing pages

Rather than only standing up new pages, the data lets every existing page get
a "civic context" sidebar/footer keyed on the TD's constituency:

- **`member_overview`** — add a "constituency at a glance" panel: waiting-list
  size, HAP take-up, deprivation decile, recent housing completions. Frames
  every other section ("their attendance vs constituency need").
- **`votes`** — on housing-related votes, surface the TD's constituency
  housing-need backdrop next to their vote. Honest accountability: "voted X
  while constituency has Y on the list".
- **`legislation`** — when a bill touches housing, show what's being legislated
  *for* (national + constituency-level need).
- **`questions` / debates** — when a TD asks a housing PQ, surface the
  constituency's housing figures alongside. Currently the question shows in a
  vacuum.
- **`lobbying`** — when a property/REIT/housing firm appears on the lobbying
  register, show the constituency housing context of who they're lobbying.
- **`interests` / `payments`** — overlay deprivation index on TD constituencies
  to give silent context on the financial-disclosure picture.
- **`statutory_instruments` / `public_appointments`** — housing-sector SIs and
  appointments to housing bodies (Housing Agency, LDA, AHB regulator) get a
  housing-need context.

### 3. Cross-cutting capabilities unlocked

- **Geographic dimension.** First time the app has a real spatial story. Even
  without a map view, the constituency dimension lets every page filter / sort
  by area, not just by TD. (A constituency map view is a separate, larger
  follow-up.)
- **Historical trend.** HSA07 goes back to 1994; SSHA back to 2016; NOAC since
  2015 — 10–30 year per-LA series support "long-arc" charts the app doesn't
  currently have.
- **Reusable `constituency_context()` component.** A single render helper that
  takes a TD/constituency and returns a small card of housing+census+deprivation
  stats. Used everywhere; built once.
- **Editorial denominators.** Most raw figures (e.g. "8,787 homeless
  households") are useless without a denominator. SAPS + boundaries give us
  per-constituency denominators for honest framing.
- **Cross-source civic synthesis.** "Council says X% vacancy, waiting list
  shows Y need, RTB shows €Z median rent, Census shows W private-rented" — the
  app could be the first place that surfaces these together at constituency
  level.

### 4. Glossary / editorial wins

- New glossary entries: HAP, SHCEP (with the Capital-vs-Current note), AHB,
  RAS, SSHA, NOAC, SHIP/CALF/CAS, Direct Provision.
- Methodology footnote pattern when surfacing waiting-list figures (the
  HAP/RAS/SHCEP exclusions, the homelessness-count divergence — all already
  written up in the SSHA summary above).
- "Why this matters" copy sourced from Housing Commission / C&AG / Ombudsman /
  DFI for design-time editorial framing.

### 5. Honest limits — what this data **won't** fix

- **TD-level housing accountability.** Most data is LA / region / Small Area.
  We can show *constituency context* around a TD, not *what a TD personally
  did about housing in their patch*.
- **Allocations transparency.** The actual "who got the house and why"
  question — addressed by the FOI wording above — is **not** in any of these
  open sources. That's a data gap the catalogue documents, not solves.
- **Real-time / current state.** Almost everything is annual (SSHA, NOAC, HSA07)
  or quarterly (Construction Status). Monthly homelessness is the freshest.
- **Geography boundaries shift.** Constituency boundaries were revised in 2023
  and will revise again. Any constituency-level rollup needs versioned
  boundaries to stay honest across years.
- **Map UI is a separate project.** Mentioned above as an unlock, but adding
  a real interactive map of Ireland is a much bigger lift than the data
  pulls — flag as a deferred follow-up, not a same-sprint task.

## What story does the data tell? (2026-05-31)

Synthesis pulling SSHA 2024/2025, PBO Ongoing Need, NOAC, HAP Performance, and
the homelessness/construction CSVs into a single narrative. Not editorial
opinion — the patterns are in the numbers above.

### 1. The waiting list is rising again — after years of decline

The list fell from **90,800 (2016) to 58,824 (2023)** — a story politicians
could tell about progress. That story is now over: **+1.9% in 2024, +3.0% in
2025 (61,719 households)**, two consecutive annual increases. The decline is
clearly halted. The trajectory has flipped.

### 2. The official figure understates reality by roughly half

The headline "59,941 / 61,719" is *net need* — households on the waiting list
whose need is *not yet met*. The PBO's reanalysis adds the **53,571
households currently in HAP** (private-rental social-housing support, no
security of tenure) and gets **113,512 households with ongoing need at
end-2024**. **The "real" social-housing demand is nearly double the official
number.** This is the single most important caveat the app would need to surface
honestly.

### 3. The list isn't the stereotype anymore

Compared to 2016:

- **One-adult households 41.6% → 60.9%** (+19pp). The list is now overwhelmingly single people.
- **"Unemployed and on welfare" 59.6% → 46.9%** (−13pp). The dominant applicant is no longer the long-term unemployed.
- **Income from employment 21.1% → 24.9%** of applicants. **Employed households on the list rose by ~1,800 in 2024 alone** (+2.3pp in one year).
- **Over-60s 7.2% → 12.1%** (+5pp). The list is also ageing — more pensioners, more accessibility needs.

The stereotype of "large unemployed family" no longer fits the modal applicant.
**The modal applicant is now a working single adult who cannot afford private
rent.** That reframing alone has political implications.

### 4. The tenure collapse — private rental has stopped being a holding pattern

The most dramatic single shift in the data:

| Current tenure | 2016 | 2025 |
|---|--:|--:|
| Private rented sector | **67.4%** | **31.0%** |
| Living with parents / relatives / friends | ~25% | **37.6%** (now the largest tenure type) |
| Emergency accommodation / none | small | **16.1%** |

The private rented sector — for decades the "where do you live while waiting
for social housing?" answer — **has more than halved as a holding-pattern
tenure**. People have either been priced out, evicted, or moved in with family.
Couch-surfing with family is now where applicants wait. Emergency
accommodation has become a holding tenure for one-in-six.

### 5. Homelessness is accelerating on every measure

Three different fields in the SSHA all show the same direction:

| Measure | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
|---|--:|--:|--:|
| Basis of need = homelessness | 13.5% | 14.7% | **16.1%** |
| Tenure = emergency accommodation / rough sleeping | 13.5% | 14.6% | **16.1%** |
| Specific requirement: household member is homeless | 7,210 | 8,076 | **8,761** |

The DHLGH monthly homelessness count (March 2026 CSV) confirms the trajectory
in real-time, by region. **This is the fastest-growing single dimension in the
dataset.**

### 6. The demographic mix is changing — and the refugee inflow is now landing

The most under-discussed shift in the 2025 data:

- **Non-EEA citizens 9.6% (2023) → 13.2% (2025)**.
- Within the non-EEA cohort, **refugee status jumped from 22.9% (1,291 HH) in
  2023 to 39.2% (3,193 HH) in 2025 — a +147% increase in two years.**
- "Permission to remain in State" share fell from 70.5% to 58.2% inside the
  non-EEA group.
- **Direct Provision residents 1,799 → 2,080** (+15.6%).

The 2022 Ukrainian / Middle-Eastern refugee inflow is now visible in social
housing applications. This is a politically charged area; the data has to be
presented as fact (with the right denominators) to avoid being weaponised.

### 7. The Traveller picture is data-quality news, not population news

Self-identified Traveller households grew **124 (2022) → 512 (2023) → 1,044
(2024) → 1,844 (2025)** — but **44.7% (27,577 households) left the question
unanswered in 2025**. The growth is the identifier becoming more accurately
captured, not an explosion in Traveller homelessness. The true Traveller share
of need is almost certainly higher than 3.0% — we just don't know by how much.

### 8. Geography shapes destiny

The **headline** is "Dublin dominates" — 4 Dublin LAs = 41.6% of need, Dublin
City alone = 21%. But the **trends** are rural:

- 2025's biggest YoY increases: **Longford +39.7%, Leitrim +16.9%, Monaghan +15.5%**.
- 2025's biggest YoY decreases: **Cork City −10.4%, Louth −9.2%, Laois −7.0%**.
- PBO showed the HAP-vs-waiting-list mix is wildly different by LA (**Louth
  71% HAP-reliant; Kildare 32%**).
- NOAC shows per-LA performance gaps: some councils have vacancy rates near 0%,
  others near 7%; some re-let in weeks, others in months.

**The same official "need" has very different lived experiences depending on
which council manages it.**

### 9. Supply isn't keeping pace — and the pipeline is opaque to applicants

- The Q4 2025 Construction Status Report shows **3,151 schemes / 51,638 units
  in the pipeline**, but split across planning / pre-tender / on-site /
  completed stages. Only a fraction will land in 2026.
- The Housing Commission estimates national housing need (all tenures) at
  **33,400–81,400 units/year to 2050**; 2024 delivered ~30,300.
- For the SSHA waiting list specifically, even at planned build rates the
  current 61,719 will not clear quickly.
- An applicant has **no transparent view of where they sit in the queue** —
  the closest proxy in the data is "time on list", and **19% have been waiting
  more than 7 years**.

### 10. What the data refuses to tell us — and that itself is the story

- **No public allocations dataset** — there's no answer in any open source to
  "who actually got allocated a house, why, and ahead of whom". The proposed
  FOI wording in the catalogue is direct evidence that this is a deliberate
  transparency void, recognised by journalists, advocacy groups, and the PBO.
- **HAP-SSC raw data and SHCEP property-level data are restricted** — the
  detailed picture of how the £500m+ HAP scheme actually distributes is held
  privately.
- **The DHLGH monthly homelessness count and the SSHA homelessness fields
  give different numbers** — definitional gaps that haven't been closed.

### The single-sentence version

> *Ireland's social-housing waiting list is rising again after a multi-year
> decline; the people on it look less like the stereotype every year (more
> single adults, more working poor, more older people, more refugees, and now
> overwhelmingly stuck living with family rather than in private rental); the
> official 61,719 figure is roughly half the real ongoing demand once HAP is
> counted; homelessness is the fastest-growing dimension on every measure; and
> the most politically important data — who actually gets allocated a house —
> isn't published at all.*

## Expansion: the "no public allocations data" claim — what each source actually says (2026-05-31)

Walking back my earlier framing ("deliberate transparency void") slightly:
"deliberate" was overcooked — there is a real privacy/data-protection tension
(see DCC 2017 PIA below). But the underlying claim that **there is no
publicly-available dataset of who gets allocated social housing, why, and ahead
of whom** is documented in four independent sources. Verbatim quotes below.

### Ombudsman — *Investigation of the HAP scheme*, June 2025 (96 pages)

The Ombudsman's full HAP investigation report makes the transparency finding
explicit and formal:

> **"This investigation has found that there is in general a lack of
> transparency about how the HAP transfer system operates and what impact
> moving to the transfer list will have on a household's chances of securing
> standard social housing."** — Finding (Ch. 6, p. 63)

On allocation schemes specifically:

> "Although most local authorities publish a social housing allocation scheme
> online, **Tipperary County Council is one of only two that includes the
> percentage earmarked for the transfer list**. … In general, references to
> the proportion of social housing to be allocated to the transfer list do not
> feature or are vague." (The other authority is Dún Laoghaire-Rathdown, and
> even DLR only states that 70% goes to "the housing waiting list and the HAP
> transfer list combined" — no further breakdown.)

And on centralised statistics:

> "**The lack of transparency in how properties are allocated along, with the
> lack of centralised statistics on the matter make it very difficult to
> determine the true effect of moving onto HAP.** … As noted by the PBO, there
> is currently **no published report that sets out the total number of local
> authority tenancies in a given year**. This Office would support its
> recommendation that, to aid in transparency **detailed statistics relating
> to all forms of social housing are published**."

The Ombudsman is the formal accountability body for public-administration
complaints, and this is a *Finding* in the technical sense — i.e. a statutory
output, not opinion.

Source: https://assets.ombudsman.ie/media/285553/ec3ccad3-4f51-4196-bbea-0164b77e6e04.pdf

### Parliamentary Budget Office — *Ongoing Need 2024*, December 2025 (32 pages)

The PBO cites the Ombudsman approvingly and adds its own diagnosis:

> "An Ombudsman investigation into HAP in 2025 highlighted similar concerns,
> including:
>
>   - The absence of security of tenure for HAP households compared to other
>     social housing tenants; and
>   - **A lack of transparency in how HAP households are managed on waiting
>     lists.**" — §2 *Defining the 'Ongoing Need'*

The Ombudsman report's footnote confirms the PBO had **separately** already
flagged the same gap in its earlier (August 2024) paper: "*no published report
that sets out the total number of local authority tenancies in a given year*".

The PBO's §4 per-LA breakdowns are presented as **charts only** in the PDF —
the underlying per-LA numbers behind Figures 1, 2 and 3 are not exposed in
the document, which itself illustrates the data-availability point.

Source: https://data.oireachtas.ie/ie/oireachtas/parliamentaryBudgetOffice/2025/2025-12-11_ongoing-need-2024-the-true-demand-for-social-housing_en.pdf

### Focus Ireland — Prof. Michelle Norris / Dr Clíodhna Bairéad (UCD Geary Institute), September 2024 conference paper (15 pp.)

Norris's paper is — to date — the most detailed quantitative analysis of how
social-housing allocations actually work in practice. It was made possible
only because the Housing Agency *assembled* the administrative dataset for
academic use; it is not routinely published. Headline transparency finding:

> "**Operation of two parallel social housing systems and the lack of public
> information about allocations to people on the 'transfer list' raises
> transparency concerns. It also means that the social housing waiting list
> is not a meaningful measure of housing need.**" — Conclusions slide

The paper also publishes a quantified allocation share that is otherwise
nowhere in the public record:

> "**Homeless applicants make up 8.5% of the social housing waiting list but
> 13% of those allocated social housing.** People with disabilities were the
> only other applicant category who received a greater share of allocations
> than their share of the waiting list."

And on HAP:

> "The paper also provides useful information on **HAP claimants' access to
> social housing and indicates they receive a larger proportion of allocations
> than social housing applicants**."

Source: https://www.focusireland.ie/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Focus-Ireland-Conference-Michelle-Norris.pdf

### Focus Ireland — *Review of Dublin City Council Scheme of Lettings*, 2018 memo (7 pp.)

Earlier — and bluntly — Focus Ireland flagged the absence of public data as a
reason a major DCC allocations-policy change shouldn't proceed:

> "Many of the considerations we raise here in relation to homeless families
> may well also apply to homeless individuals and to childless couples … .
> However, **data on the role of social housing allocations to these groups
> is not available to us. The absence of published data on such a large group
> is further reason for putting the proposal on hold** and seeking expert
> advice."

And on a specific proposed change:

> "We are **not aware of any data on how many homeless families would qualify
> for social housing in the absence of homeless priority**, and without this
> it would be very unwise to rely on this measure."

On scheme-detail publication:

> "However, **the detail of this scheme have [sic] not been published** (would
> it only apply where HAP tenancies were terminated by the landlord, or would
> it apply even where there was a secure on-going tenancy in place?). Without
> the details of the scheme it is hard to assess its impact."

Source: https://www.focusireland.ie/wp-content/uploads/2021/09/Memo-on-the-proposed-DCC-Scheme-of-letting-priorities-FINAL.pdf

### Dublin Inquirer — *Councillors want sight again of who gets social housing in their areas*, 2025

This is the lived-experience side of the same picture — Dublin City
councillors lost their internal allocation reports a decade ago, and even
elected representatives can no longer answer constituents' questions about
how the list works:

> "**Council managers should go back to providing councillors with reports on
> who has recently got social homes in their areas**, according to a motion
> supported by the Protocol Committee. … Up to around 10 years ago, the
> council used to provide a report like this to councillors. **It stopped due
> to concerns about privacy and data protection.**"

Cllr Dermot Lacey (Labour), who tabled the motion:

> "**Councillors need more information to be able to explain to people how the
> system is working.** It isn't about advancing anyone's cause. It's about
> fully understanding the integrity of the process. … **So many people raise
> questions and concerns about how homes are being allocated.** When you saw
> the allocations, you could stand over that process."

And from an applicant, Sarah Lawless, 20 years on the list, currently facing
eviction:

> "**There is just so much confusion. The whole system doesn't make sense.**"
>
> Lawless said that after two decades, she's number 10 on the list. But she
> said she knows someone who joined the list 12 years later and is number 3.
> Both get HAP, are from the same area, and are approved for a four-bedroom
> home.

The article confirms the **legitimate privacy tension**: a 2017
council-commissioned privacy impact assessment by barrister David Fennelly
found that allocations data includes "**acutely sensitive cases, involving
issues such as domestic violence and witness protection**" and that disclosing
names and addresses presents a "heightened risk". So "publish the data" cannot
simply mean "publish names".

Source: https://www.dublininquirer.com/councillors-want-sight-again-of-who-gets-social-housing-in-their-areas-amid-confusion-about-how-lists-work/

### What I'd say now, more honestly

The earlier "deliberate transparency void" framing was too strong. What's
actually documented in these sources is:

1. **There is no centralised, published, anonymised allocations dataset** —
   Ombudsman finds it, PBO repeats it, Focus Ireland operates without it,
   Dublin Inquirer reports councillors can't access it.
2. **The information *exists* operationally** — local authorities make the
   allocations, HAP-SSC processes the tenancies, the Housing Agency has
   assembled administrative records for academic analysis (Norris/Bairéad).
   It just isn't released in a public, comparable, machine-readable form.
3. **There's a real privacy reason for the legacy gap** (DCC's 2017 PIA),
   which is why the Ombudsman's recommendation is for "detailed *statistics*"
   to be published — i.e. aggregated, anonymised — not for names.
4. **The Ombudsman has formally recommended publication** of those
   statistics. The DHLGH had not implemented that recommendation as of any
   source in this catalogue.

So the *honest* version of the original story-point is: "There is **no
published statistical dataset** on who actually gets allocated social housing —
a gap the Ombudsman, PBO, Focus Ireland, Norris/Bairéad and at least one
elected councillor have all formally objected to. Publishing aggregated
statistics (with small-cell suppression) is the Ombudsman's recommended fix
and the basis of the FOI wording in the catalogue above."

## Spotlight: Tipperary County Council — what the "transparency leader" actually publishes (2026-05-31)

The Ombudsman singled Tipperary out as **one of only two LAs** (with DLR) that
"includes the percentage earmarked for the transfer list" in its allocation
scheme. So what's the gold standard? Pulled and read the actual documents.

### 1. Allocation Scheme PDF — Section 1.4 (the line the Ombudsman cited)

The current [Tipperary Housing Allocations Scheme (Scheme of Letting
Priorities), 01/02/2024](https://www.tipperarycoco.ie/sites/default/files/2024-09/Tipperary%20County%20Council%20Approved%20Housing%20Allocation%20Scheme%20010224.pdf) (20 pp.) publishes this rule
explicitly:

> **"Ratio for Allocation of Vacancies — Where possible, 60% of allocations
> will be to applicants not in receipt of social housing supports and 40% will
> be allocated to applicants in receipt of social housing supports (approved
> for a transfer or in receipt of HAP). At least 20% of all allocations will
> be based on length of time and reserved for [length-of-time priority]."**
>
> Footnote: excludes allocations to existing RAS tenants with notice-to-quit,
> tenants in situ of purchased properties, and succession allocations.

That single sentence is genuinely unusual — most LAs publish only "the council
will, from time to time, as it considers appropriate, designate a particular
number or proportion…" (Ombudsman quoted Galway County Council's wording).

### 2. Section 3 — Full priority order (a–j)

Tipperary's scheme also publishes the **complete ranked priority order**, not
just a list of categories:

> a) dangerous dwellings / displaced by LA operations
> b) homeless (per Housing Act 1988 s.2)
> c) unfit dwellings (per Housing Act 1966 s.66)
> d) exceptional medical / compassionate grounds
> e) overcrowded / down-sizing
> f) disability (physical/mental health/sensory/intellectual)
> g) unsuitable accommodation — particular household circumstances
> h) dependent on Rent Supplement
> i) sharing accommodation, reasonable requirement for separate accom.
> j) unable to meet mortgage cost, no affordable alternative

Within a category, **length of time qualified** breaks ties (§3.2).

### 3. Choice Based Letting (CBL) — weekly published listings

Tipperary publishes the **actual properties on offer each week** via CBL.
Examples from local press (Tipp Mid West Radio):

- **Week of 13 May 2026:** 19 homes on CBL — four 4-bed + fourteen 3-bed.
- **Week of 14 Aug 2024:** 12 homes on CBL — ten 3-bed in Carrick on Suir,
  one 2-bed + one 4-bed in Clonmel.

CBL = approved applicants self-bid on listed homes, the council says it
"speeds up the allocation process and reduces the rejection rate". This is
**arguably the most transparent allocation mechanism in use anywhere in the
country** — you can see what's on offer in real time.

### 4. Aggregated waiting list by Municipal District (via press, May 2026)

Tipperary's CEO/council communications publish per-MD waiting-list breakdowns
periodically — picked up by local press:

| Municipal District | Approved applicants (May 2026) |
|---|--:|
| Thurles Templemore | 826 |
| Tipperary Cahir Cashel | 792 |
| Nenagh | 755 |
| Clonmel | 557 |
| Carrick on Suir | 338 |
| **Total** | **3,268** |

Council stock: **5,663 houses** (May 2026).

### 5. Delivery against target (published)

- **2025**: 232 new homes delivered vs **target 170** (137% of target).
- **2022–2026** projection: **1,061 vs target 887** (120% of target).

### 6. Voids Programme — vacant social homes brought back into use

National Voids Programme figures (Minister for Housing, May 2025) include per-LA
detail; for Tipperary:

- 2024: **88 vacant social homes re-let** (vs 100 in 2023).
- 10-year cumulative: **1,105**.
- Snapshot (end March 2025): **100 social homes currently vacant**, by MD:
  Tipperary Cashel Cahir 27 / Templemore Thurles 25 / Carrick on Suir 19 /
  Clonmel 15 / Nenagh 14.

### What Tipperary still *doesn't* publish

Even at the transparency leader, the gap the Ombudsman / Norris / Focus
Ireland flag is still there:

- **Allocation *outcomes* per category** — i.e. of last year's allocations,
  how many actually went under priority (a), (b), …, (j); how many to HAP
  transfer vs main list. The scheme publishes the *rule*, not the *output*.
- **Time-on-list distribution for households actually allocated** — Norris's
  finding that homeless applicants get 13% of allocations vs 8.5% of the list
  can't be reproduced for Tipperary from public data.
- **Refusal rates**, **CBL bid counts**, **median time from approval to offer**.
- **Per-applicant audit trail** (and shouldn't, for privacy — but aggregated
  cohort flow figures would be in scope).

### So is Tipperary actually the transparency leader?

**For the *scheme/rules* side: yes, clearly.** They publish:
- The 60/40 main-list-vs-transfer split (rare)
- The complete ranked priority order a–j
- Per-MD waiting list counts via regular communications
- Real-time CBL property listings (weekly)
- Voids per MD
- Delivery vs target

**For the *outcomes* side: no, not really** — same gap as every other LA. The
Ombudsman's specific compliment is about §1.4 of the scheme document; it
doesn't extend to publishing the actual allocations data.

**Implication for the app:** Tipperary's published material is rich enough to
build a "what does my council *say* it will do, and how is it tracking?"
feature today. The "who actually got the house" feature still hits the same
wall everywhere — including here.

## Council-by-council scan: what's actually published (2026-05-31)

Pulled the allocation schemes and operational reports for Galway City, Galway
County, Fingal, Dublin City, South Dublin and Dún Laoghaire-Rathdown. This
**substantially updates the "no allocations data is published" framing** — two
Dublin councils publish more than I'd realised.

Uses the **same vague template** the Ombudsman called out: *"makes provision for a proportion of dwellings … to be reserved for all or any of the following purposes"* — no actual percentages published. Does describe the HAP transfer mechanism with no minimum time-in-HAP criterion.
- **CBL portal**: [cbl.galwaycity.ie](https://cbl.galwaycity.ie/) — live property listings.

Uses the **literal "vague" wording the Ombudsman cited as an example** of the problem: *"the council will from time to time, as it considers appropriate, designate a particular number or proportion of dwellings for allocation to households transferring from other forms of social housing support"*. No percentages.
- **CBL portal**: [apps.galwaycoco.ie/CBL](https://apps.galwaycoco.ie/CBL/login.aspx).
- **Published outcomes report?** Not found.

### Fingal County Council

- **Allocation scheme**: [Allocations Scheme for Social Housing Support](https://www.fingal.ie/sites/default/files/2020-02/housing-allocations-scheme-document.pdf) — **adopted 15 June 2011**, never substantively updated. **The entire 13-page scheme has zero references to transfer-list percentages**; the only `%` figures are about income thresholds and refunds. This is the source of the bland "the HAP transfer list … will receive a percentage of dwelling allocations provided by the council" language the Ombudsman quoted directly from Fingal's FAQs.
- **Published outcomes report?** Not found.

### Dublin City Council — substantially more than I claimed

- **Allocation scheme**: [Housing Allocations Scheme 2018](https://www.dublincity.ie/sites/default/files/2025-06/adopted_allocations_scheme_2018.docx-converted-la.pdf) (30 pp.). Uses a **3-band priority system** (Band 1 = highest need, Band 3 = lowest). Within a band, allocations are date-order. Does NOT publish an explicit % split of housing vs transfer lists.
- **Housing Allocations Report (July 2024)** — [14-page detailed periodic report](https://dublin.moderngov.co.uk/mgConvert2PDF.aspx?ID=46176). **This is real published outcomes data.** Contains:
  - **Waiting list by Band × 10 Area Committees** (Areas B, D, E, H, J, K, L, M, N, P). Sample numbers: Band 1 total 528 / Band 2 total 7,722 / Band 3 total 6,717 = **Housing List total 14,967** (excluding transfer list).

## Why is citizenship breakdown of allocations not published? (2026-05-31)

Confirmed by exhaustive grep across every council allocations document gathered:
**no Irish council publishes a citizenship breakdown of who received social
housing**. The SSHA publishes citizenship for the waiting list (Table 2.9) but
not for allocations. The Norris/Bairéad UCD paper — the only academic
re-analysis using privileged administrative data — also didn't break down by
citizenship. The Ombudsman's transparency recommendations don't single it out
either. So *why*?

The sources don't say directly. Below is what can be cleanly attributed +
honest inference flagged as such.

### What the sources actually say

- **DCC's 2017 Privacy Impact Assessment** (barrister David Fennelly, cited
  via Dublin Inquirer) — the published rationale for *not* releasing names is
  the sensitivity of allocation casework: *"acutely sensitive cases, involving
  issues such as domestic violence and witness protection"*. This addresses
  name-level publication, not aggregated citizenship %.
- **The Ombudsman's recommendation** is for *"detailed statistics relating to
  all forms of social housing are published"* — generic. Doesn't specify
  citizenship as a needed dimension.
- **DCC Allocation Scheme 2018, §residency rules** confirms citizenship *is*
  collected and verified at application stage (non-Irish nationals must meet
  residency criteria). So the data sits in council systems — it just isn't
  surfaced in published outputs.

### Plausible reasons (inference — not directly stated in sources)

1. **No statutory reporting requirement.** The Housing (Miscellaneous
   Provisions) Act 2009 and the Social Housing Assessment (Summary)
   Regulations 2016 mandate certain SSHA fields (waiting list size, basis of
   need, household composition, etc.) but **don't require allocation outcomes
   to be reported by citizenship**. Councils therefore have no obligation —
   only a discretion — to publish it.

2. **GDPR / "special category" treatment.** Article 9 of GDPR treats data
   revealing *racial or ethnic origin* and (for refugees/asylum seekers) data
   revealing *political opinions / persecution status* as "special category"
   personal data, subject to stricter publication tests. Aggregated statistics
   with small-cell suppression normally clear that bar, but for smaller LAs
   small cohort sizes (a handful of refugee allocations per year) create
   real re-identification risk. Council legal/DPO functions default to "don't
   publish" when in doubt.

3. **Political weaponisation risk.** This is the elephant in the room. Ireland
   has seen a sharp rise in housing-focused anti-immigration mobilisation since
   the 2022 refugee inflow. Raw citizenship-of-recipient percentages,
   stripped of context (eligibility verification, length of residence, the
   asylum vs refugee distinction, population denominators), are highly
   exploitable. Councils, the Department, and oversight bodies appear to
   weigh that risk against the transparency benefit and lean toward
   non-publication.

4. **Absence from the oversight asks is itself a signal.** Notable that the
   Ombudsman, PBO, Focus Ireland, and Norris all explicitly critique the
   transparency void but **none specifically ask for citizenship breakdowns**.
   That's not accidental — civil-society researchers in this space appear to
   have made a deliberate choice not to push for a cut that could be
   weaponised, even though Norris had the admin data and could have published
   the analysis. Compare with the SSHA which *does* publish citizenship at
   the national waiting-list level — but never broken down by local authority
   in the public report. The pattern is: national-level fine, sub-national or
   per-allocation not fine.

5. **Operational vs reporting split.** Councils collect citizenship for
   *eligibility* (do you have the right to live here?), not for *prioritisation*
   (basis of need rules priority once eligible). From the council's POV,
   publishing citizenship of allocation would imply citizenship was a factor
   in allocation — which they'd contest. Need-based and time-based publication
   reinforces the official framing; citizenship-based publication undermines
   it.

6. **The Housing Commission report (2024) and Delivering Homes 2025–2030**
   plan both frame the housing challenge in terms of supply, demographics
   and structural demand — not in terms of who is housed. Citizenship-of-
   recipient data isn't a metric in any government framework, so there's no
   policy/political pull demanding publication.

### Honest summary

The full answer is **roughly half "real privacy/legal reasons" and half
"deliberate political risk-aversion"**, with the SSHA's existing
national-level citizenship table demonstrating that the data *can* be
published when the geography is coarse enough to depoliticise the figures.
The omission from the Ombudsman's recommendation list confirms this isn't
just oversight — it's a choice. Asking for sub-national citizenship-of-
recipient data via FOI would force councils to make the privacy-vs-
transparency trade-off explicit; some would refuse on small-cell grounds,
some might release at LA level with suppression. **It hasn't been seriously
asked for in public — by anyone whose ask would carry weight.**

| **SSHA 2025 Table A1.9** | **Same breakdown for all 31 local authorities + both 2024 and 2025**. e.g. Dublin City 2025: 8,346 Irish / 2,086 EEA / 2,519 Non-EEA / 51 UK = 13,002. Dún Laoghaire-Rathdown 2025: 1,933 / 196 / 290 / 1 = 2,420. | Per-LA | **Waiting list (need-not-met)** |

but cross-tabbed by LA — so per-Dublin-City, per-DLR, etc. **The waiting-list

### Notably absent

| Question | Public dataset? |
|---|---|
| Citizenship of **people who actually received** a social housing allocation in year X (national) | **No** — confirmed by exhaustive grep of SSHA + DLR + DCC + Tipperary + Galway + Fingal + SDCC reports |

The CSO has the raw data but doesn't publish the cross-tab |
| Sub-national / per-constituency / per-LA breakdown of any of the above | **No** |

That last row is the most striking. The CSO Census 2022 has nationality for
**every resident** and tenure for **every dwelling** — same form, same source,
same cleanup. They publish nationality by every conceivable other dimension
(age, sex, county, religion, ethnicity, work) and they publish tenure by every
other dimension (age, county, weekly-rent band). They **do not publish them
crossed together**. That's a deliberate editorial choice, not a data
limitation.

### Refined answer to "is there any public statistic that shows who receives houses by citizenship at national level?"

**No** — there is no public statistic of *who receives* (allocations or current
tenants) social housing broken down by citizenship at any geographic level.

**Yes** — there is a per-LA published table of *who is on the waiting list*
broken down by citizenship (SSHA Table A1.9). For 31 LAs, every year, four
buckets (Irish / EEA / Non-EEA / UK). For non-EEA, basis-of-stay
(refugee / permission to remain / subsidiary protection) is published at
national level (Table 2.9) but not at LA level.

So if the question is "who is *waiting* for social housing by nationality?",
that's answered. If the question is "who is *getting* social housing by
nationality?" — that data simply does not exist in public form anywhere,
including in CSO products that could have published it. The choice is
consistent across the entire data ecosystem.

## How does Ireland compare to other EU / UK on this? (2026-05-31)

The Irish gap is real but **not unique** — though it is starker than the UK's.
Comparative scan of UK, France, Germany, and Eurostat.

### UK (England) — the opposite of Ireland

The UK has **exactly what Ireland doesn't have** and publishes it routinely.

- **CORE (COntinuous REcording of Lettings and Sales in Social Housing in
  England)** — administered by DLUHC (now MHCLG). It's described as a
  *"complete census of new social housing lettings"*, not a survey. It records
  ethnicity and nationality of the lead tenant for every new social-housing
  tenancy and sale.
- **GOV.UK Ethnicity Facts and Figures — Social Housing Lettings**
  ([service page](https://www.ethnicity-facts-figures.service.gov.uk/housing/social-housing/social-housing-lettings/latest/)) — publishes annual breakdowns:
  - **Ethnicity of lead tenant**: e.g. Black ethnic group = 7.8% of new
    lettings (vs 3.9% of England's population aged 16+); Asian = 4.9% (vs
    9.0% of population).
  - **Nationality of lead tenant** in 2024/25 (England):
    **UK nationals 89% (233,000) / European 4% (10,000) / non-EEA 8% (20,000)**.
  - Plus: routes into social housing by ethnicity, income spent on rent by
    ethnicity, vulnerable households by ethnicity, etc.
- Available as downloadable CSV. **The Inside Housing trade press writes
  regular analytical commentary on the figures** — meaning the data is
  routinely used in public policy debate without political collapse.

The UK's example puts Ireland's omission in sharp relief: this can be done.

### France — publishes, with a constitutional twist

France has a constitutional/legal prohibition on collecting **"ethnic"
statistics** (the "republican model"), but **country of birth and immigrant
origin are permitted and published**. INSEE has published HLM (social housing)
breakdowns for years:

- 2016: **31% of households whose reference person was born abroad live in
  social housing**, vs **14% of households whose reference person was born in
  France**.
- 2016: **50% of sub-Saharan African households / 39% of Turkish / 38% of
  Maghrebi households are in HLM** social housing.
- 2022: **30% of immigrants live in HLM vs 11% of non-immigrants**.

These are routine INSEE outputs ([example](https://www.insee.fr/fr/statistiques/3635547))
— published at national level by immigrant/country-of-origin category, not by
ethnicity per se. The legal framing differs from the UK; the **practical
result is similar transparency**.

### Eurostat — covers Ireland too, just at survey-level

This is the most directly relevant finding for the original question: **the
data the Irish government doesn't publish, Eurostat does — for Ireland, in
EU-SILC**.

- Dataset **[ilc_lvho02](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/databrowser/view/ilc_lvho02/default/table?lang=en) — Distribution of population by tenure status,
  type of household and income group** — supports breakdowns by tenure ×
  citizenship × country of birth × age × NUTS region.
- Includes the bucket **"Tenant, rent at reduced price or free"** — which
  EU-SILC uses as the proxy for social/subsidised housing.
- Ireland is **ranked first among EU-15 countries** for the share of
  population identified as *"tenant, rent at reduced price or free"*.
- The Eurostat "Migrant integration statistics — housing" page publishes
  EU-wide cross-tabs of overcrowding rate, housing cost overburden, and
  housing deprivation by citizenship — comparing nationals to non-nationals
  for every member state (Ireland included).

The big caveat: EU-SILC is a **sample survey** (~6,000 Irish households/year),
not the administrative-data census the SSHA waiting-list table is. So
Eurostat's Ireland citizenship-tenure cross-tab exists but is:
- **Coarser categories** (nationals / EU-non-nationals / non-EU only)
- **National-level only** for cross-tabs (NUTS region available but with
  much wider confidence intervals)
- **Survey-uncertainty** rather than census-precision
- Published with a **1-2 year lag**

### Germany — closer to Ireland's position

Germany collects "Migrationshintergrund" (migration background) in many
contexts and federal/state housing research papers analyse social-housing
access by this dimension, but I didn't find a **standing administrative
publication** equivalent to the UK's CORE. The federal structure (Länder run
their own housing programmes) likely fragments publication, similar to
Ireland's 31 councils. Closer to Ireland than to the UK on the transparency
spectrum, though the absence of a single landlord-of-record dataset is
partly structural.

### Comparative scoreboard

| Jurisdiction | Citizenship/origin of recipients published nationally? | Citizenship of waiting list published? | Sub-national breakdown? | Cadence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **UK (England)** | **Yes — CORE annual** (ethnicity + nationality, downloadable) | Yes (by various routes) | Yes — to LA district level | Annual |
| **France** | **Yes — INSEE** (country of birth / immigrant origin; "ethnic" data forbidden) | Yes via Census | Yes — partial | Multi-year |
| **Eurostat (covers IE)** | **Yes — EU-SILC for all EU-27** at country level | Indirect | NUTS region with caveats | Annual, 1-2yr lag |
| **Germany** | Mostly no (research-paper level, not standing publication) | Partial | Mostly no | n/a |
| **Ireland** | **No** — gap confirmed across SSHA, all 31 councils, CSO Census, CSO HAP, Norris | **Yes — SSHA Table 2.9 + A1.9** (national + per-LA) | Per-LA for waiting list; nothing for recipients | Annual (waiting list) |

### Honest verdict — is Ireland's omission specific or part of a broader trend?

**Both, with caveats:**

- **Ireland is genuinely an outlier compared to its closest comparator (UK).**
  CORE in England publishes — annually, in detail, downloadable — exactly the
  kind of data Irish bodies don't. The two countries share a legal heritage,
  a Common Travel Area, very similar housing systems, and similar GDPR
  obligations. The UK's example demonstrates that "we can't because GDPR /
  privacy / political sensitivity" is not the binding constraint it's
  sometimes presented as.

- **Ireland is closer to the continental European norm.** Germany doesn't
  publish a CORE-equivalent either; the centralised admin dataset model is
  partly an Anglo-Saxon feature. France publishes via INSEE but uses
  "immigrant origin" rather than "ethnicity" — and France is the only major
  continental country with detailed routine published cross-tabs of social
  housing by origin.

- **Ireland *is* in the Eurostat data** via EU-SILC, just at a coarser,
  survey-based level the Irish state doesn't surface domestically. If you
  want a country-level citizenship-of-subsidised-housing-occupant figure for
  Ireland today, Eurostat is where to look — not the Housing Agency, not
  the CSO, not DHLGH.

- **The political sensitivity is real but isn't uniquely Irish.** The UK
  publishes the same dimensions without policy collapse — though UK political
  weaponisation of housing-and-migration data certainly happens. France's
  immigrant-origin framing was developed *because* of political sensitivity,
  not despite it; it's a deliberate compromise.

- **The deepest difference** is institutional: the UK has CORE (one
  centralised system everyone reports to), France has INSEE as a powerful
  central producer, Eurostat has EU-SILC. Ireland has SSHA (covers waiting
  list, not allocations) + 31 council datasets that don't aggregate. The
  Irish gap is structural as much as ideological — though the choice not to
  build a CORE-equivalent for allocations is itself a structural choice that
  could be reversed.

So the most accurate framing: **Ireland's gap isn't unique in Europe, but it
is starker than the UK's, and the gap is filled internationally (Eurostat) at
a coarser level the Irish state itself doesn't surface domestically.**

## Eurostat deep-dive for Ireland — what's actually in the data (2026-05-31)

Verified by hitting the Eurostat JSON-stat API directly. Important correction
to the earlier claim: **the dataset I named (`ilc_lvho02`) does NOT have a
citizenship dimension**. The actual dataset that crosses tenure with
citizenship is **`ilc_lvps15`** — but it only splits *Owner vs Tenant*, not

rent (`ilc_lvho28`) doesn't have citizenship. **There is no Eurostat dataset
that crosses social-housing-tenure with citizenship**, for Ireland or any
other member state. Walking back the earlier overstatement.

|---|--:|--:|

### What Eurostat *doesn't* tell us about Ireland

- **Cannot distinguish social rent from private rent** within the citizenship
  cross-tab. So we cannot answer "what % of social housing tenants are
  non-Irish citizens" from Eurostat.
- **Country-level only** — no LA, no constituency, no urban/rural split for
  the citizenship × tenure cross-tab. Some other dimensions (NUTS region)
  exist but at survey sample sizes too small for confident sub-national
  comparison.
- **Survey not census** — ~6,000 Irish households per year. Confidence
  intervals widen as you slice finer.
- **No allocation/flow data** — only stock at survey time. Eurostat shows
  who currently lives in what, not who got allocated what during the year.

### The honest summary on Eurostat for Ireland

- The question "**what's the citizenship breakdown of who currently rents
  social housing in Ireland**" is **not directly answered** by Eurostat,
  contrary to what I claimed earlier.
- The question "**how do housing outcomes differ between Irish nationals and
  non-nationals in Ireland**" *is* answered, and the gap is enormous:
  - ~4× ownership gap (Irish 80% vs non-national ~30%)
  - 5–9× cost-overburden gap
  - 3–6× overcrowding gap
- These are EU-comparable indicators, available annually since 2003-ish,
  free to download via the [Eurostat data browser](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/databrowser/view/ilc_lvps15/default/table)
  or JSON-stat API.
- For the "social housing recipient by citizenship" question specifically,
  **no public data source exists at any level — Irish, UK-style, or
  EU-comparable**. The CSO has the raw Census data to compute it; INSEE in
  France publishes the equivalent; the UK's CORE publishes the
  newly-allocated equivalent for England. Ireland and Eurostat both stop one
  step short.

## Deep-dive: how do housing outcomes differ between Irish and non-Irish in Ireland? (2026-05-31)

All numbers verified by direct pull from the Eurostat JSON-stat API. Free,
reproducible. The picture is unusually clean.

### The headline 9× cost-overburden gap, explained

"Housing cost overburden" is the EU-SILC standard measure: **% of adults in
households spending more than 40% of disposable income on housing costs**
(rent or mortgage + utilities + maintenance + structural insurance).

**Ireland 2025 (`ilc_lvho25`):**

| Citizenship | Overburden rate | Multiple of Irish rate |
|---|--:|--:|
| Irish (national) | **2.5%** | 1.0× |
| EU27 non-Irish | 13.0% | 5.2× |
| Non-EU citizens | **22.6%** | **9.0×** |
| Any foreign citizenship | 17.7% | 7.1× |

That **9× gap is not normal by EU standards**. Compare the EU27 average for
the same year:

| Citizenship | EU27 avg overburden | Multiple of EU national rate |
|---|--:|--:|
| National | 7.1% | 1.0× |
| EU27 non-national | 15.2% | 2.1× |
| Non-EU | 18.2% | **2.6×** |

So the EU-wide gap is 2.6× nationals → Ireland's gap is **9.0× nationals**.
**Ireland's housing-cost gap by citizenship is roughly 3.5× as wide as the EU
average gap.** The actual non-national absolute rate (22.6%) is high but not
extreme — it's the Irish-national rate (2.5%) being so low that drives the
ratio.

### The gap has widened (2015 → 2025)

Housing cost overburden by citizenship, Ireland time series (% of adults):

| Citizenship | 2015 | 2018 | 2020 | 2022 | 2024 | 2025 |
|---|--:|--:|--:|--:|--:|--:|
| Irish | 3.5 | 2.5 | 2.3 | 2.6 | 2.8 | **2.5** |
| EU non-Irish | 11.3 | 9.7 | 16.3 | 9.2 | 9.6 | 13.0 |
| Non-EU | 16.8 | 14.6 | 18.8 | 14.1 | 18.5 | **22.6** |
| Foreign | 13.1 | 11.4 | 17.2 | 11.0 | 13.6 | 17.7 |
| **Ratio (Non-EU / Irish)** | 4.8× | 5.8× | 8.2× | 5.4× | 6.6× | **9.0×** |

Irish nationals' overburden rate actually *improved* slightly over the decade
(3.5% → 2.5%). The **non-EU rate worsened sharply, especially in 2025**
(18.5% → 22.6% in one year — the largest jump on record). 2025 is the **worst
year in the series** for non-EU cost-overburden in Ireland.

### Overcrowding tells the same story (and doubled in 10 years)

Overcrowding rate (% of adults in households below the EU standard for rooms
per person), Ireland time series:

| Citizenship | 2015 | 2018 | 2022 | 2025 |
|---|--:|--:|--:|--:|
| Irish | 2.5 | 1.3 | 2.0 | **2.6** |
| EU non-Irish | 7.0 | 10.2 | 14.7 | **14.9** (5.7× Irish) |
| Non-EU | 4.2 | 7.5 | 15.5 | **9.7** (3.7× Irish) |
| Foreign | 6.1 | 9.3 | 15.0 | **12.4** (4.8× Irish) |

Foreign-citizen overcrowding has **doubled in 10 years** (6.1% → 12.4%);
Irish-national overcrowding is essentially flat. EU comparison:

| | EU27 avg 2025 | Ireland 2025 |
|---|--:|--:|
| National | 13.6% | 2.6% |
| Non-EU | 33.5% | 9.7% |
| Ratio | 2.5× | **3.7×** |

Same pattern: Ireland's absolute numbers are lower across the board, but the
**gap is wider** than the EU average.

### Tenure — the structural driver

Owner vs Tenant share by citizenship, Ireland 2025 (`ilc_lvps15`):

| Citizenship | Owner | Tenant |
|---|--:|--:|
| Irish | 80.1% | 19.9% |
| EU non-Irish | 28.9% | **71.1%** |
| Non-EU | 33.9% | **66.1%** |

Tenant share by citizenship, time series, Ireland:

| Citizenship | 2015 | 2018 | 2020 | 2022 | 2024 | 2025 |
|---|--:|--:|--:|--:|--:|--:|
| Irish | 21.9 | 20.7 | 22.3 | 23.0 | 23.3 | **19.9** |
| EU non-Irish | 84.6 | 80.1 | 76.4 | 77.0 | 64.6 | 71.1 |
| Non-EU | 50.5 | 57.3 | 59.5 | 53.3 | 64.1 | **66.1** |

This is the **structural reason for everything above**. Irish nationals are
overwhelmingly owners and therefore not exposed to private-rent inflation.
Non-Irish are overwhelmingly tenants and fully exposed to it. Ireland has had
some of the steepest rent inflation in the EU since 2015. The 9× cost gap is
essentially **the gap between "people who bought before the rental-price
escalation" and "people who arrived during it"**.

### Critical insight: the gap is *specifically* a housing gap, not a general living-standards gap

This is the cleanest finding in the dataset. Same 2025 cohort, three measures:

| Indicator (Ireland 2025) | Irish | Non-EU | Multiple |
|---|--:|--:|--:|
| Housing cost overburden | 2.5% | 22.6% | **9.0×** |
| Overcrowding | 2.6% | 9.7% | 3.7× |
| At-risk-of-poverty rate (income) | 10.4% | 23.0% | 2.2× |
| **Severe material & social deprivation** | **4.4%** | **4.3%** | **≈ 1.0×** |

Non-Irish in Ireland are not dramatically poorer on a basic-deprivation
measure (4.4% vs 4.3% — essentially identical). They are modestly more
income-poor (2.2× gap). They are being **squeezed specifically by the housing
market**, at a rate massively disproportionate to their broader economic
disadvantage. **This is a tenure-driven inequality, not a poverty-driven
inequality.** That distinction matters for policy framing — it implies the
problem isn't that non-Irish in Ireland are poor; it's that the housing
market makes being a tenant in Ireland extremely punishing, and non-Irish
are disproportionately tenants.

### Connection back to SSHA findings

This Eurostat picture aligns with — and contextualises — what's visible in
the SSHA waiting-list data:

- SSHA: non-EEA share of the social housing waiting list rose from
  **9.6% (2023) to 13.2% (2025)** (Table 2.9).
- SSHA: within non-EEA, **refugee status jumped from 1,291 to 3,193
  households (+147%) in two years**.
- Eurostat: non-EU residents are 66% tenants in Ireland (vs Irish 20%)
  and 9× more likely to be cost-overburdened.
- The combination: the population most exposed to private-rent inflation
  (non-EU tenants) is also the population whose social-housing demand is
  rising fastest. The two datasets corroborate one another.

### Honest survey caveats

- All figures from EU-SILC are from a **survey of ~6,000 Irish households per
  year**. Confidence intervals widen on the foreign-citizen subgroups
  (smaller cell sizes — that 2021 spike of "non-EU = 5.6%" overburden is
  almost certainly a sampling artefact, not a real one-year improvement).
- "Citizenship" classifies people by *current* legal nationality.
  Naturalised people who came to Ireland as foreigners and now hold Irish
  passports are counted as "Reporting country" (Irish). So the "Irish"
  category is not "ethnically Irish" — it includes naturalised residents.
- "Country of birth" is a separate Eurostat dimension that captures
  foreign-born regardless of current passport, and that's where the bigger
  population is (Census 2022: ~12% of Irish residents are non-Irish citizens,
  but ~20% are foreign-born). Country-of-birth versions of these indicators
  also exist on Eurostat and would show somewhat wider populations.
- Non-EU includes refugees, students, IT workers, healthcare workers,
  long-term residents — wildly different sub-populations lumped together.
- Eurostat overburden ratio differs from the SSHA definition of "in housing
  need" — they measure different things and shouldn't be added.

### The single sentence

**Non-Irish residents in Ireland are 9× more likely than Irish nationals to
be spending over 40% of their income on housing — a gap roughly 3.5× wider
than the EU average and the worst on record, driven not by poverty (which
shows only a 2× gap) but by tenure: 80% of Irish nationals own, 66% of
non-EU residents rent, and Ireland has had some of Europe's steepest
rent inflation.**

## Story map — what could actually be told (2026-05-31)

Synthesis of everything above into the distinct narratives the data supports.
Each story rated on **data strength** (how solid the evidence is) and
**framing risk** (likelihood of misreading/weaponisation). Pick the ones that
match the editorial voice; skip the rest.

### Tier 1 — Defensible headline stories (low framing risk, high data strength)

**1. "The list is rising again — after years of decline"**
- *Data:* SSHA national totals 2016 → 2025. 90,800 → 58,824 → 59,941 → 61,719.
- *Hook:* The "progress" narrative is over. Two consecutive annual increases reverse a multi-year decline.
- *Visuals:* Single 10-year line chart; one number for the year-on-year delta; per-LA league table of biggest movers.

**2. "The headline figure is roughly half the real demand"**
- *Data:* PBO Ongoing Need 2024 — 59,941 SSHA + 53,571 active HAP = 113,512 households.
- *Hook:* HAP tenancies are private rental with no security of tenure; counting them as "social housing" disguises the real need.
- *Hazard:* needs the PBO methodology footnote attached. Don't claim it as DHLGH official; it's the PBO's framing.

**3. "Who's on the list isn't who you think"**
- *Data:* SSHA tables 2.1, 2.2, 2.3, 2.4.
- *Numbers:* one-adult households 41.6% (2016) → 60.9% (2025); unemployed share 59.6% → 46.9%; over-60s 7.2% → 12.1%.
- *Hook:* The modal applicant is now a **single, working adult who can't afford private rent**, not the unemployed family of stereotype.
- *Visuals:* "Then vs now" mosaic across four dimensions.

**4. "Homelessness is the fastest-growing dimension on every measure"**
- *Data:* SSHA basis-of-need (13.5%→16.1%), tenure emergency-accom (13.5%→16.1%), specific accommodation requirement homeless (+21% in 2 years), DHLGH monthly homelessness count.
- *Hook:* Three independent fields, same trajectory; not a measurement artefact.
- *Visuals:* The three series on one chart, all pointing up.

### Tier 2 — Accountability / civic-tech stories

**5. "Postcode lottery: same need, different odds"**
- *Data:* SSHA per-LA waiting list, PBO per-LA HAP-vs-list-mix (Louth 71% HAP, Kildare 32%), NOAC H1–H7 per-LA performance indicators, council allocation outcomes (where published).
- *Hook:* Your chance of being housed — and how long it takes — depends heavily on which council manages your application.
- *Visuals:* Map (where boundaries exist) or LA scoreboard; per-LA comparator cards.
- *Caveat:* LA ≠ constituency; needs the M:N crosswalk and honest framing.

**6. "What your council publishes — and what it hides"**
- *Data:* The council-by-council scan above (DLR full transparency / DCC area-level detail / Tipperary the rule / Galway, Fingal, SDCC the void) + Ombudsman, PBO, Focus Ireland and Dublin Inquirer evidence on the data gap.
- *Hook:* Three councils have built models for what transparency looks like; the other 28 haven't followed.
- *Visuals:* A "publishes / doesn't" grid like the council scoreboard; pull-quotes from named officials/councillors (Cllr Lacey's "When you saw the allocations, you could stand over that process").
- *Hazard:* Acknowledge DCC's 2017 PIA on genuine privacy concerns — the gap isn't malicious, it's a policy default.

**7. "The council scoreboard buried in a 240-page PDF"**
- *Data:* NOAC LA Performance Indicator Report H1–H7 (housing stock, vacancies, re-letting time, maintenance cost, PRS inspections, long-term homeless, retrofit). fitz can lift the per-LA tables cleanly.
- *Hook:* The accountability data already exists; nobody surfaces it.
- *Visuals:* Per-LA card with the seven indicators; year-on-year arrows; rank within national distribution.
- *Honest gap:* H6 (long-term homeless) only has 5 reporting LAs — Dublin City returns jointly for all four Dublin authorities.

**8. "TD voting record × constituency housing need"**
- *Data:* SSHA per-LA waiting list (you have it) + your existing TD voting data + LA→constituency crosswalk (~50–60 hand mappings).
- *Hook:* "Your TD voted X on this housing bill while [N] households in your constituency are on the list."
- *Visuals:* On the member page, a "constituency housing context" panel attached to housing-relevant votes.
- *Risk:* Needs honest "constituency context shown via LA aggregation" framing; LA ≠ constituency.

### Tier 3 — Structural stories (high data strength, careful framing required)

**9. "Ireland's 9× housing-cost gap by citizenship"**
- *Data:* Verified Eurostat `ilc_lvho25` 2025 — Irish nationals 2.5%, non-EU 22.6%, ratio 9.0× (vs EU27 avg 2.6×).
- *Hook:* Ireland's housing system produces world-leading citizenship-based inequality — but it's tenure-driven, not poverty-driven (severe deprivation gap is ~1.0×).
- *Visuals:* The four-row comparison table (overburden / overcrowding / income poverty / deprivation) is the cleanest single visual asset in the dataset.
- *Framing imperative:* Lead with the mechanism (80% Irish own, 66% non-EU rent, severe rent inflation), not the disparity number. Without the tenure mechanism it reads as ethnic-inequality framing, which it isn't — it's a tenure-exposure story.

**10. "The refugee inflow is showing up in the social-housing data"**
- *Data:* SSHA Table 2.9 — non-EEA share 9.6% (2023) → 13.2% (2025); within non-EEA, refugee status 1,291 → 3,193 households (+147%).
- *Hook:* The 2022 Ukrainian / Middle-Eastern inflow is now landing in social housing applications. Honest fact, easy to weaponise.
- *Framing imperative:* Lead with denominators (refugees are <0.5% of Ireland's total non-EU resident population per Eurostat), not raw counts. Pair with Eurostat's "non-Irish are 9× more cost-overburdened" — the framing becomes "system is squeezing recent arrivals into the only path available", not "newcomers are taking houses".
- *Hazard:* Could be the single most exploitable line in the whole dataset if presented without context. Strong editorial discipline required.

**11. "Ireland in EU comparative context"**
- *Data:* Eurostat (`ilc_lvho25`, `ilc_lvho15`, `ilc_lvps15`) — EU27 averages alongside Irish numbers.
- *Hook:* Ireland ranks worst in EU-15 for share at "reduced/free rent"; cost-overburden gap by citizenship 3.5× wider than EU average; non-EU overcrowding rose faster than peer countries.
- *Visuals:* Ireland-vs-EU-average bars for each indicator.
- *Caveat:* Acknowledge naturalisation effects (Irish category includes naturalised foreign-born) and the country-of-birth alternative dimension.

### Tier 4 — Meta-narrative stories

**12. "The data void is itself the story"**
- *Data:* Ombudsman 2025 finding (verbatim quote), PBO repetition, Focus Ireland 2018 memo, Norris 2024 paper, Dublin Inquirer / Cllr Lacey, DCC 2017 PIA tension.
- *Hook:* The most politically important question — "who got the house, why, and ahead of whom?" — isn't published anywhere by anyone in Ireland.
- *Honest version:* Walked-back framing — not a "deliberate void"; a combination of statutory absence, GDPR caution, political risk-aversion, and the legitimate-but-resolvable privacy tension DCC's PIA flagged.
- *Comparator:* UK CORE publishes exactly this for England. France INSEE publishes a country-of-origin version.
- *Civic angle:* "Here's what we have, here's what we don't, here's who's asked for it" — with the FOI template ready to go.

**13. "Tipperary's 60/40 rule and other things only one council publishes"**
- *Data:* Tipperary §1.4 verbatim (60% main list / 40% transfer + HAP / 20% length-of-time); DLR's 70/10/10/10/10; DCC's 10 area committees.
- *Hook:* The fragments add up to a near-complete model of transparency; nobody has assembled them.
- *Civic angle:* Could publish the comparative table as a standing reference for advocates / journalists / oireachtas committees.

### How to sequence these as features

If building incrementally:

1. **Start with Tier 1 stories on a "Housing & Social Housing" page** — pure
   SSHA + HSA07 + HAP01 data, no spatial join, no controversial framing.
   Ships in a week, gives the app a real housing dimension.
2. **Add Tier 2 next** — per-LA scoreboard (NOAC + SSHA per-LA), council
   transparency comparator. Takes ~2–3 weeks; differentiates the app from
   every other Irish civic-tech site.
3. **Tier 3 last and carefully** — citizenship/EU-comparison stories need
   strong editorial framing. Don't ship until the page has earned trust
   on the Tier 1/2 material first.
4. **Tier 4 runs in parallel as editorial copy** — the data-void narrative
   is the explanatory glue across the other tiers; it belongs in the
   methodology / about / glossary copy rather than its own page.

### The single most distinctive story

Of the thirteen, the one nobody else has surfaced and that the data
genuinely supports without political risk: **"The list isn't who you think"
× "The headline understates by half" × "What it actually costs to be a
tenant in Ireland"** — the structural story of how Ireland's housing system
has shifted from being a problem for the unemployed family to being a
problem for the working single adult who arrived too late to buy. That's a
story with a clear data spine (SSHA + PBO + Eurostat tenure), no exploitable
edge cases, and a genuine civic-information value: it would change how a
reader understands what the social-housing waiting list actually is.

2. **AHB Provision tables have huge empty-cell ratios from multi-column
   layouts.** The numeric values are there, but they're surrounded by blank
   spacer cells. Post-processing needs to compact rows before parsing.
3. **Most council allocation schemes contain almost no tables** — Fingal
   adopted 2011, Tipperary 2024 with 0 tables (the rule is in prose),
   Galway City has 0 tables. The "allocation rules" data isn't tabular and
   needs prose parsing or hand-transcription.
4. **Housing Commission "per-LA" is sparse** — only 2 actually useful per-LA
   tables in 256 pages (p38 + p210). The rest is policy/methodology prose.
5. **NOAC "per_LA_full = 52" includes some false positives** — fitz catches
   any table mentioning many LAs as "per-LA"; some are commentary tables
   that list LAs as text rather than data rows.

### Highest-value extractable datasets (recommended priority)

If building anything, these are the genuinely clean targets:

1. **SSHA 2025 appendix A1.1–A1.9** (29 per-LA tables, ~5% empty cells, all 31
   LAs × 9 dimensions × 2 years). Trivial to lift.
2. **NOAC H1, H2, H4, H5, H7 per-LA tables** (~30 tables, clean extraction,

6. **Spending Review 2021 SHCEP p13** (capital-spend time series by

## Data model + fidelity logic (2026-05-31)

What tables we'd actually build, and how we'd know the data is correct. The
fidelity layer is a **cascade of 5 checks** — each catches different errors,
and only data that survives all five should land in a user-facing card.

### Tables — the schema

Three categories: **dimensions** (the geographic + reference vocabulary),
**facts** (the data points themselves), **derived** (cross-source
computations). All facts carry source provenance.

#### Dimension tables (~8)

| Table | Rows | Source of truth | Notes |
|---|--:|---|---|
| `local_authority` | 31 | Department of Housing list | id, name, type (city/county/CC), region, NUTS3, lgma_code |
| `constituency` | 43 | Electoral Commission 2023 review | id, name, replaces_old (for pre-2023 series) |
| `la_constituency_xref` | ~55 | Hand-curated | LA × constituency M:N + share-of-area weight |
| `municipal_district` | ~95 | LGMA | sub-LA breakdowns (Tipperary 5 MDs, Galway Co, etc.) |
| `dcc_area_committee` | 10 | DCC report | Dublin City B/D/E/H/J/K/L/M/N/P |
| `electoral_division` | ~3,400 | Pobal CSV | ED id, name, county |
| `homelessness_region` | 8 | DHLGH | Dublin, Mid-East, Midlands, etc. |
| `year` | ~30 | — | 1994 → present |

|---|---|---|

#### Provenance — one row per fact, on every fact table

Every row also carries:

```
source_doc_id     -- FK to data_source table
source_page       -- page number where extracted
source_url        -- canonical URL of source doc
extracted_at      -- timestamp of extraction
extraction_method -- 'fitz' / 'pxstat_api' / 'eurostat_api' / 'manual_csv' / 'manual_pdf'
extraction_hash   -- sha256 of the source file at extraction time (detect upstream changes)
```

### Fidelity logic — the 5-check cascade

A datapoint surviving the cascade is **defensible**. Showing one that fails
any check without flagging it would be a feature bug. The cascade runs after
every refresh; only green rows feed the user-facing API.

#### Check 1 — Extraction fidelity (did fitz read it right?)

- **Round-trip fixture set** — for ≥1 row per source page, manually verify
  the extracted cells match the PDF visually. Maintain ~100 hand-verified
  cells across all PDFs as CI fixtures; build fails on drift.
- **Empty-cell ratio bound** — reject any extracted table where `empty_pct >
  50%` *unless* the source is whitelisted as multi-column sparse (AHB
  Provision, Ombudsman p81 — both legitimately have 60–70% blanks from
  spacer columns).
- **Numeric-vs-text consistency** — if a column is labelled `2024` and any
  cell isn't numeric, flag that row.
- **Row-count match** — for per-LA tables, expected `n_rows ∈ {31, 32, 33}`
  (LAs + 1–2 header rows). Anything else means a row was split or merged.
- **Required-cells present** — for known table shapes (SSHA A1.9, NOAC H1),
  hard-code the 31-LA list and reject extraction if any LA is missing.

#### Check 2 — Internal consistency (does the table sum correctly?)

- **Sum-equals-total**: for every per-LA table publishing a `Total` row,
  `sum(LA_values) == total_value`. I verified this on SSHA A1.9 (national
  total 61,719 reconciles).
- **Sum-equals-100%**: for proportional dimensions (employment status,
  basis of need, citizenship), each row's percentages should sum to 100% ±
  0.2 (rounding tolerance).
- **Year deltas match published change column**: where the source publishes
  a change column (SSHA Table 1.1 has "Change 2024/2025"), `value_2025 −
  value_2024 == published_change`. Catches both extraction errors and
  source errors.
- **Reasonableness bounds**: vacancy rates 0–20%, rent ceilings €100–€2000,
  household counts ≥ 0, percentages 0–100%, retrofit numbers ≤ stock.
  Values outside these are flagged (could be real, could be junk).

#### Check 3 — Cross-table consistency (do related tables agree?)

Across different fact tables on the same source:

- **SSHA per-LA totals = SSHA national** — sum of `ssha_waiting_list` per-LA
  rows must equal `ssha_national` for the same dimension. Catches
  misalignment between A-tables and main 2.x tables.
- **NOAC H1.E (end of year stock) = next year's H1.A (start)** — when both
  years are loaded, end of 2023 should equal start of 2024 per LA. Catches
  data-quality issues in NOAC itself.
- **HAP01 yearly LA totals = HAP Performance Indicators report HAP-start
  totals** — sum of HAP01 per-LA × family-type for a year should match the
  headline "new HAP households". Catches version drift between CSO and
  DHLGH publications.
- **Sum of `dcc_band_table` waiting list = DCC report top-line 14,967** —
  reconciles the sub-area extraction to the headline.

#### Check 4 — Cross-source consistency (do independent sources corroborate?)

The most powerful check — different publishers, different methodologies,
should be in the same ballpark.

- **HSA07 starts vs Construction Status Report scheme count** — same LA,
  same year, won't match exactly (different scope) but >2× divergence is a
  red flag.
- **HAP Funding XLSX per-LA vs HAP01 PxStat per-LA** — same scheme, two
  publication paths. Should agree to the household-unit.
- **SSHA homeless basis-of-need vs DHLGH monthly homeless, regional
  aggregates** — won't match (different definitions, methodology, dates),
  but should be same order of magnitude for the same region/year. The SSHA
  methodology itself documents how they differ.
- **PBO ongoing need = SSHA + HAP active** — methodology is reproducible.
  If our recomputed figure ≠ PBO's published figure, our extraction or
  join is wrong.
- **Census 2022 LA-rented dwellings (153,192) ≈ sum of LA stock per NOAC
  H1.A 2022** — different denominators, but within 5–10% expected.

#### Check 5 — Semantic / user-facing check (does the card make sense?)

Before any number lands on a user page:

- **Card has a date** — every figure shows when it's from.
- **Card has a source link** — provenance click-through to the original.
- **Card has a denominator where relevant** — "8,761 homeless" must be
  paired with the population total or the percent.
- **Card flags methodology gotchas** — SSHA homelessness ≠ DHLGH count
  needs an inline asterisk.
- **Card refuses to display** if any value comes from a row that failed
  checks 1–4. Better "data unavailable" than wrong numbers.

### The one-line answer to "how do I know the data has fidelity?"

**A datapoint has fidelity if it survives all 5 checks** — extraction,
internal sum, cross-table, cross-source, semantic / has-provenance. Build a
`fidelity` view in the warehouse that runs the cascade after every refresh
and emits a green/amber/red flag per fact-row; only green rows feed the
user-facing API. Amber rows get inline flags; red rows display
"data unavailable."

The first time you run this, **you'll find multiple genuine errors in
published government data** — SSHA Table 1.1 has had cited inconsistencies
with the appendices in prior years (footnote 13 of the 2024 report
acknowledges one); HAP01 occasionally diverges from HAP Performance
Indicators by a few %. That's not a bug — it's the cascade doing its job,
attaching the right caveats, and letting you publish the **honest** picture
rather than the apparent one.
