1 
  Lord Mayor's Business 
 
Birthday wishes 
The Lord Mayor extended birthday wishes to Ronnie Delany, Freeman of the City of 
Dublin who celebrates his 90th birthday on the 6th March. 
  
 

 
Lord Mayor’s Committee on Animal Welfare Oversight Committee 
The Lord Mayor informed members of her intention to re-establish the Animal Welfare 
Oversight Committee as a joint sub-committee of the Housing & Social Inclusion SPC 
and the Community, Gaeilge, Sport, Art and Culture SPC.  This committee will be 
open to all Councillors to join.   
  
Joint Policing Committee 
The Lord Mayor advised members that while we are currently transitioning from the 
JPC structure to the new Local Community Safety Partnership, that she wishes to re-
establish the interim JPC. She confirmed that management are in the process of 
setting up the area based Community Safety meetings that includes the appointment 
of new chairs, but this is taking time to put in place. 
  
Whilst we wait for the Community Safety Partnership meetings to be put in place, the 
Council has re-established the area JPC meetings to provide a forum for the Council, 
An Garda Síochána and key stakeholders to meet.  
  
We are now proposing to hold a City-wide meeting to allow Councillors and An Garda 
Siochána to meet. Invites will be sent out in due course. 
  
Following discussion on the topic, the Lord Mayor confirmed that she will be meeting 
with the new Justice Minister Jim O’Callaghan in the coming weeks and will convey 
concerns raised including extending an invite to him to attend the City Wide JPC 
meeting, as suggested by Councillor Hazel Chu and Councillor Declan Flanagan.  
  
Announcement of Special Council Meeting  
The Lord Mayor advised of her intention to convene, a Special Meeting of Dublin City 
Council in order to discuss the use of the Irish Language in the City of Dublin and 
within Dublin City Council. Training will be provided in advance, and Lorraine Brogan 
will be in contact regarding this. The meeting will be held on the 20th March at a later 
time so as to facilitate an ETB meeting that takes place until 6.30 p.m. of which 12 
Councillors will be attending.  
  
St. Patrick’s Day 
Councillors were reminded to respond to Natalie’s email as soon as possible advising 
if they wish to attend the Parade Grandstand on St. Patricks day as soon as possible. 
Demand for tickets this year is high.

COUNCILLOR CHRISTY BURKE 
To ask the Chief Executive if funding has been approved by DOE to acquire private 
houses in Dublin central in order to assist Dublin City Council housing department 
acquire 3 bed homes for the housing list. 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
In March 2024 the Department of Housing issued Dublin City Council with an allocation 
of 350 acquisitions, which allowed DCC to acquire properties to prevent homelessness 
and properties for specific categories of need, without having to seek Departmental 
approval in advance.     
As DCC has yet to receive guidance from the Department on what delegated sanction 
may apply for 2025 therefore we are not in a position to proceed with negotiations on 
any newly proposed acquisitions at this time. Acquisitions which were sale agreed and 
approved by December 2024 are being processed these purchases will complete over 
the coming weeks and months.

To ask the Chief Executive for a detailed report on the Active Land Management Unit: 
how many staff are dedicated to tackling vacancy and dereliction in the city currently

Register. Staffing levels are broadly comparable year to year as vacancies arise and 
are filled as panels are formed.

The Residential Zoned Land Tax (RZLT) introduced in the Finance Act 2021 is a new 
tax that has replaced the Vacant Site Levy as a land activation measure and is first 
due on 1st February 2025 and payable in May 2025 in respect of land which is included

Dublin City Council is a key member of the Dublin City Taskforce which published a 
report in October 2024 outlining 10 “Big Moves” to revitalise the City Centre. These 
include addressing vacancy rates through the regeneration of (1) the O’Connell Street 
area, (2) Social housing, and (3) converting derelict sites to residential use.  
Through the Dublin Economic Monitor the Council tracks hospitality and retail data and

2) Rates policy on vacant rateable properties 
3) Dublin’s Local Economic and Community Plan (LECP)  Aims to guide the

investors. There are several policies that seek to reduce vacancy and underuse of 
existing building stock and to support the refurbishment and retrofitting of existing

sites. 
(iii) To encourage and facilitate the rehabilitation and use of vacant and under-utilised 
buildings, including their upper floors. 
(iv)To promote and facilitate the use, including the temporary use, of vacant

COUNCILLOR CLODGH NÍ MHUIRÍ 
To ask the Chief Executive what Dublin City Council currently does, and what is within

CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
Childcare is under the Department of Children, Equality, Disability, Integration and 
Youth (DCEDIY). The Dublin City Childcare Committee (non-profit company limited by 
guarantee) is one of 30 County and City Childcare Committees operating nationally 
and funded by the DCEDIY to support the development of quality, affordable, 
accessible and inclusive early childhood care, education and out of school services for 
all children. https://childcareonline.ie/about-us/   
. At the February Planning & Urban Regeneration SPC meeting it was agreed to 
reissue the letter to the DCEDIY which the previous SPC had sent to the former 
Minister for Children, Equality, Disability, Integration & Youth and Minister for Housing, 
Local Government and Heritage (see below).  
“ 
The sub-group of the SPC is concerned that the current Childcare Facilities Guidelines 
for Planning Authorities date from 2001 and have not been reviewed or updated 
since.  It is requested that a comprehensive review be undertaken and that a new set 
of Guidelines is prepared for the sector that reflects the current policy and regulatory 
context and gives relevant direction and guidance to Local Authorities and landowners 
alike. 
  
In considering the content and issues to be addressed in a future updated version of 
the guidelines, the SPC wish to highlight a number of areas that should be incorporated 
in the review. 
  
1. New primary schools should be designed to incorporate pre-school facilities.  This 
shared campus approach would be more sustainable for parents in allowing combined 
trips for young children; reducing stress for parents and traffic congestion and would 
also provide the opportunity to increase crèche provision in rapidly growing areas.   
  

2. Clear guidance is needed with regard to the design of childcare facilities.  There is 
a need for minimum standards and space requirements.  Such guidance should 
incorporate TUSLA standards – giving architects and planners the information needed 
to ensure that the spaces designed in planning applications is fit for purpose.  The 
guidance should be informed by research into what is the minimum scale of building 
needed to ensure viability for future operators so that the likelihood of the space being 
used is maximised.  It is recommended that the Department consult with stakeholders 
in the sector on this.    
  
Design guidance for new buildings should also address other relevant planning 
concerns such as access, parking, open space, preference in location within larger 
schemes and best practice design where new facilities are in dense urban locations 
and within multi-use buildings. 
  
3. The review should examine some of the emerging challenges with childcare facility 
provision in residential infill schemes; particularly taking into account the NPF targets 
for brownfield regeneration.  Options to enable a combined approach between 
landowners, and the methodology required to facilitate this,  in providing one viable 
facility rather than two smaller unviable units should be explored including a range of 
possible solutions such as contributions, levies and legislative options; and the option 
to locate the facility on another parcel of land in the area. 
  
4. The review should consider the most appropriate methodology to estimate demand 
for childcare in a given area as there is a need for the Guidelines to set this out in order 
to inform the Development Management process.  Too often cases are made by 
landowners that there is no demand in an area where Councillors are informed of 
pressure by parents in the area. 
  
Side by side with the review of the childcare Guidelines the SPC also wish 
consideration to be given to a number of issues that require to be addressed in a future 
updated version of the Apartment Standard Guidelines. 
  
1. The review should examine the decision to exclude schemes with a high number of 
one-bed and studio units from the requirement to provide childcare as set out in the 
2018 Apartment Standard Guidelines (Section 4.7) and again in the 2020 
Guidelines.  For many young families with pre-school children, their first home is often 
a small apartment and it can take years for the family to have the resources to move 
to a larger unit; especially as the costs associated with rearing young children and 
paying for childcare are so high.  The presumption that all one-bed units are occupied 
only by single people/couples without children is inappropriate; particularly in a housing 
context where the price of both renting and buying locks young families into small scale 
accommodation.   
  
2. The review should explore making funding available as part of approved social 
housing developments so that the childcare element is funded fully for construction 
and fit out. 
  
3. Finally the review should consider options for funding childcare provision for 
example a childcare contribution fund which developers would pay into in 
circumstances where a childcare facility in a particular location is not viable or a facility 
where developers could combine their obligations to provide one larger facility between 
them in circumstances where smaller services would not be viable. 
“ 
Childcare will remain an agenda item of the Planning & Urban Regeneration SPC.  
 

In relation to childcare support while the DCEDIY has tasked DCCC with this role the 
city council does itself play a significant role through the implementation of policies and 
objectives of the Dublin City Development Plan 2022 – 2028 which recognises that the  
provision of good quality and fit-for-purpose neighbourhood-based and local childcare 
services are central to providing for sustainable communities.  Policy QHSN55 states; 
“To facilitate the provision of appropriately designed and sized fit-for-purpose 
affordable childcare facilities as an integral part of proposals for new residential and 
mixed-use developments, subject to an analysis of demographic and geographic need 
undertaken by the applicant in consultation with the Dublin City Council Childcare 
Committee, in order to ensure that their provision and location is in keeping with areas 
of population and employment growth.”  
  
In addition where property is in DCC ownership the council is supportive of childcare 
provision where appropriate and in this regard  
• 
All current licences due for renewal are reviewed to ascertain if a lease 
agreement is more appropriate, or the agreement remains as a licence. 
• 
When a lease or a licence comes up for renewal, Property Management as part 
of its’ process, consults with the Area Manager/Office about the group. 
• 
When the requirements for Capital Grant Funding become known lease/licence 
arrangements will be reviewed where required.

Q.123  COUNCILLOR NIAL RING  
To ask the Chief Executive for the reasoning and legal basis for DCC requiring that a 
house has to be rented out for at least 12 months before renting to a family member 
who is approved for a HAP payment, and what evidence does Dublin City Council 
accept as evidence of a prior landlord and tenant relationship between the landlord 
and the HAP tenant 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
In cases where the tenant is related to the landlord, the local authority must be satisfied 
that the tenancy is a bona fide tenancy or a “tenancy in good faith” 
This is referenced in the Housing (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2014, Section 39 (2) 
(e), in order for housing assistance to be provided under this Part to a qualified 
household in respect of a dwelling – the housing authority shall be satisfied that the 
tenancy concerned is or would be a tenancy in good faith. 
 
In addition, in accordance with the Housing (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2014 42 
(5) (b), a landlord of a dwelling in respect of which housing assistance is being paid or 
sought under this Part shall, on request by the housing authority concerned - provide 
such information as the authority may require from time to time for the purposes of 
determining whether housing assistance is payable, or continues to be payable, in the 
case. 
Dublin City Council (DCC) request the following documentation in such cases so as to 
satisfy the Council that the tenancy is a tenancy in good faith: 
 

• 
Proof of 12 months consistent rental payments paid from the tenant’s bank account 
into the landlord’s account  
• 
Tenancy Agreement  
• 
Proof of registration of current tenancy with RTB 
 
If a tenancy is a new tenancy, it will not be possible for the applicant to provide 12 
months rental payments.  However, the application can be considered if the property 
was previously rented and the landlord can provide the following: 
 
• 
Tenancy Agreement for previous tenant(s) 
• 
Proof of registration of previous tenancy with RTB  
 
Dublin City Council has operated this procedure since 2017.  This same criteria is 
applied consistently in all cases, to all applicants, where there is a familial relationship 
between the tenant and landlord.
