Meeting with Minister James Browne 
The Lord Mayor informed members that she met with Minister James Browne, Minister 
for Housing, Local Government and Heritage on 21st May 2025 and circulated a note 
from the meeting to all Councillors the following day.  A follow-on meeting is being 
arranged with Minister Browne. 
 
Dublin joining EU Mayors for Housing Alliance 
Following on from the Lord Mayors meeting with Mayor of Rome Roberto Gualtieri in 
March, she announced that Dublin has been brought forward to meet the 
requirements of becoming a fully incorporated member of the EU Mayors for Housing 
Alliance. 
 
The Alliance comprises 16 European cities united in their mutual demands for the EU 
to urgently mobilize additional resources—not just a reallocation of existing funds—
and to act swiftly in response to growing housing needs, ensuring investment in 
housing policies that work to meet our housing needs. 
 
Critically, the Mayors for Housing Alliance is advocating for the reuse of unspent 
Recovery and Resilience Facility resources and revising its architecture to include 
housing and establishing emergency mechanisms to directly fund cities delivering 
social and affordable housing and emergency accommodation services. 
 
Core to the plan is the evidenced need for major investment in the Alliance cities - €80 
billion for housing construction and renovation is required by 2030, and we advocate 
the need to mobilize €300 billion annually across the EU for housing.  
 
Dublin, as a leading international EU capital city, will bring its influence and voice to 
advocate for major changes in the EU to support and make real our European 
Affordable Housing Plan. 
 
The Lord Mayor called on all Councillors to work to strengthen the visibility and impact 
of this Mayor’s for Housing City Alliance on the key EU institutions and to support our 
work within our European Parliament, our national government, regional assemblies 
and for all local government here in Dublin.

The Lord Mayor advised members that the Dublin City “Made of Memories” exhibition 
in the basement of City Hall has been extended to 14th June.  It is open to all between 
10am and 5pm.  She wished to thank the Staff of City Hall and to Tony Smithers for 
facilitating this. 
 
Present on Taskforce on Integration 
The Taskforce has met 4 times during the current Lord Mayor’s tenure with next 
meeting coming up on 18 June. The Taskforce focuses primarily on 3 key elements: 
 
1) 
The developing work of the Dublin City LAIT (Local Authority Integration Team) 
onboarded in January 2025.  The LAIT facilitates ongoing integration supports 
to IP Applicants, Programme Refugees, BOTP and those with permission to 
remain status by signposting to services and supports that promote positive 
community integration whilst working closely with various stakeholders.  
 
2) 
Structures outside of the Taskforce to include the reconvening of the 
Community Integration Forum by Q4 2025. Terms of Reference are in 
development. 
 
3) 
A 3-month pilot project using an evidence-based approach to address barriers 
to integration implemented by Sport Against Racism Ireland (SARI) in the Dubin 
1 and Dublin 8 Areas consisting of: 
• 
Needs Assessment / Research 
• 
Leverage of existing resources 
• 
Delivery of football activities to centre and local residents including 
weekly sessions & tournament linked with Refugee Day 
• 
Employability and leadership development  
• 
Sustained engagement 
• 
Positive & meaningful relationships within the community  
• 
Evaluation Report & Toolkit scheduled to be completed by 
September 2025.  
 
The members of the Taskforce have a keen interest in the approach to Integration in 
Liverpool and Manchester.  Following recent meetings with the Mayor and Lord Mayor 
of Liverpool, our engagement with Liverpool will include a focus on diversity, inclusion 
and integration.  
 
Portrait of female Lord Mayors of Dublin   
The Lord Mayor informed members of her final Lord Mayors initiative where she has 
requested that a portrait be commissioned, representative of all 12 female Lord 
Mayors of Dublin City Council and that this portrait be placed in the Mansion House to 
ensure there is female representation where currently there is not, in an effort to 
address the gender imbalance.  
 
This work will be supported by the Arts Office, Ray Yeates, Arts Officer and Dr. 
Margarita Cappock, Assistant Arts Officer and driven by Dublin City Councils 
Women’s Committee. The first step is to set up an Advisory Group that will include 
myself, members of the Women’s Committee and Arts Office to establish and feed into 
a brief for to procure an artist. Following a call out to artists with this brief, an 
Assessment Panel will be formed to select the potential artists to be put forward for 
selection. It is hoped that the delivery of this Commission will take between 18 months 
to 2 years to be complete.

Emergency Motion No. 2 in the name Councillor Daithí Doolan  
Seconded by Cllr Hazel De Nortúin 
Dublin City Council expresses deep concern at the announcement by the Minister for 
Housing to delay the construction of social housing on three sites in Dublin, totalling 
264 homes. 
 
Dublin City Council calls on the Minister to immediately provide funding straight to 
Dublin City Council to deliver housing directly with developers removing any further 
delays. 
 
Emergency Motion No. 5 in the name Councillor Gayle Ralph and Danny Byrne  
Seconded by: Cllr Clodagh Ní Mhuirí & Fine Gael Group 
Emergency Motion – Suspension of PPP Housing Scheme by Minister for Housing 
 
That this Council calls on the Minister for Housing to immediately reverse the recent 
decision to suspend the Public Private Partnership (PPP) housing delivery 
programme, specifically PPP Bundle 3. 
  
This decision, communicated to Dublin City Council only days before taking effect is 
unacceptable and has immediate and damaging consequences for urgently needed 
social and affordable housing in the city. The complex and lengthy nature of housing 
development means any disruption of this scale significantly delays delivery timelines, 
putting hundreds of homes at risk, including those projects contained in PPP Bundles 
4 and 5. 
 
This action directly undermines local authorities’ ability to respond to the worsening 
housing crisis, at a time when: 
 
• Homelessness continues to rise to record levels 
• House prices and private rents are increasingly unaffordable 
• Council housing waiting lists are growing 
  
If the suspension of the PPP scheme is not reversed, this Council demands that the 
Minister urgently allocate direct capital funding to Dublin City Council to enable these 
housing developments to proceed without delay.  
 
We call on the Minister to engage in meaningful consultation with this Council before 
making any future decisions that significantly affect our capacity to deliver housing to 
those in need. 
 

 
 
The motions were put and carried.

Q.4

north side of Dublin to promote road safety and cycling education. (Details Supplied). 
 
 
 
 

CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
 
The Mobility School is a Dublin City Council initiative designed to raise awareness 
about road safety among cyclists and to promote the use of bicycles as a flexible, 
sustainable mode of transport. It provides a safe, educational space that replicates real 
road conditions, including signs, signals, and pedestrian crossings. This setup allows 
people to learn the rules of the road and safe road behaviour. 
 
Since its recent launch at Eamonn Ceannt Park, the Mobility School has received a 
positive response from the public. It has become a great example of a successful, 
community-focused project, delivered in a short timeframe. This success was made 
possible through the strong collaboration of multiple internal departments, including 
the Road Safety and Micromobility Section, Active Cities, Dublin City Sport & Wellbeing 
Partnership (DCSWP), Culture, Community, Leisure & Area Services (CCLAS), Parks, 
and the Crumlin Area Office. The project also benefitted greatly from the support of 
key external partners such as RSWTG, The Bike Hub, Sundrive Track Club, and 
Cycling Ireland. Following this positive outcome, we are continuing to work in 
collaboration with the Parks and Sports departments to explore the potential for 
bringing similar Mobility Schools to more communities. We remain committed to 
promoting safe cycling and road safety education and are actively looking at how we 
can build on this success in the future.

COUNCILLOR PADDY MONAHAN 
To ask the Chief Executive, on foot of DCC’s recently published “Review of Current 
Development - Clongriffin and Belmayne” (April, 2025), to comment on the observation 
that “the scale of change in the form of tenure raises a number of key challenges for 
the area and for Dublin City Council” (p. 14). Can the Chief Executive comment on the 
key challenges referred to? The report states that 22% of housing in the area will be 
owner occupied, 19% private rental and 55% social housing. Do these figures 
represent the optimum blend of tenure types and best practice? How do these figures 
compare with other areas of Dublin, what challenges have arisen in those areas and 
what supports have been put in place to address such challenges? 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
The April 2025 “Review of Current Development – Clongriffin and Belmayne” Report 
highlights challenges regarding the area’s tenure mix. A high concentration of social 
housing can increase demand for public services, and place pressure on estate 
management. It can contribute to negative perceptions of the area, potentially affecting 
investment and community morale.  
DCC does not prescribe a fixed “optimum” tenure mix in numerical terms, but the 
Housing Strategy — informed by the national policy (“National Planning Framework” 
and “Housing for All”) emphasizes the importance of creating balanced, mixed-tenure 
communities to support sustainable, inclusive urban development.  
Ballymun and Cherry Orchard have similar tenure mixes to Clongriffin / Belmayne. 
Both areas have experienced challenges related to service provision and community 
development. In response, DCC has implemented targeted supports, including 
community programmes, employment initiatives, and infrastructure investment. 
To address these issues in Clongriffin and Belmayne, DCC has committed €13 million 
in capital funding for social, cultural, and recreational infrastructure. Planned measures 
include community, amenity and recreational hubs, green spaces, and improved 
connectivity, all aimed at supporting a resilient and inclusive neighbourhood.

Q.30  COUNCILLOR GAYLE RALPH 
To ask the Chief Executive re Dublin City Development Plan 2022–2028 to regulate 
drone deliveries in residential areas such as Glasnevin and Drumcondra. 
Given the absence of any drone-related policies in the Plan, and growing concerns 
from vulnerable communities about safety, privacy, and amenity impacts, we propose 
adding policy objectives to: 
• 
Prohibit commercial drone deliveries in residential zones without local consultation and 
planning approval 
• 
Require oversight of launch sites, flight paths, and noise/privacy standards 
• 
Establish a public engagement framework for drone infrastructure and services 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
Dublin City Council recognises that the commercial use of drones is a complex and 
evolving situation as the technology itself is moving so fast.  There is no published 
policy, legislation or guidance at a national level for drones so this creates significant 
challenges. The Department of Transport are currently finalising a policy framework to 
guide high-level strategic planning and development of the drone sector in Ireland. A 
joined-up approach will be required across government departments to support this 
policy framework. 
Drone flying restrictions are regulated by the Irish Aviation Authority (IAA), which 
publishes maps showing restricted zones. These geographical zones restrictions are 
established by the IAA under the IAA Act. A number of these already exist for safety 
and security reasons, for example around airports and prisons. Access to a restricted 
UAS (drones) Geographical Zones can be requested through the published zone 
authority. DCC is not a zone authority in this respect. It is important to note that 
Restricted Zones are not absolute no-fly areas. Instead, they require operators to seek 
permission from the relevant Zone Authority, which will need to allocate resources and 
staff to assess and manage these requests on a case-by-case basis. 
 
For any concerns about drone safety or unusual drone activity, you can contact the 
Irish Aviation Authority (IAA), which oversees drone regulation in Ireland. The IAA’s 
public reporting portal at iaa.ie/dronealert allows you to report drones that appear to 
be flying unsafely or outside expected norms. Many drones now use Remote 
Identification (Remote ID), which acts like a digital license plate by broadcasting real-
time information such as location, speed, and registration. This improves transparency 
and helps ensure drones are operated safely and responsibly. The IAA website also 
offers guidance on drone insurance, pilot requirements, and safe flying practices. 
 

At European level, the European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) is the 
regulatory authority responsible for ensuring the safe integration of drones, also 
referred as Unmanned Aircraft Systems (UAS). EASA sets the common safety rules 
for drone operations, including developing regulations, operational categories, risk-
based frameworks, guidance on technical requirements, pilot competency and 
harmonising standards. 
  
Airspace is managed by AirNav Ireland. Anyone wishing to fly a drone in restricted 
airspace must submit a UF101 request to AirNav for review. The zone authority, for 
example Dublin Airport Authority or the Irish Prison Service must be consulted before 
permission to fly is granted. Temporary restrictions can also be requested by other 
organisations for events like the St. Patrick’s Festival or major concerts at Croke Park 
or Aviva Stadium. 
  
DCC has established an internal Drones Governance Group dedicated to discussing 
the potential role of the city in any future legislation or regulations regarding drones in 
the urban space. In addition the Governance Group aims to engage with DCC 
departments to proactively explore where additional drone flying restrictions might be 
appropriate as part of a wider research and innovation partnership.  
 
Quiet Areas have been highlighted as potential zones for further consideration under 
the Environmental Noise Directive. Initial discussions surrounding DCC Quite Areas 
are ongoing reviewing relevant regulations to see how a potential proposal for 
permanent restriction zones can be made to the IAA. Note that current research on 
drone noise impacts is limited. To progress with such proposals, additional testing and 
trials will be required to assess whether drone operations over Quiet Areas would 
exceed noise thresholds — for example, whether altitude limits or specific buffer zones 
are needed to mitigate noise levels and remain compliant with Quiet Areas standards. 
 
Dublin City Council has also been collaborating and exchanging knowledge with other 
European Cities, by being a member of the UIC2 (Urban Innovation Community) and 
the Polis Network, which are key European platforms supporting cities in the safe and 
sustainable integration of drones and urban air mobility. UIC2, under the EU Smart 
Cities Marketplace, helps local authorities align drone initiatives with public needs and 
EU policies, while Polis promotes knowledge exchange and policy development to 
ensure drone use supports wider urban mobility and sustainability goals. 
 
Dublin City Council through it’s Smart City and Survey and Mapping Units established 
a Drone Innovation Partnership with Maynooth University and the Irish Aviation 
Authority in October 2024 which is co-funded through Research Ireland. Through this 
Partnership, DCC is working collaboratively to explore the opportunities and concerns 
around the use of drones.  
As with many new and emerging technologies, Dublin City Council will continue to 
explore and examine how these can enhance service delivery and benefits to citizens 
in accordance with the Corporate Plan and the Dublin City Development Plan. 
Importantly, DCC values the voices of our citizens to ensure transparency, 
accountability, and the continued trust of the community. 
  
We are in the process of arranging further engagement and consultation around 
drones in the city and will be hosting a series of community engagements to discuss 
issues outlined. Once a date has been agreed for these engagements, DCC will 
contact you and all residents interested in attending will be welcome.

CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
Dublin City Council have in place Skip Bye-laws for the control and management of 
skips called Dublin City Council Control on Skip Bye-Laws 2025 (as amended from 
Dublin City Council Control on Skip Bye-Laws 1999).  The skip bye-laws set out the 
conditions in which a skip may be sited within Dublin City Council’s administrative area. 
 
A permit is required from the council when a hirer of a skip wished to place the skip 
between the operational hours of a restricted area such as Pay and Display Parking 
Bays, Residential Parking Scheme or other parking prohibitions, restrictions and 
conditions. The permit includes information such as the skip hirer details, times, dates 
and exact location for where is skip is to be placed and the name of the skip company. 
The skip company must ensure that the hirer of the skip has secured a permit from the 
council before delivering and siting the skip and that the skip company remove the skip 
before or on the last day stated on the permit. There is a daily charge for the permit 
that is calculated based on the tariff zone in which the skip is being sited.      
 
The council restricts the placement of a skip, at any location, up to 7 days at any given 
time.  However, on some occasions a skip may be required for a longer period of time 
depending on the project such as house renovation, etc. An application for an 
additional permit/s maybe considered base on a number of factor such as complaints, 
availability of parking space, litter management, etc, and complying with any 
instructions or conditions set out by the council. The council can impose conditions 
regarding the placement of the skip and that the a skip company must remove a skip 
not more than one hour after a request has been submitted by the council to do so.

is a noticeable increase in insects and birdlife as a result of this change in management 
practice.  If they so wish, families of the interred can maintain their plots in a different 
way.

Q.98  COUNCILLOR JOHN STEPHENS 
To ask the Chief Executive could a Pay and Display System be introduced in (Details 
Supplied) to elevate non-residents parking their cars there all day? 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
The purpose of a Pay and Display and Permit Parking Scheme is to eliminate long 
term commuter/business parking in residential areas.  
Dublin City Council will proceed with the preparation of a Pay and Display and Permit 
Parking Scheme and subsequent ballot of residents where it is first established that 
there is a demonstrable and clear desire in favour of a Scheme and in accordance with 
the criteria below. This may take the form of a number of written requests or a signed 
petition from residents of the road. 
Please note, therefore, that on receipt of written supporting evidence from other 
residents of the road, the request for a Parking Scheme will be referred to the 
Transport Advisory Group for examination and report. 
On referral to the Transport Advisory Group, the request will be examined in 
accordance with the following guidelines: 
• 
Suitability of the road for a Pay and Display and Permit Parking Scheme. 
• 
That the road is mainly residential where in excess of 80% of available on-street 
parking is normally occupied on inspection during business hours. 
• 
A proposed Parking Scheme will be subject to a plebiscite of the residents concerned 
listed on the Register of Electors. 
• 
The road must have a minimum width of 6.5 metres for two-sided parking and a 
minimum width of 4.6 metres for one-sided parking to allow access for emergency 
services and refuse collection.  Please note that these are minimum dimensions which 
only provide for one lane of traffic and are only suitable for roads with low traffic 
volumes. 
 
It is important to note that a Parking Scheme cannot be recommended on a road where 
the minimum width is less than 4.6 metres. Where a road is wide enough for a Scheme 
with one-sided parking only, double yellow lines will be required on the opposite side of 
the road.  As a result, there may be a reduction in the number of parking spaces currently 
available to residents.  
Please provide this information on the initial petition so as residents can make an 
informed decision in relation to the potential loss of parking on one side of the road. 
If a road is less than 6.5 metres and more than 4.6 metres, then parking will only be 
available on one side of the road. 
In summary, in order to proceed with the request to introduce Pay and Display and Permit 
Parking, it will be necessary for the City Council to receive, in the first instance, written 
supporting evidence for the introduction of a Parking Scheme, such as a number of written 
requests or a signed petition from residents of each road within the estate, giving names 
and addresses, indicating their clear support for such a Scheme.  Ideally, this should be 
35% of the households concerned.

Matters relating to bus schedules and routes are primarily a matter for the NTA.

Response from the NTA: 
 
I refer to the matter raised in Council Question, Q106, from Cllr Daniel Ennis. 
 
A fundamental objective of the Clontarf to City Centre Scheme, developed by Dublin 
City Council, is to provide safe, fully segregated cycling facilities.  However, fitting in 
bus stops along North Strand Road whilst also maintaining segregated cycling 
arrangements, is very challenging in terms of space requirements.  This is made 
even more difficult by the level of planned bus services under BusConnects, requiring 
implementation of double length bus stops. 
 
When the issue of the bus stop spacing was raised with the NTA in late 2023, the 
NTA undertook its own analysis and developed a solution that enabled the 
installation of a shorter bus stop arrangement at the Strand Ville Avenue bus stop 
rather than the full double bus length stops provided at other locations along this 
scheme.   As Strand Ville Avenue bus stop only provides a shorter length of bus stop, 
it is not capable of safely facilitating all of the bus services using the road and a more 
limited bus stopping pattern is required.  This lower level of stopping buses was the 
central rationale enabling the provision of these additional stops. 
 
Accordingly, and because of the reduced layout, these two additional bus stops are 
served by Route 130 only, which operates approximately every 10 to 12 minutes 
during weekday daytime hours.  Other bus routes can continue to be accessed from 
the adjacent bus stops.

Q.121  COUNCILLOR SUPRIYA SINGH 
To ask the Chief Executive: 
In light of two serious traffic incidents that occurred within a 15-minute window 
outside Nos. 156–162 Newbrook Avenue—one involving parents during school drop-
off and the other an ambulance responding to an emergency call, which was forced 
to collide with a parked vehicle due to illegal and obstructive parking—can the Chief 
Executive advise what urgent and long-term measures will be taken to address the 
critical health and safety risks on Newbrook Avenue? 
Specifically, can the Council consider: 
• 
Increased enforcement and the presence of traffic wardens during school drop-off 
and pick-up times, in order to prevent traffic congestion that regularly blocks 
residents from exiting their homes—particularly in the event of an emergency. 
• 
Deployment of school traffic wardens or temporary stewards at peak times, similar to 
the successful pilot previously conducted on Newbrook Road? 
• 
Temporary or permanent closure of the school’s back gate onto Newbrook Avenue, 
given that it is not the main entrance? 
• 
Regular enforcement patrols during peak hours to deter illegal parking on double 
yellow lines and footpaths? 
• 
Direct engagement with the school to develop a comprehensive traffic management 
plan that ensures the safety of both residents and students and guarantees access 
for emergency services? 
Residents are deeply concerned, as the current situation poses a significant risk to 
public safety. There is a strong sense that immediate and coordinated action is 
needed. Furthermore, residents have expressed dissatisfaction with the suggestion 
from the Sustainable Mobility Department to simply place a bollard outside the 
school, as they do not believe this measure will meaningfully address the problem. 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
Our team has been working with the school communities and local residents for over 
a year to find solutions to the traffic challenges and related safety concerns in the 
area as part of our Safe Routes to School programme. It was decided to trial a 
School Street on Newbrook Road as a way to reduce the cars entering this road 
where four schools are located. Following the success of the trial the School Street 
was permanently installed at the end of March. The feedback from the school 
communities, parents and local residents after the trial all reported an improvement in 
safety for children on their journeys to school. A slight decrease in the number of 
families driving their children to school was also recorded during the trial. 
  
Following the concerns raised by the residents on Newbrook Avenue, our team 
looked at various solutions to try and improve the situation for residents on this road. 
We shared a potential solution with residents but as there was not agreement on this, 

we didn’t move forward with it. If the residents would like us to move ahead and 
implement the bollards as previously suggested, we can implement that suggestion. 
Similarly, if the residents have other options they’d like to suggest, we can explore 
their feasibility.  
  
The team has contacted the four schools in the locality to ask that they send a 
notification to parents and guardians in their school communities to flag the traffic 
challenges being experienced and request that all members of the school community 
park with consideration for the local residents, as well as suggesting that the carpark 
at Donaghmede shopping centre is used instead of local residential streets. The 
schools have confirmed that they remind parents on a regular basis to park with due 
care as well as encourage parents to consider walking/cycling or scooting to 
school. Every Wednesday they hold a cycle/scoot/walk to school event to encourage 
more families will choose alternative travel methods. 
 
 
We’re not in a position to deploy a school warden to work on Newbrook Avenue. 
  
The schools would need to make the decision on whether or not to close the gate. 
However, we would suggest that having more entrances to the school is beneficial for 
the area as a whole. If this entrance was to close it would mean more people using 
the other entrances each day which would exacerbate the traffic challenges at those 
locations. 
  
In terms of enforcement, we encourage residents to report issues or illegal parking to 
An Garda Síochána as they have the power of enforcement.  
 
 
 
 
Q.122 COUNCILLOR SUPRIYA SINGH 
Can the Chief Executive provide an update on the expected timeline for the opening 
of the bus lane on Belmayne Main Street? Are there any planned public transport 
routes that are expected to utilise this bus lane? 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
 
We have been advised that the bus lanes on Belmayne Main Street should be in use 
by the end of this year 2025. 
Phase 8 of the BusConnects Network Redesign is due to be implemented in Q1 
2026. This phase of the project includes the D-Spine services and various local and 
radial routes. New routes D1 and L80 will launch in Phase 8 and travel via Belmayne 
Main Street using the bus gate. The bus lanes and bus gate will become operational 
for the start of these routes. 
Phase 9 of the project is currently scheduled for Q3 2026 and as part of this phase, a 
new orbital route, route N8, will begin operation between Blanchardstown SC and 
Clongriffin via Dublin Airport. Route N8 is planned to also operate via Belmayne Main 
Street, using the bus gate at the Malahide Road end.
