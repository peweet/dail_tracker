What is The Housing
Agency?

The Housing Agency is a Government body working with the Department
of Housing, Local Government and Heritage, local authorities and Approved
Housing Bodies in the delivery of housing and housing services. The
Agency's purpose is to accelerate housing supply, in partnership with our key
stakeholders, by providing our expertise, support and resources to deliver
high-quality homes in vibrant communities. The Agency has three strategic
themes - being a centre of knowledge for housing insights, addressing
housing supply and affordability and supporting the creation of sustainable
and inclusive communities.

Executive summary

The Summary of Social Housing Assessments 2025 brings together information provided
by local authorities on households in their functional area that are qualified for social
housing support but whose social housing need is not currently being met. It is a point-in-
time assessment of the identified need for social housing support across the country.
Purpose of the Assessment: This is a statutory assessment to count the total number of
households qualified for social housing support across the country on 3 November 2025, in
order to better inform policy and plan for the delivery of the right types of housing support.
`Net need' or the total number of households qualified: This is the total number of
households qualifying for social housing support whose social housing need is not being
met. All the figures in this report are based on net need. The methodology used to produce
these figures is outlined on pages 14-17.
Criteria: Households must be qualified for social housing support. Households currently
living in local authority rented accommodation, approved housing body accommodation,
accommodation provided under the Housing Assistance Payment (HAP) scheme,
accommodation provided under the Rental Accommodation Scheme (RAS), accommodation
provided under the Social Housing Capital Expenditure Programme (SHCEP) schemes or any
household on a transfer list are not included in the total number.
The baseline income thresholds increased by 5,000 for all local authorities with effect
from 1 January 2023. The thresholds increased to 40,000, 35,000 and 30,000 for Bands
1, 2 and 3 respectively. These thresholds are net income thresholds, i.e. gross household
income less income tax, PRSI, Universal Social Charge and Additional Superannuation
Contribution. The income bands are expressed in terms of a maximum net income threshold
for a single-person household, with an allowance of 5% for each additional adult household
member, subject to a maximum allowance under this category of 10% and separately a 2.5%
allowance for each child.1

household composition, band and local authority, see here.

Key findings 2025
                                                                    Summary of Social Housing Assessments 2025 10

In total, 61,719 households were assessed as qualified for social housing support as of
3 November 2025.

The number of households qualified for social housing support increased by
1,778 households (3.0%) since the previous assessment on 4 November 2024 and
decreased by 29,881 households (32.6%) compared to the first annual assessment
conducted in September 2016. A total of 11 of the 31 local authorities recorded a
decrease in numbers in 2025.

The four Dublin Authorities (Dublin City, Dún Laoghaire-Rathdown, Fingal and South
Dublin) account for 41.6% of the national total social housing need in 2025.

One-adult households remain the majority household grouping on the waiting list and
increased slightly from 60.8% of the total in 2024 to 60.9% in 2025.

One-parent family households continue to be the second highest household grouping,
increasing from a proportion of 21.2% to 22.2% between 2024 and 2025.

The number and proportion of households whose basis of need for social housing is
homelessness increased from 14.7% in 2024 to 16.1% in 2025.

The proportion of households living with parents, relatives and friends combined is the
most common tenure type for households, increasing from 36.6% in 2024 to 37.6%
in 2025. Consequentially, the Private Rented Sector (with and without RS) no longer
remains the largest tenure type for households on the waiting list and its proportional
representation has more than halved since 2016 (from 67.4% to 31.0%).

In 2022, the SSHA began reporting the number of households living in Direct
Provision. In 2025, there were 2,080 households recorded in this current
accommodation type.

A Traveller Identifier was introduced in 2022. In 2025 1,844 households identified as
Irish Travellers.

There were less households on the waiting list for a period of more than 7 years, with
this proportion declining from 19.2% in 2024 to 18.9% in 2025. There were also fewer
households on the list for less than 2 years with these proportions decreasing from
42.9% in 2024 to 41.9% in 2025.

The proportion of households with a main applicant over 60 years of age has been
growing year on year and now makes up 12.1% of total households.

The employment status of most qualified applicants remains unemployed, however this
continues to fall (from 47.6% in 2024 to 46.9% in 2025) and the largest proportion of
households continue to have an income source from social welfare only, although this
is also falling (from 52.3% in 2024 to 51.6% in 2025).

Introduction

What is the SSHA?

The purpose of the Summary of Social Housing Assessments (SSHA) is to capture the total
number of households qualified for social housing support across the country whose social
housing need is not being met, in order to better understand the level of need for such
support. It is a snapshot at a point in time of the housing lists of 31 local authorities. This
year's count was carried out on 3 November 2025.

The format of this report provides a breakdown of the national social housing need by
various categories, such as:
· Age profile and employment status of the primary applicant
· Source of household income
· Basis of need for social housing support
· Specific accommodation requirements

A breakdown of all the main tables by local authority area is available in the Appendix. All
data from the Local and National Figures, and Household Profile tables published here,
along with those of previous assessments are available for download from the Data Hub
on the Housing Agency's website2. Previously published full reports are available from the
Publications Section of this website3.

Need for social housing support

Sections of this report break down the identified net need for social housing support
with reference to each qualified household's main need for support (often referred to as
the `basis of need'). When determining the main need for support, local authorities must
consider the household's circumstances at the time of applying.

Regulation 23 of the Social Housing Assessment Regulations 2011 (and subsequent
amendments) details the types of needs that must be considered when making a
determination on a household's main requirement for support. For example, under
Regulation 23, local authorities must determine if the household applying for support is
currently residing in accommodation and requires a rent supplement payment or is living in
overcrowded or unfit accommodation.

In addition, the regulations require local authorities to determine if a member of the
household is homeless, living in an institution, emergency accommodation or hostel. This
category of need, and the method used to collect the data, differs from other counts of
homelessness. The SSHA is administrative data that gives a point-in-time snapshot of those
households recorded on local authority housing lists that are qualified for social housing
support. Within this dataset, the number of households recorded as homeless will differ
from homelessness figures published elsewhere 4.

Who is reported on?

The reported data refers only to households who have been deemed qualified for social
housing support. This means that households who are already living in local authority or
approved housing body accommodation, or accommodation provided via the Housing
Assistance Payment (HAP), the Rental Accommodation Scheme (RAS) or accommodation
provided under the Social Housing Capital Expenditure Programme (SHCEP), are excluded
from this report. Households in these categories of social housing support are deemed to
have had their housing need met.

Why produce the report?

The SSHA provides an up-to-date picture of the level of need for social housing support
and the types of supports that are required. The 2025 Summary is the continuation of a
process of annualised assessments of identified national housing need that commenced
with the 2016 Summary. The results of the SSHA provide planners and policymakers with
important information to allow for the delivery of the right types of housing supports in the
right locations to the people who need it most. The data contained in the SSHA is also an
important reference point for assessing progress towards the realisation of objectives set
out in `Delivering Homes, Building Communities', the Government's housing plan to 2030.

Background and methodology

The SSHA is carried out annually by local authorities under Section 21 of the Housing
(Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2009 and the Social Housing Assessment (Summary)
Regulations 20165. To qualify for social housing support, households must be both eligible
for and in need of social housing support.

The key figure reported in the SSHA is referred to as `net need'. Net need is the total
number of households qualified for social housing support whose need for support is not
being met. This total excludes the following households:

I. Duplicate applications: A standardised procedure for social housing assessments was
       introduced in 2011, which restricted households from applying to more than one
       local authority. Prior to this, households could apply for social housing support to
       multiple authorities. Where a household has applied to more than one authority for
       social housing support, only their first application was included in the count. In this
       Assessment, 421 duplicate applications were identified, representing a decrease of 15
       duplicate applications compared to 436 identified in the 2024 Assessment.

II. Those in receipt of social housing support: For example, households currently living
       in local authority rented accommodation, approved housing body accommodation or
       accommodation provided under the HAP, RAS or SHCEP schemes.

III. Households on a transfer list: Any household that has applied for a transfer from an
       existing form of social housing support including HAP.

Information on the profile of households qualified for social housing support is also
collected as part of this process. This information informs social housing policy and
enhances the capacity of local authorities to plan to provide the right types of housing
support. The Government's `Delivering Homes, Building Communities, 2025-2030 Action
Plan'6 commits to delivering 72,000 social homes over the lifetime of the plan, including an
average annual new-build component of 12,000 social housing homes per year.
The SSHA helps to ensure the social housing supports delivered match more closely to the
profile of those in need.

The process used to carry out the SSHA is outlined below. The purpose of this process is
to ensure that the net need for social housing support on the count date is recorded as
accurately as possible.

Local authorities were given detailed guidance in relation to the process to be carried out.
Households entered on the waiting list or reviewed after 27 June 20247 could be included
in the return without a need to update the existing record, unless the local authority was
aware of a particular reason to update the file. All other qualified households (all households
other than those entered on the list or assessed since 27 June 2024) were written to and
requested to update their details, including the following information:

I. Confirmation that they were still seeking social housing support.
II. Updated information (including verification documentation, as required) to ensure that

       they qualify or continue to qualify in accordance with the Social Housing Assessment
       Regulations.
III. Any new household members.
IV. Any changes in circumstances of the household ­ a recent change in marital status, for
       instance.
V. Any change to employment status.
VI. Income verification for all households.
VII. Details of rent or mortgage supplement, if in receipt of this form of State support.

In addition, any household on the system not yet fully assessed was required to have their
details updated to finalise a decision on their application.

Typically, households received a letter that outlined the need to engage with the local
authority regarding their application for housing support and a form asking them for
relevant information. Follow-up letters were issued to non-respondents. In some areas,
authorities also texted, emailed or telephoned applicants, and raised general awareness of
the process by placing advertisements in local newspapers and in other public offices.

The guidelines underpinning the SSHA allow an application to be closed if several attempts
to contact a household are made and there is no response. However, authorities were given
discretion to reactivate an application where, within a reasonable time frame, a household
that was removed from the list due to a failure to engage with the process, subsequently
provided information that demonstrated it remained qualified for social housing support.

The data returned from applicants was verified and the waiting list updated based on
the replies. Those assessed as not meeting the eligibility criteria for social housing were
removed from the waiting list.

The data was returned to the Local Government Management Agency (LGMA), where
further data checks were carried out and duplicate applications removed (see below for
details). The data was then passed to the Housing Agency for analysis and reporting.

National and local figures

The SSHA 2025 is the 10th annual assessment, following on from the assessments of
need conducted in September 2016, June 2017/2018/2019, and each November from
2020 to 2025.

Timely data on the requirement for social housing that is grounded in the application of
clear guidelines provide a more precise snapshot of the scale of need and the type of
supports required.

The results of this year's assessment are comparable to data derived from assessments
conducted in 2013, in addition to each year from 2016 to 2024, as these exercises were
guided by a common legislative framework.

In total, 61,719 households were assessed as qualified for housing support as of 3
November 2025. The number of households on the waiting list increased by 1,778
households (3.0%) compared to the assessment conducted in November 2024. Table 1.1
presents the number of households deemed qualified for social housing support by local
authority area.

Of the 31 local authorities, Dublin City has by far the greatest identified need for social
housing support, with 13,002 households on the waiting list, representing just over a fifth
(21.1%) of all households qualified for housing support. The four Dublin local authorities
(Dublin City, Dún Laoghaire-Rathdown, Fingal, and South Dublin) have 25,684 households
on their waiting lists, an increase of 1,086 households (4.4%) compared to November 2024.
Together, the four Dublin authorities account for 41.6% of the national total.

The Mid-East (Louth, Kildare, Meath and Wicklow) had 8,865 households, or 14.3% of the
national figure, identified as being qualified for social housing support. This figure was up by
2.3% (200 households) on the 2024 assessment.

Across the two Cork local authorities (Cork City and Cork County) there were a total of

Across the two Galway authorities (Galway City and Galway County) there were a total of
3,463 households on the waiting list, an increase of 289 households (9.1%) since November

Limerick City and County Council reported 2,419 households deemed qualified for social
housing support, which represents 3.9% of the national total and a 0.2% relative decrease

Waterford City and County Council saw 1,458 households qualified for social housing
support, or 2.4% of the national total. This was a decrease of 39 households, or 2.7%,
from 2024.

The largest decreases compared to results reported in 2024 were recorded in Cork City,
Louth, and Laois, with drops of 10.4%, 9.2%, and 7.0% respectively in the number of
households on the social housing waiting list.

Conversely, increases of 15.5%, 16.9%, and 39.7% in the number of households qualified
for social housing support were recorded in Monaghan, Leitrim, and Longford County
Councils respectively (see Table 1.2).

As of the November 2025 count date, over two-fifths, or 11 local authorities reported
a decrease in the number of households on their respective waiting lists compared to
the results of the previous assessment. Table 1.2 shows the ranking of local authorities

Household profile

Data collected in the Summary of Social Housing Assessments allow households on the
list to be profiled by characteristics such as age, employment status and nationality of the
main applicant, and income source, current tenure and composition of the household. This
section presents a summary of these household profiles.

The tables that follow in this section present a summary of the number and proportion
of households in each profile category. The percentage change column shows the rate of
change relative to the previous year, however, the amount of change between 2024 and
2025 should be measured by percent difference which allows for any change in the total
number of households between years. For example, there is an overall increase in total
households since the previous assessment. For this reason, the text in the Household Profile
section refers to percentage point difference when interpreting change between years.

Main applicant age

The largest proportion of households in need of social housing support in 2025 were aged
between 30 and 39 years (27.2%), as shown in Table 2.1. This was followed by those aged
between 40 and 49 years old, with 22.8% of households with main applicants in this age
group. There was an increase in households aged under 30 compared to 2024. This was led
mainly by a relative increase of 3.8% (224 households) in main applicants aged under 25 and
a 4.8% increase (398 households) with applicants aged 25 to 29.

The count of households with a main applicant aged 70 years or more increased since the
last assessment from 2,208 households in the 2024 count to 2,316 households in 2025 (an
increase of 4.9%). The proportion of those in the over 60s age group has been growing year
on year and makes up 12.1% of total households. This is a 4.9 percentage point increase on
the proportion in this age range in 2016 (7.2%).

Employment status and household income source

The majority of main applicants continue to have an employment status of `unemployed
and in receipt of social welfare'. In 2025, 28,949 households (46.9%) fell into this category,
a relative decrease of 0.7 percentage points compared to 2024 (47.6%), as shown in Table
2.2. Additionally, this figure has almost halved since 20169.

Basis of need and accommodation requirements

The Basis of Need category `Dependent on Rent Supplement' was replaced with `Requires
Rent Supplement to provide for accommodation needs' from 2022 onwards. This more
clearly reflects the two types of households contained here ­ households that are currently
private renting and in receipt of Rent Supplement and households that may require financial
assistance such as HAP to provide for their housing need.

Further clarification was also issued in relation to the use of `Unsuitable accommodation ­
particular household circumstances' and `Unfit accommodation', which, combined with the
above, may impact how data are disaggregated by local authorities in the appendices
(Table A1.5).

`Unsuitable accommodation ­ particular household circumstances' remained the most
common basis of need for households in 2025, with 16,221 households (26.3%) with this
basis of need. This was a relative increase of 1.5% (241 households) on the 2024 count, but
an absolute decrease of 0.4 percentage points on last year's proportion (26.7%)
(see Table 2.5).

The number of households categorised as `Requires Rent Supplement to provide for
accommodation' decreased by 855 households to 11,937 (or a relative decrease of 6.7% on
the 2024 count).

The number of households with a requirement for separate accommodation as their
basis of need increased between 2024 and 2025 from 9,994 to 10,783 (or a relative
increase of 7.9%). As a proportion of all households, those with a requirement for separate
accommodation as their basis of need increased from 16.7% to 17.5%.

The fourth largest category of need for social housing support is `Homeless, institution,
emergency accommodation or hostel' which increased by 1,146 to 9,933 households
between the 2024 and 2025 assessments and now represents 16.1% of all households
on the list.

The number of households whose need for social housing is due to being `Overcrowded'
increased by 450, or 12.5%, on the 2024 count. This cohort now accounts for 6.5% of all
households on the housing list.

As in other years, the majority of households on the waiting list do not have any specific

requirement continues to decrease from 74.9% in 2024 to 74.1% in 2025 (see Table 2.6).

by 685 to a total of 8,761 households, a relative increase of 8.5%. The proportion of
households with this requirement has increased from 13.5% in 2024 to 14.2% in 2024.

Traveller households

Sections 6 and 7 of the Housing (Traveller Accommodation) Act 1998 require housing
authorities to assess the accommodation needs of Travellers normally resident in their
administrative areas. Without a formal Traveller identifier in past assessments, the number
of Traveller households in each local authority area was drawn by proxy from the Specific
Accommodation Requirements table using the figure for `Household member is a Traveller'.
However, some households were recorded as having a Traveller specific accommodation
requirement in cases where they had not requested Traveller specific housing (Traveller
Group Housing, Halting Site or Bay in a Caravan Park), leading to inaccurate estimates
of the number of Traveller specific housing units required. In 2025, 954 households
were recorded as requiring specific accommodation because a `household member(s) is a
Traveller' (a decrease of 37 households from 2024). However, this was likely to be a proxy
count of the overall number of Traveller households known to local authorities rather than
the number who require Traveller specific accommodation.

Arising from recommendations in the Traveller Expert Review Group Report 2019, a new
Traveller identifier question was added to the social housing support application form (part
8) in March 2022. This allows the specific accommodation requirements of applicants,
who chose to identify as Travellers, to be recorded vis-ŕ-vis requiring Traveller specific
accommodation, mainstream social housing or any other social housing option, and will
allow progress in meeting their housing needs to be monitored effectively. This was first
reported in the 2022 count, which allowed 124 households to identify as Travellers.
In 2023, this increased to 512 households and by 2024, this increased again to 1,044
households. Local authorities were given a period of two years to review and update files
accordingly, and as of the 2024 count, the Traveller housing accommodation requirement
field previously used to measure Traveller accommodation requirements by proxy is now
defunct. Additionally, the `unanswered' option for the Traveller identifier is now available
for all local authorities, and not just the Dublin Region as in previous years. Therefore, more
direct comparisons can now be made between the 2024 and 2025 counts.

The 2025 count showed that 1,844 households identified as Irish Travellers (Table 2.6a). A
total of 30,392 responses were recorded as `No' on the application form/review form, while
1,906 were recorded as `Prefer not to say'. The remaining 27,577 responses
were unanswered.

The preferred housing option for households that identified as Travellers in 2025 are
reported separately in Table 2.6b. This shows that 1,761 (95.5%) of the 1,844 households
that identified as Travellers prefer mainstream local authority, AHB, HAP or RAS
accommodation. An additional 25 Traveller households (or 1.4%) are recorded as preferring
halting site accommodation or a caravan bay. A further 34 households (1.9%) did not specify
any particular housing option.

Wheelchair liveable housing option

Another housing option,12 `Wheelchair liveable' was introduced to a revised version of the
social housing application form in 2021. This addition is intended to help local authorities to
identify and prepare dwellings for social housing applicants who require housing specifically
catered to their needs. The 2025 SSHA identified 12 households with either a first or
second preferred housing option as `wheelchair liveable'. As this was added in recent years,
in addition to being an optional field in the assessment form, it is likely to take time for
these households to emerge in new and revised applications.

Current tenure

In 2025, there were 19,150 households residing in the private rented sector (Table 2.7), a
relative decrease of 5.9% (1,192 households) on the 2024 count. This figure, as a proportion
of the total list, represents a fall from over half of all households in 2016 (67.4%) to less
than one-third (31.0%) in 2025. Consequentially, this cohort no longer represents the
majority of households on the waiting list. Households living with parents, relatives and
friends collectively represent 23,216 households (or 37.6% of all households on the list).

The number and proportion of households with a current tenure of emergency
accommodation or no accommodation (rough sleeping) increased from 8,773 households in
2024 to 9,924 households in 2025 (from 14.6% to 16.1%), representing a relative increase
of 13.1% on 2024.

Direct provision accommodation type

In 2021, `Direct provision centre' was added to the social housing application form as a
new category of current accommodation type13. This provided local authorities with a more
accurate representation of the number of households in direct provision in recent years,
which for 2025 was 2,080.

Length of time on the housing waiting list

Table 2.8 shows that in 2025, there were 204 more households on the list for `more than
7 years'. While this represents a relative increase of 1.8% (204 households) on 2024, the
proportion of these households continues to decrease (from 19.2% in 2024 to 18.9% in
2025). This corresponds with an increase in those on the list for less than two years. The
proportion of those on the list for 1-2 years increased by 0.6 percentage points, from
18.6% in 2024 to 19.2% in 2025, while the proportion of those on the list for 6-12 months
decreased by 1 percentage point, from 12.3% in 2024 to 11.3% in 2025.14 There was
a marginal decrease in the proportion of households on the list for less than 6 months
between 2024 and 2025 (from 12.0% to 11.4%).

The greatest relative percentage change occurred in households on the list between 2
and 3 years, seeing an increase in 1,044 households (or 13.9% on the 2024 count). This
is followed by households on the list between 1 and 2 years, seeing an increase of 707
households (or 6.3% on the 2024 count). This could be related to households leaving the list
more quickly, for example, due to a shorter waiting time for those who enter the list in order
to access HAP housing15. It may also be related to the increase in households joining the list
in the period since the previous count date. Households who enter the list at a point in time
following the previous count date and are removed from the list before the next annual
assessment, either because they have been allocated social housing or for some other
reason, will not be included in the current count.

Main applicant citizenship

Following from previous counts, the majority of main applicants continue to be Irish citizens
(71.3%, or 43,991 households) (see Table 2.9). A total of 8,820 households, or 14.3% of
applicants, are citizens of other countries within the EEA. This year saw an increase of
860 households with main applicants from non-EEA countries, bringing the total to 8,142
households, or 13.2% of total households.

The basis of stay for the majority of households with non-EEA citizenship continues to be
`permission to remain in the State', however this proportion has decreased from 70.5% in
2024 to 58.2% in 2025. Consequently, there was an absolute increase of 12.5 percentage
points in non-EEA citizens whose basis of stay is `refugee', from 26.7% (1,946 households) in
2024 to 39.2% (3,193 households) in 202516. The proportion of non-EEA households with
`subsidiary protection status' decreased marginally, from 2.7% in 2024 to 2.6% in 2025.

In 2021, following the UK's withdrawal from the EU in 2020, a `UK citizen' category was
added to the nationality details on the social housing support application form. In 2025,
766 households were recorded as having UK citizenship.17
