1 
  Lord Mayor's Business 
 
In Memoriam 
A minutes silence was observed in remembrance of Nell McCaffrey, Journalist and 
Advocate for Social Justice and Women’s Rights, Edna O’Brien, Author, Jim Crowley 
former Dublin Corporation Housing Manager and All-Ireland Football Champion in 
1958 and Matt Twomey former Assistant City Manager.  
  
Noting that this is National Recovery Month, the Lord Mayor wished to remember all 
those who have died due to addiction. 
  
Olympics and Paralympics 
Congratulations were given to all our athletes in the Olympic Games held in Paris in 
August.  It was a pleasure to welcome them home on O’Connell Street on their return 
to Ireland and the Lord Mayor wished all our Paralympians who are currently 
competing in Paris the best of luck. 
  
Submission of Election Expenses Statement for the Local Elections 
Reminder given to members that a hard copy of this statement must be returned to 
Michael Gallagher in the Chief Executive’s Office by 5th September 2024.  Failure to 
make a submission will result in a Councillor being suspended and possibly 
disqualified.    
  
Presentation of plastic bag bin waste 
A presentation on the new Waste Management Strategy to deliver a cleaner Dublin 
City was provided by Barry Woods, Senior Executive Engineer, Waste Management.    
City Centre businesses will no longer be able to put out rubbish in plastic bags. A copy 
of this presentation is attached to these minutes in Appendix A. 
  
Task Force on Integration 
The Lord Mayor outlined his intention to establish a ‘Lord Mayor’s Integration 
Taskforce’ which will deliver an evidence based approach to community integration in 
Dublin City that supports resident communities as well as new communities and that 
can be used to provide recommendations to the Minister for Integration and 
separately, recommendations as how to maximise existing integration outreach in 
Dublin City Council, including through sport, while recognising that the role for housing 
international protection applicants is exclusively the competence of the Minister and 
not Dublin City Council.

Q.1 
COUNCILLOR JOHN LYONS 
To ask the Chief Executive to provide a detailed breakdown of the construction cost of 
each unit being built by Glenveagh on the public lands at the Oscar Traynor Road site; 
to provide a detailed analysis of the construction costs versus the affordable purchase 
price being charged for the 20% of new homes on the site;  to provide a detailed 
analysis of the construction costs versus the cost to Dublin City Council in acquring 
the new social homes; to provide a detailed analysis of the construction costs versus 
the proposed cost rental rents; and details on the original contract between Dublin City 
Council and Glenveagh and any changes to the agreed pricing structures and costs. 
 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
The tender price for each unit is made up of the entire development costs (hard and 
soft costs) and includes land costs, enabling works, abnormal works, infrastructure, 
construction costs for homes, apartments, civic amenities (crèche, community centre, 
etc.), retail units, Parks (5 parks over 3.6 Hectares), associated infrastructure and VAT. 
The tendered prices are commercially sensitive and cannot be released for legal and 
procurement reasons. This is for the purposes of ensuring the competitiveness of 
tenders in future to the Council.   
 
The Developer’s contract price is as fixed in the Development Agreement with the 
Council. There has been no variation in that price and there is no mechanism through 
which the Developer can seek increases in that price. All construction risk and cost 
overruns/inflation are the responsibility of the Developer. Therefore, in relation to 
Affordable Purchase homes, the Developer’s contract price is fixed in the Development 
agreement and does not move as a result of any changes in the market value of the 
affordable homes.     
 
Information given to the Elected Members in October 2021 (Table 1) was on the Price 
Range per Unit type, was excluding VAT, assumed subvention of €100,000 per unit 
and applied the €30,000 reduction available through the Help to Buy (HTB) scheme.

Q.13  COUNCILLOR PADDY MONAHAN 
To ask the Chief Executive if Dublin City Council will install visible timers at all 
pedestrian crossings so that pedestrians can see how long they must wait until they 
may cross the road. Further, can the Chief Executive ensure that a maximum waiting 
time applies to any pedestrian crossing in the Dublin City Council area - for example, 
30 seconds? 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
The Intelligent Transportation Systems team no longer recommend the installation of 
countdown pedestrian timers at pedestrian crossings in the city as these were 
previously found to be incompatible with the smart operation of the traffic signals via 
our SCATS Adaptive Traffic Management System. For example, pedestrian 
countdown timers are required to count down predictably from a fixed time which 
reduces the adaptability of SCATS to allocate time appropriately to other movements 
such as bus corridors or to provide high priority calls for the Luas. We have also found 
from past experience that countdown timers interfere with the flexibility required of 
traffic control room operators to allocate time to congested movements as the 
pedestrian countdown timer causes certain traffic phases to run for set times. We have 
also previously encountered technical issues with these timers where they have 
remained ‘stuck’ on the last second of the countdown potentially causing safety issues 
for pedestrians. 
 
Regarding the issue of wait time for pedestrians – the ITS section endeavour to 
minimise wait times for pedestrians where practicable through a range of measures 
including lower maximum cycle times at junctions, double cycling of the pedestrian 
phase among other things. However, we treat each junction on a case by case basis. 
For busy public transport corridors restricting the wait time to a defined maximum 
would significantly impact public transport priority and upset the balance that we strive 
for in servicing sustainable and active modes of transport. We will continue to provide 
the optimum level of service for pedestrians and are happy to examine specific 
junctions in greater detail should you wish to contact us about these directly.

Q.28  COUNCILLOR DANNY BYRNE 
To ask the Chief Executive for the up to date figure re rent arrears. 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
Dublin City Council has over 26,000 tenancies with active rent accounts from our 
Council properties. The total rent arrears owed to Dublin City Council on the 
19/08/2024 was €39,377,942.73 and the credit on rent accounts was €4,969,300.08. 
Approximately, 68% of the total number of our live tenancies have rent accounts that 
are compliant. The remaining 32% of rent accounts are split between accounts that 
are in rescheduling agreements (where tenants pay their weekly rent and an additional 
portion to reduce their arrears), and accounts with irregular payments. 
 
It is essential that all tenants pay their rent as per their tenancy agreement and the 
Council has a dedicated team of Executive Housing Officers, who engage with tenants 
where issues with rent might arise. 
 
Where a tenant has missed rent payments, the Executive Housing Officer will attempt 
to engage with the tenant to resolve the matter. If the tenant cannot be contacted or 
does not engage in a meaningful way, we then proceed to our early intervention system 
that alerts tenants to the increasing nature of the problem, with a first, second, and 
then a third warning letter. 
 
If the problem persists and rent arrears continue to be unresolved, a Statutory Tenancy 
Warning letter will be issued. This is the start of the legal process and we strongly 
encourage tenants to avoid the risks of Court proceedings by engaging with the 
Council and reaching an agreement to prevent the matter from escalating further.

Q.35  COUNCILLOR MANNIX FLYNN 
To ask the Chief Executive to issue a report as to the legislation regarding the use of 
drones for deliveries within the Dublin City Council administrative area. This report to 
include whether such devices are exempt from planning in relation to the airspace and 
the basis that these devices are flown from?  
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
The Irish Aviation Authority (IAA) are the national body responsible for the 
implementation of the EU (European Union Aviation Safety Agency – EASA) 
regulations of drone (UAV) flights in the airspace above Dublin and the rest of the 
country. Any drone delivery company seeking to fly within the airspace above the DCC 
administration area can only do so when they have been certified and granted 
permission by the IAA allowing them to fly under specific conditions e.g. flying beyond 
visual line of sight from the pilot. 
 
To date, Dublin City Council has received no planning applications relating to the use 
of land for Drone take-off/landing bases. The existing Planning Legislation does not 
currently define or set out regulations regarding the statutory planning requirements 
for such applications, each application would therefore need to be considered on its 
own merits, on a case-by-case basis and subject to the specific site location, 
designations and existing land use in the vicinity.

“The establishment of the states first Medically Supervised Injection Facility forms part 
of the National Drugs Strategy and MQI was selected by the HSE to operate the 
service at its Riverbank facility on Merchants Quay. The facility will operate out of the 
basement at riverbank and will complement the  harm reduction and primary health

in the oversight committee and the composition of the committee is a matter for the 
HSE. The facility will operate for fifty fours hours per week over a seven day period. 
The facility will comprise of an a reception area, seven injecting bays and a recovery 
room. It will be staffed by a combination of nursing, addiction support staff and a visiting 
GP. Refurbishment works on the development of the MSIF began in June 2024 and 
the facility will commence operations in late 2024. 
 
MQI have established three fora to oversee and manage the MSIF project.  
 
1. MSIF Delivery Group 
This group coordinates all strands of the project to ensure that the facility 
commences operation in Q4 2024, the delivery group, which comprises MQI and 
HSE representatives, has met  on ten occasions to date. 
2. MSIF Refurbishment Project Group 
This group was established to monitor the works program and ensure that timelines 
and expedenditure remain within proifile. This project team is currently meeting on 
a forthnightly basis. 
3. MSIF Stakeholder Forum 
Prior to commencemnt of the refurbishment works a stakeholder forum was 
convened providing a mechansim for ongoing engagement with community, 
school, business, people with lived experience and statutory and non-statutory 
representatives, to ensure the efficient operation of the facility and to protect the 
amenity and safety of the local neighbourhood. The stakeholder forum has met on 
three occasions. A City Council official from the South Central Area was invited to 
be a member of this Forum and attends meetings. 
 
I wish to assure all local elected representatives that MQI is committed to open and 
transparent engagement regarding the development of the MSIF. It is our intention to 
host a number of walk-throughs of the facility when completed and invitations will be 
extended to all local representatives at the appropriate time”.

City Development Plan namely:-  
“College Green Dame Street Project. To implement a programme of environmental 
and public realm improvements along the Grand Civic Spine from Parnell Square to

CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
The Homeless HAP (HHAP) scheme is in place to assist those availing of emergency 
accommodation to exit such accommodation with the assistance of a deposit and the 
first two months’ rent at the appropriate rate paid in advance. It is also available to 
those who are considered as being imminently homeless due to receiving a Notice to 
quit and subsequently being made eligible for the scheme following assessment by the 
appropriate team in the Dublin Regional Homeless Executive. 
Once the tenancy is in place, the tenant is considered housed and no longer homeless. 
A tenant availing of HHAP may move from the property after two years, in line with the 
overall HAP scheme. However, as they are no longer considered homeless, this 
movement would be under the mainstream HAP scheme. The maximum available 
under mainstream HAP is at a lower level than HHAP. In this case the tenant should 

seek to recoup the deposit paid on their behalf under HHAP from their landlord/agent 
to help them secure a new tenancy. 
If a Prohibition Notice is served by an Officer from the Environmental Health Section 
on a premises tenanted by a HHAP tenant, the tenant is automatically eligible to look 
for a property under HHAP. Also, if a Garda notice indicates there is a risk to the tenant 
if the tenant remains in place, the tenant is automatically eligible for HHAP.

Estimated Housing Costs (social and Affordable):  
 
Since the letter of 9th July 2021, a final grant of planning was only received on 16th May 
2022. Bartra were to be on site within four weeks of this. However, delays to the 
O’Devaney Gardens Phase 1a completion meant that the Council were unable to make 
the Phase 2 site fully available to the Developer.  
 
Together with this, a number of unprecedented and challenging events occurred over 
this period. These included Brexit, Covid and the Ukrainian Conflict all of which 
understandably presented difficulties to the Council and the developer in mobilising 
construction at O’Devaney Gardens. As is widely understood, these events led to large 
increases in the costs of construction materials. Construction cost inflation over this 
period rose to 24.6% (SCSI). For example, the Detailed Wholesale Price Indices for 
Building and Construction Materials published by the Central Statistics Office showed 
increases for materials such as timber and reinforcing steel in excess of 60% over 12 
months.  
 

As a result of these impacts, the financial model underpinning the original Development 
Agreement became unviable such that Bartra were not be able to deliver the Project 
in line with the original tender process commenced in 2017. DCC and Bartra engaged 
for a period of over six months in 2022 with a view to considering what measures could 
be taken to address these issues in order that the O’Devaney Gardens Project might 
be delivered. This was in the context of the urgent need for the delivery of homes in 
the city.   
 
At the same time (July 2022) the Office of Government Procurement devised the 
Inflation/Supply chain Delay Co-operation Framework Agreement for use on Public 
Works contracts. This was for the purposes of managing inflation costs with respect to 
construction projects and was applied to all other Housing construction projects 
underway by the Council at that time. As such DCC examined a solution for delivering 
O’Devaney Gardens that aligned with the Government’s co-operation framework and 
agreed a methodology for dealing with inflation costs related to this. A subsequent 
uplift in costs representing little over half of actual construction cost inflation was 
agreed.  Detailed analysis of the costs were undertaken by Construction Cost 
consultants Mitchell McDermott in a value for money exercise review. A detailed Risk 
Assessment and options analysis was undertaken by Mazars. All options open to the 
Council were considered. It is notable that any option to collapse and retender the 
project would have resulted in delays of 3-4 years at a minimum and would be 
impacted by increased construction cost inflation in the order of 15 -20%, this excludes 
all other project related costs which would also be subject to impact of inflation of this 
period. This Solution was presented to the Housing Land Initiative Project Governance 
Board and Department of Housing.   
 
In line with EU Legal and Procurement Regulations a Modification notice was 
published in December 2022 ensuring the required transparency and the Solution was 
approved by the Housing Land Initiative Board in a meeting in February 2023 ensuring 
construction commenced in March 2023.

Q.97  COUNCILLOR CAT O’DRISCOLL 
To ask the Chief Executive what policy has changed in relation to weddings in City Hall 
and are religious ceremonies catered for. How many wedding took place in City Hall 
last year and how many were religious ceremonies? 
 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
The Health Service Executive (HSE) is responsible for the civil registration service 
nationally in the Republic of Ireland.  
In order to host civil marriage ceremonies, City Hall is bound by the regulations set out 
by the HSE’s Civil Registration Service Arrangements for the Approval of Venues for 
the Solemnisation of Civil Marriage. 
These regulations prevented venues from hosting both civil and religious ceremonies.  
In July 2024, following legal action, the HSE revised these regulations. 
The new HSE guidance on the Civil Registration Service Arrangements for the 
Approval of Venues, relates to any venue which holds wedding ceremonies nationally. 
All wedding venues nationally are required to implement the HSE guidance on the Civil 
Registration Service Arrangements for the Approval of Venues. 
As a result of these changes, the legal advice to the Council made clear that City Hall 
could no longer host civil ceremonies without also accepting religious marriage 
ceremonies. 
The Senior Management Team subsequently agreed that City Hall would also host 
civil, humanist and religious wedding ceremonies in future.  
A report will be made to the Protocol Committee on these changes in September.    
In 2023, there were 202 weddings in City Hall – all of which were civil or humanist.

Q.104  COUNCILLOR PUNAM RANE 
To ask the Chief Executive what plans are in place by Dublin City Council for any 
structures built in the DCC area that must have facilities that support the disabled. If 
new facilities and developments will take on board the inconveniences that the 
disabled face on a daily basis, therefore making it easier to allow disabled people to 
access facilities in a more sufficient manner"? 
 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
All new buildings, extensions to buildings, and material alterations to existing buildings, 
are required to comply with the requirements of the Building Regulations, including 
Part M (Access and Use).   Oversight of compliance with the requirements of the 
Building Regulations is provided by the Building Control Unit who inspect a very large 
proportion of all new buildings constructed in the city.  Many new buildings are also 
required to apply for a Disability Access Certificate where the design is expertly 
assessed for compliance with the requirements of Part M by our Building Control 
Officers.    
The Disability Act 2005 requires Dublin City Council, as a public body, to 
retrospectively comply with the requirements of the Part M of the Building Regulations 
(Access and Use), with respect to the public areas of its public buildings, no later than 
10 years from the date of any revision to the regulations.  The most recent revision to 
Part M came into operation on January 1st 2024 and Dublin City Council is actively 
examining the implementation of these revisions which includes the provision of 
Changing Places Toilet Facilities in certain buildings.    
 
Compliance with the Building Regulations (including Part M) is mandatory for all new 
buildings. Part M aims to improve accessibility to the built environment for all 
regardless of age or ability, these regulations require that people can safely and 
independently approach gain access and use it facilities.

Dublin City Council BETA  
Dublin City Council BETA is an initiative created to imagine, trial and establish

understand and respond to issues, and for citizens it’s an easier way to be heard on 
these issues.

(Two examples of solutions which arose from this initiative: Dublin Canvas boxes - a

close to their home and under cover, saving space and worry). 
 
SharedBinsBETA

dumping and waste – one of which is the Shared Bins project.

improve the domestic waste experience for residents of urban neighbourhoods and 
ensure cleaner streets and more navigable footpaths.
