# BI Spinout — Final Independent Assessment

## 1. Bottom line

**Verdict: PROCEED-WITH-CHANGES. Confidence: medium-high.** All five lenses independently reached the same verdict, and the disagreements between them are about ordering and scope, not direction. The core architecture (one curated core, two front doors) and the reports-first sequencing are right, and the honesty rails are genuinely structural rather than aspirational. But the plan as written has three load-bearing defects: it builds engineering (Stage A trust-UI) before it has a single piece of demand evidence; its two mid-tier report products (€4k–10k) depend on PHASE-2-TODO views that contradict the "packaging, not a build" thesis, so the first buyer-dossier or category-map sale triggers an unplanned pipeline build under customer deadline; and two owner overrides — selling ministerial-diary co-occurrence and shipping Iris-derived distress facts before solicitor clearance — attach unbounded reputational/legal downside to the free civic product (the plan's own stated only-durable-asset) in exchange for revenue no pricing tier actually depends on. Fix those three and this is a cheap, self-terminating experiment worth running; skip them and you are risking the asset to fund the hypothesis.

## 2. The strongest cases

**FOR (steelmanned):**
- Reports-first is close to the cheapest possible test of this market: no accounts, no GDPR lift, no scheduler; a failed Stage B costs weeks of analyst time against infrastructure that is already built, deployed, and paid for by the free product. The 43-view spine, live API, and PSI inputs mean marginal cost per report is analyst hours only.
- The awards↔payments reconciliation is a real, unserved differentiator: no free tool (eTenders, TED) and no UK incumbent reconciles award ceilings against actual cash across Irish registers, and the never-sum/honest-coverage discipline is exactly what converts to money with consultants and journalists, where a wrong number in a client deliverable is a fireable offence.
- The free civic product is distribution most data startups pay years for — journalist citations, an already-live explorer, credibility with the exact people who introduce you to buyers.
- The execution rigour is unusually high: the Stage A/B plan is ground-truthed to real symbols and line numbers, caught a concrete 20,314-row/€1.04bn PII leak path before it happened, and corrected its own stale claims. That discipline is itself evidence the paid product's accuracy pitch is deliverable.

**AGAINST (steelmanned):**
- There is zero demand evidence anywhere in five documents — no interviews, no named prospects, no pre-sale — in a market whose bottom-up ceiling is plausibly €100k–€400k ARR, against free official eTenders/TED alerts and UK incumbents (Stotles, BiP/Tracker, Tenders Direct) that already index Irish notices. The pricing is anchored to comparables from a market ~15x larger.
- The product's own honesty guts its flagship: ~7% payments visibility, ~35% award↔payment linkage, ~33% CPV coding (0/2,363 on the live snapshot), 6-month award lag. The chosen design-partner archetype — a mid-size supplier who knows its own history — is precisely the customer who will spot the gaps immediately and anchor the value down. "Rigour → honest gaps → unimpressive report → no repurchase" is a coherent, likely failure mode the docs never confront.
- A solo founder cannot simultaneously run a home-machine, WAF-constrained refresh pipeline, hand-write €1.5k–10k reports, do direct sales, and maintain the free product's backlog. The DoD's "second-analyst spot-check" — impossible for a solo shop — is a symptom of a plan written for a team that doesn't exist.
- Even a fully successful Stage B validates bespoke consulting, not the Phase-5 alerts SaaS that is the actual recurring-revenue product. The most expensive build decision would rest on evidence about a different product.

## 3. What the author got right

1. **The sequencing and the deferral.** Reports → metered API → SaaS, with the GDPR/identity/scheduler lift owner-gated at the end, is the correct crawl-walk-run for a solo builder. "Separate full SaaS now" and clean-room were both correctly rejected.
2. **One core, two front doors.** Splitting at the serving/identity layer, not the data layer, avoids the classic spinout maintenance death. Sole copyright makes clean-room pointless; the doc saw that.
3. **Structural honesty rails.** Verbatim caveats bound into templates, the supplier_class/public_display double-gate verified per surface, hard-fail-rather-than-fuzzy-match, honest stubbing of unbuilt sections. This is enforcement by construction, and it caught real defects (the payments-export leak, the two-view year-path break) before they shipped.
4. **§11's self-critique.** Free data, small TAM, free official competition, 7% coverage, key-person risk — all named, and the original free-only recommendation preserved next to the owner override rather than retconned. Most strategy docs never write this section.
5. **The graduation signal.** "A partner pays again unprompted" is a genuinely good revenue-quality test — it just needs a threshold, a price condition, and a clock (see P0-5).

## 4. Top risks, ranked, with mitigations

1. **Unvalidated demand drives an engineering commitment.** Stage A builds five trust-UI primitives before any sales conversation — the classic solo-technical-founder inversion. *Mitigation:* flip the order. Attempt to pre-sell 2–3 supplier dossiers to named Irish bid consultancies within a fixed 6-week window before writing Stage A code; a signed commitment (even €1k) is the gate for building anything.
2. **Diary-as-paid contaminates the funnel.** One journalist framing — "Dáil Tracker author sells ministers' meeting data to state-contract bidders" — is un-rebuttable by caveat text and burns the free product's trust, which §13 designates as the entire top-of-funnel. Revenue at stake: approximately zero (optional panel, no tier priced on it). *Mitigation:* remove diary data from paid scope entirely (see §7).
3. **The mid-tier products are unshippable as specified.** The buyer dossier (9 PHASE-2-TODO markers) and category market map (13) have their most saleable sections — buyer concentration, top suppliers/buyers per CPV, year trends, per-buyer competition — stubbed. *Mitigation:* either rescope Stage B to supplier-dossiers-only at €1.5k–3k, or add the minimum view set (top_suppliers_for_authority, top_suppliers_for_cpv, top_buyers_for_cpv, awards_for_cpv, year trends, wire `competition()`) to Stage A as "Primitive 6" with effort estimates. Hard rule: never take money for a report whose landscape/competition sections render blank.
4. **Iris ships to paying customers before clearance.** The doc calls Iris "the weak link & highest legal risk," then includes it pending review — and the copyright-only analysis ignores the gazette's possible sui generis database right and the "personal use only" site-terms contract claim. Exposure begins at the first sale, not at scale. *Mitigation:* flip the gate to confirm-before-first-sale; stub distress sections like any PHASE-2-TODO; expand the solicitor brief to database right + site terms; have the solicitor simply request re-use permission from Government Publications. If cleared, Iris facts go in hand-built reports only — never exports or API, where the ToS pass-through cannot grant rights you don't hold.
5. **Coverage reality vs the flagship pitch.** 7% payments, 33% CPV, 6-month lag hit exactly the sections all three templates centre on. *Mitigation:* scope the sales pitch to market-mapping and renewal-planning (where lag is tolerable), not tender-chasing; state the freshness envelope on the report cover; lead every reconciliation section with the coverage bound rather than letting the customer discover it.
6. **Professional-liability exposure of paid reports.** A wrong distress flag or single-bid figure in a €5k document used in a bid decision is a defamation/negligence claim vector; the solicitor checklist covers data licensing only. *Mitigation:* engagement-letter template with liability cap and "information, not advice" disclaimer, PI insurance, and a published correction/complaints policy — all before the first invoice. Cheap now, expensive to retrofit after a dispute.
7. **Solo-operator ops and QA gates that can't be executed.** The second-analyst DoD will be silently waived; the refresh pipeline runs from one home machine behind the gov.ie WAF; paid reports have no snapshot pinning, so "numbers reproduce from the query layer" is false after the next refresh. *Mitigation:* replace the human gate with a scripted reproduction check that re-derives every headline figure and diffs it (extend the existing, un-referenced `pipeline_sandbox/bid_intelligence/` prototype — bid_pack.py/render_report.py already do half of this); pin every shipped report to a manifest (parquet hashes + data_currency + view-layer git SHA) archived to R2; document the WAF/home-box freshness constraint before any currency promise appears in an engagement letter.
8. **No kill criteria.** A lukewarm Stage B will drift into Stage C by momentum. *Mitigation:* pre-commit in the plan doc: "if fewer than 2 of 5 design partners repurchase at non-discounted price within two quarters of report #1, park the spinout and evaluate the grant route with equal rigour."

## 5. Prioritised changes (deduplicated)

**P0 — before any sales conversation or first sale:**
1. **Sell before building.** 5–10 structured conversations with named Irish bid consultancies and active SME suppliers; attempt to pre-sell one report each at design-partner rates within 6 weeks. Include a one-day competitive scan of Stotles/Tussell/BiP-Tracker/Tenders Direct Irish coverage and pricing — reposition or cut the SME tier against it. No committed spend → no Stage A primitives.
2. **Split the co-occurrence decision:** diary out of paid entirely (including the buyer-dossier who_ministers_meet panel); lobbying-register co-occurrence stays, but per-report owner-gated and never in bulk exports/API (see §7).
3. **Flip the Iris gate** to confirm-before-first-sale, expand the solicitor scope (sui generis right, site-terms contract, Art. 6(1)(f) analysis for any office-holder data, ePrivacy SI 336/2011 for digests), and carve Iris to reports-only even after clearance.
4. **Reconcile "packaging vs build":** rescope Stage B to supplier-dossiers-only, or pull the report-critical Phase-2 views into Stage A as Primitive 6. Simultaneously produce **one fully-filled sample report** per sellable template — real supplier, real numbers, owner string-gates signed for the sample — with a **key-facts front page** (5–7 facts ranked by decision relevance; selection and ordering are editorial, not inference, and fully rails-compatible) and **caveats as inline chips referencing a one-page methodology annex** carrying each verbatim string once. The sample is the sales artifact, the owner-gate forcing function, and the first honest test of whether the output reads as worth paying for.
5. **Pre-commit numeric kill/graduation criteria** with a clock (per risk #8).
6. **Legal shell for paid work:** engagement letter with liability cap, PI insurance, published correction/complaints policy — as a sixth Stage B DoD gate.

**P1 — before Stage C / scaling:**
7. Snapshot pinning + R2 archiving for every shipped report; replace the second-analyst DoD with the scripted reproduction check, built by extending `pipeline_sandbox/bid_intelligence/`.
8. Insert a **concierge-alerts step** into Stage B: manually-run weekly tender-diff digest emailed to contracted design partners (contractual basis, no accounts, no scheduler). Stage E's gate becomes "partners would pay to keep the digest," not just "partner buys a second report" — otherwise the SaaS is entered on evidence about a different product.
9. Comparative baselines as pipeline-owned view columns (buyer single-bid rate vs national median, CPV median vs all-CPV median), estimated end-date derivation as a registered view so the supplier report gets a real renewal calendar, and relax the awarded-vs-paid rail to permit *coverage-bounded* narrative comparison — the customer does the division in five seconds anyway; refusing to speak while showing both numbers is theatre, not integrity. Ban unbounded ratios, not bounded ones.
10. Publish an ethics-and-funding disclosure on the civic site plus a one-page editorial-independence policy (paying customers get no influence over or advance notice of free-side coverage). Fix the press inconsistency: journalist design partners participate free, consistent with §8.
11. Resolve forbidden-vocabulary enforcement as **CI-enforced**, one canonical list (superset of the three documents' variants), scanned over rendered report prose.
12. Write the missing unit-economics one-pager: hours per report incl. QA, bottom-up customer counts per tier, 3-year ARR ceiling with/without EU-UK extension, founder time-split. This is what decides whether Stage E is ever worth its GDPR/SLA burden.
13. Document the WAF/home-machine freshness dependency and a mitigation before any freshness expectation is offered.

**P2:**
14. Evaluate public-sector buyers (councils, OGP, departments wanting competition/single-bid benchmarking) as a customer class — plausibly the highest-WTP segment in a small market, absent from all five documents, and where Tussell derives real revenue.
15. Before Stage C, write the grant/institutional-funding alternative memo with the same rigour as §7's option table — the one comparison the docs never make.
16. Fix the cross-document effort scale (L means opposite things in the two docs), add calendar estimates, and make the Stage D frontend's repo placement (public AGPL vs private) an explicit owner decision. Note the unreconciled fact that the public repo already contains the spine *and* the gold parquet — the "sell curation, not data" pitch needs a sentence on why a lawful clone doesn't matter (answer: ops, freshness, trust, analyst time — Tussell over free UK data proves it — but say so).
17. Prepare the scripted, rails-compatible answer to the inevitable in-meeting question "so should we bid?" — facts-plus-questions framing — or partners will experience the no-verdict discipline as evasiveness.

## 6. Blind spots the documents never address

- **Irish competitive landscape** — incumbents are cited only as pricing anchors, never as competitors; the SME tier is priced blind against free official alerts.
- **Bottom-up TAM and founder-hours economics** — prices exist, volumes and costs don't; "lifestyle niche vs something bigger" is currently a vibe.
- **Public-sector buyers as a segment.**
- **Report reproducibility** — gold parquet mutates every refresh; the challenge-a-figure-in-six-months scenario the trust UI exists for is unanswerable without snapshot pinning.
- **Defamation/correction/PI insurance** for paid documents that flag named companies' distress and bid patterns.
- **Source-revocation risk** — no contingency for eTenders/OGP or a payments publisher changing terms once visible commercialisation begins; PSI publishers have historically reacted exactly this way.
- **The AGPL-clone reality** and the public gold parquet vs the moat narrative.
- **Opportunity cost** — grants/philanthropic/EU civic-tech funding could plausibly match the realistic ARR ceiling with none of the SLA/GDPR/reputational load; it gets zero pages.
- **Stage-B-era GDPR** — design-partner contacts and billing make you a controller at the first invoice, not at Phase 5.

## 7. Verdict on the contested decision: co-occurrence-only lobbying/diary as paid features

**Split it. Diary out of paid — permanently, or at minimum until well past validation. Lobbying stays, gated.** Three lenses examined this and the disagreement is only about the lobbying half. The decisive points: (a) "co-occurrence, never a score" governs the arithmetic but not the *meaning of the sale* — a bid consultant buys the diary panel precisely because minister-proximity matters to winning, so the commercial context performs the inference the caveat disclaims; (b) the premise on which the owner accepted the risk ("not legal, just reputational") is wrong for diaries — office-holder diaries are personal data, and commercial resale is a different Art. 6(1)(f) balancing than civic republication, asserted but never analysed; (c) the revenue is approximately zero — the panel is optional and no tier is priced on it — while the downside is the free product's trust, which the strategy itself calls the only durable asset and the entire top-of-funnel; (d) reversal costs nothing built — the diary data-access path is PHASE-2-TODO. The lobbying-register half is genuinely different: SIPO's PSI policy invites republication, supplier-keyed due-diligence facts are standard commercial practice, and the existing per-report owner gate is adequate — keep it, but never in bulk exports or the API, and move the award-€ totals out of the same table as lobbying counts so the layout doesn't compose the causation narrative the caveats deny.

## 8. The one experiment that de-risks the whole bet

**Pre-sell the one product that is fully built.** Fill one real supplier dossier end-to-end (real supplier, key-facts page, caveat annex, snapshot manifest), then spend six weeks trying to close 2–3 named Irish bid consultancies at €1.5k–3k each — before Stage A code, before Phase-2 views, before any brand work. Cost: roughly one week of analyst time plus outreach. It simultaneously tests willingness-to-pay at the real price point, surfaces the Tussell/Stotles objection live, forces the owner string-gates to be signed, and produces the reference artifact everything downstream needs. The decisive follow-on signal is already latent in the plan and just needs numbers attached: **an unprompted, non-discounted repeat purchase within two quarters.** If it happens, the niche is real and every remaining problem is scope discipline. If it doesn't, park everything past Stage B and write the grant memo.

**Weaknesses**
- [critical] Zero demand evidence anywhere in five documents. ### Architecture & Sequencing — verdict: **proceed-with-changes** (high confidence)

**Headline:** The one-core/two-front-doors architecture and the reports-first-before-Phase-5 sequencing are correct, but Stage A spends roughly half its effort on free-app UI chrome the static PDF reports never render through, while the pipeline views the buyer and category reports actually need (9 and 13 PHASE-2-TODO holes respectively) are left out of Stage A entirely — and even a successful Stage B validates consulting demand, not the Phase-5 alerts SaaS that is the real recurring product.

**Single biggest risk:** The sequencing validates the wrong thing while missing its own dependencies: Stage A invests in free-app trust chrome that the static PDF reports never render through, the pipeline views the buyer and category reports actually require are deferred to an unscheduled Phase 2, and even a fully successful Stage B only proves demand for bespoke consulting — leaving the Phase-5 alerts SaaS, the actual recurring-revenue product, unvalidated at the moment the most expensive build decision must be made.


**Strengths**

### Ethics, Legal & Reputation — verdict: **proceed-with-changes** (high confidence)

**Headline:** The no-inference rails are genuinely well-engineered and mostly structural, but the two owner overrides — selling ministerial-diary access and shipping Iris-derived data before legal review — concentrate exactly the reputational and legal exposure the architecture was built to avoid, and the diary half of the co-occurrence decision should be reversed.

**Single biggest risk:** The ministerial-diary-access paid feature: 'co-occurrence only' governs the arithmetic but not the meaning of the sale, and one journalist framing it as 'the Dáil Tracker author sells minister-access data to state-contract bidders' irreversibly burns the free civic product's trust — the asset §4 itself calls the only durable one — in exchange for revenue no tier even prices.


**Strengths**
- Caveat discipline is structural, not aspirational: verbatim strings from dail_tracker_core/caveats.py and api/routers/exports.py are bound into every template section, with an explicit never-soften rule (supplier template rail 8) — this is rare and real.
- The company-class/PII double-gate (supplier_class / public_display / privacy_status) is designed in at snapshot/export time, and the Stage A/B plan even catches a concrete leak path (v_procurement_payments lacking public_display would expose 20,314 individual rows / €1.04bn) and the awards_for_authority individual-row trap in the category template step 7 — the gating is verified per-surface, not assumed.
- Honest stubbing: PHASE-2-TODO sections must stay blank rather than be hand-computed, the buyer-dossier competition panel hard-fails on non-exact TED buyer_name match rather than fuzzy-matching, and unmatched records are counted and shown — these prevent the classic paid-report fabrication failure.
- The architecture doc's own risk candour (§11) is above average: small TAM, free official competition, ~7% payments coverage, key-person concentration are stated rather than papered over, and the doc preserves the original free-only recommendation alongside the owner override instead of retconning it.
- Attribution rigour: per-source verbatim strings including the exact TED Decision 2011/833/EU wording, [owner to confirm] placeholders for EPA/diaries rather than invented licences, and ToS pass-through of attribution/never-sum/no-re-identification to re-publishing customers (§6.3) — most projects miss pass-through entirely.

**Weaknesses**
- [critical] Selling ministerial-diary access in a paid competitor-intelligence product is monetising political-access proximity, and 'co-occurrence, never a score' does not neutralise that — the commercial context performs the inference the caveat disclaims. A bid consultant buys the diary panel precisely because 'this supplier meets the minister' matters to winning; placing diary counts beside 'won €X awarded' in one dossier table (supplier template OPTIONAL PANEL) composes the influence narrative without ever computing a number. The (ii-extended) buyer-dossier rule makes it worse: who_ministers_meet on a buyer dossier is a minister-keyed access map sold to bidders, however the 'subject' is labelled. — The story 'civic transparency site's author sells minister-access data to companies bidding for state contracts' writes itself, is un-rebuttable by caveat text, and destroys the free product's trust — which §4 itself calls the only durable asset. The incremental revenue is negligible (the panel is optional and no tier is priced on it), so the risk/reward is plainly bad. The lobbying-register half is defensibly different (SIPO's PSI policy invites republication; supplier-keyed due-diligence facts are standard commercial practice); the diary half is not — its only commercial meaning is access-mapping.
- [major] §4 asserts the lobbying/diary risk 'is not legal, it is reputational.' That is wrong for diaries: a minister's diary is personal data of a natural person. Re-publishing it on a free civic-accountability site rests comfortably on public-interest/public-task reasoning; selling it inside a commercial intelligence product is a different Art. 6(1)(f) legitimate-interests balancing (commercial gain vs the office-holder's — admittedly thin — privacy interest), and neither the architecture doc nor §6.4 performs that analysis. — The claim that only reputation is at stake anchors the owner's risk acceptance on an incomplete premise. Probably still defensible for official-capacity data, but 'legally permissible' is asserted, not analysed, and GDPR is the regime a disgruntled office-holder or the DPC would actually reach for.
- [major] The Iris Oifigiúil analysis is copyright-only (facts vs expression) and ignores two other heads of exposure: (1) a possible sui generis database right in the gazette as a compilation — extraction/re-utilisation of substantial parts is restricted independently of copyright in the text, and §6.3 only considers the author's OWN database right; (2) the site terms' 'personal use only' as a contract claim — systematic commercial extraction is the exact negation of the permitted use. The docs also make no distinction between Iris facts in a hand-built PDF report (low exposure, contained) and Iris-derived fields flowing into bulk exports/API (redistribution you cannot licence downstream — the ToS pass-through cannot grant rights you don't hold). — §15 says 'include in the paid product' unqualified, so an engineer wiring exports would reasonably include corporate-distress fields. The mitigations are cheap (report-only carve-out; ask Government Publications for re-use permission) but absent. Practical enforcement risk is low — commercial gazette republication has decades of Irish precedent (Stubbs Gazette et al.) — but that precedent is nowhere cited or relied on either.
- [major] No error-correction, complaints, or right-of-reply process exists anywhere in Stage B for paid reports that carry reputationally damaging facts about named companies — corporate-distress flags, single-bid rates, ambiguous CRO matches. A paid dossier that wrongly flags a solvent company as in receivership (or attributes another entity's distress notice via the known one-name-→-112-companies ambiguity) is defamation with commercial publication. — The MVL-is-solvent caveat and the ambiguous-match gates reduce but cannot eliminate error; the moment money changes hands, 'we showed the caveat' is a weaker defence and damages are real. A correction policy, complaint route, and professional-indemnity cover are standard for paid intelligence products and cost little; their absence is the most likely route to an actual legal letter.
- [major] The brand firewall is contradicted by the go-to-market: §13 explicitly uses the civic product as the paid product's funnel (links from procurement pages, journalists' citations as 'the awareness engine', free analyses framed as 'content marketing'), while no document commits to (a) publicly disclosing the commercial arm on the civic site, (b) an editorial-independence policy when a paying customer appears unfavourably on the free side, or (c) how press queries about one product from the same solo author are handled. — A transparency site that quietly lead-generates for a commercial product owned by the same person is the classic own-goal — the conflict is manageable, but only if disclosed proactively rather than discovered by a journalist. Separate branding does not separate a solo founder.
- [minor] Enforcement of the no-inference vocabulary is left undecided (Stage A/B decision #6: 'CI-enforced or advisory'), and bespoke reports — hand-written analyst prose — are precisely the surface where verdict-framing will leak under sales pressure, guarded today only by a manual checklist item. — §10 itself predicts the no-list 'is the thing most likely to be eroded under sales pressure', then leaves its own enforcement optional. The forbidden-word lists also differ between the three documents (the category template adds 'uncompetitive', 'red flag'; the architecture doc does not), so 'the' binding list is ambiguous.
- [minor] GDPR coverage has practical gaps beyond Phase 5: ePrivacy Regulations (SI 336/2011) for email digests are never named; Stage B itself processes personal data (design-partner contacts, billing) with no privacy notice mentioned; no Art. 16/17 rectification-erasure process for published data; and 'company-class only' does not fully dispose of personal data — one-person companies and eponymous partnerships ('John Murphy Ltd') identify natural persons. — None is fatal, but §6.4's framing ('budget for a privacy notice + DPA review before launching alerts') implies GDPR starts at Phase 5, which is incorrect — controller obligations begin with the first design-partner invoice.
- [minor] Internal inconsistency: §8 keeps press/academia free 'to protect the civic brand', but Stage B recruits an 'agency/journalist desk' as a PAYING design partner. — Charging a newsroom for procurement intelligence while claiming press stays free is a small hypocrisy a competitor or critic can quote.

**Recommended changes**

Retain lobbying-register co-occurrence as paid, but per-report owner-gated (as the supplier template already requires) and never in bulk exports/API. — The diary panel is minister-keyed access-mapping whose only commercial meaning is the inference the caveats disclaim; it is the single feature that can destroy the free brand, for near-zero revenue. The lobbying half is defensible (SIPO PSI policy invites republication; supplier-keyed due diligence is standard), so a split preserves most of the commercial value while shedding most of the risk. Conveniently, the diary path is currently PHASE-2-TODO (no data_access function exists) — reversing costs nothing built.

- [major] Nothing forward-looking beyond published end dates and open tenders: no pipeline forecast, no 'expected re-tender volume', and win-probability/recommended-price are forbidden vocabulary (correctly), but no rails-safe substitute (e.g. 'historical re-tender rate for this buyer's expiring frameworks' as a stored view fact) is offered. — Bid intelligence is bought for the future. Competitors sell forecasting; this product's honest substitute (dates + historical cadence facts) is achievable within the rails but is currently PHASE-2-TODO (renewal_cycle) or absent, leaving the reports mostly retrospective.
- [minor] No named contacts (procurement officers, buyer points of contact) anywhere, and the docs never acknowledge this as a competitive gap or price it in. — Tussell/Stotles-class products sell contacts as a core feature. The PII rails plausibly forbid it here (though published procurement-officer roles on tender notices are arguably professional, not personal, data — a solicitor question worth asking rather than assuming). Either way, positioning and pricing must account for the gap explicitly.
- [minor] Report shipping is blocked behind ~7 unsigned owner gates (badge wording, tier labels, SME definition, caveat-per-frame, chip decisions), and the templates are written as engineering specs — no mock/sample filled report exists to show a design partner. — The 'ship in weeks' claim is optimistic: you cannot recruit design partners with a template full of {{OWNER_BADGE_*}} tokens and PHASE-2-TODO stubs. The first sales artifact is a filled sample report, and none is planned.
- [minor] The ~6-month upstream award-publication lag is disclosed but its product implication is never confronted: a 'current' supplier dossier's award picture is half a year stale. — For market mapping this is tolerable; for bid intelligence it weakens the pitch. The live-tender (PLANNED) lane partially compensates, but the sales copy and pricing should be honest about which use cases the freshness supports.

**Recommended changes**
- [P0] Build the small set of Phase-2 views that ARE the sellable content before selling: top_suppliers_for_cpv, top_buyers_for_cpv (with stored share_pct), awards_for_cpv, a supplier-scoped per-publisher payments view, the buyer-name crosswalk (or wire fetch_competition_result), and the two year-trend views. Hard rule: do not take money for a category market map or buyer dossier until its landscape/competition sections render. — The category map's C4/C5 and the buyer report's Section 6 are the product; everything else is garnish. Selling stubbed reports to design partners burns the only 3–5 reference customers you plan to recruit.
- [P0] Add a mandatory 'Key facts' front page to each template: 5–7 facts selected and ordered by decision relevance (e.g. 'largest incumbent framework expires 2027-Q3', 'median award €X vs your typical contract size', 'buyer's single-bid rate Y% on N lots'). Facts only, denominators shown, no verdicts — selection and ordering are editorial, not inference. — This is the difference between a €5k report and a data dump. It is fully compatible with every rail: nothing is scored, summed, or inferred; facts are merely ranked by relevance to the buyer's stated question.
- [P0] Replace inline verbatim-caveat repetition with a one-page methodology annex carrying each canonical string once, plus short standardised inline chips ([AWARD ceiling], [TED 2024+], [coverage: see annex M2]) that reference it. Amend the templates' 'subtitle, not buried footnote' rule to 'chip inline, verbatim once in annex'. — Preserves the caveat-travels rail and the verbatim-never-softened rule while stopping the report from reading as 40% disclaimer. The current spec resolves the honesty/punch tension entirely against punch, unnecessarily.
- [P0] Produce one fully-filled sample report per template (real supplier, real buyer, real CPV) before approaching design partners, with the owner string-gates signed for at least the sample. — You cannot sell from a tokenised template; the sample is simultaneously the sales artifact, the owner-gate forcing function, and the first honest test of whether the assembled facts carry a so-what.
- [P1] Add comparative baselines as pipeline-owned view columns: buyer single-bid rate vs national median, CPV median award vs all-CPV median, supplier buyer-concentration vs category norm. Render as 'X% (national median: Y%)'. — A number without a baseline has no so-what. Comparatives are stored facts with denominators — rails-safe — and they are the cheapest large upgrade to decision-usefulness across all three templates.
- [P1] Move estimated end-date derivation (award_date + contract_duration_months, with an est-basis label) into a registered view so the SUPPLIER report gets a real renewal calendar, and ship renewal_cycle (historical re-tender cadence per buyer/category) as the rails-safe substitute for forecasting. — The re-tender window is the single most actionable output for both personas; today it works only in the buyer report. Historical cadence facts ('this buyer re-tendered 8 of 10 expiring frameworks within 6 months') deliver forward value without any win-probability claim.
- [P1] Relax the awarded-vs-paid 'no derived ratio, gap, or netting' rail to permit a coverage-bounded narrative comparison written by the analyst ('paid figures cover only N of M publishers; within covered publishers, payments recorded are €X against €Y of sum-safe awards'), with the coverage bound mandatory in the same sentence. — The customer will do the division anyway; doing it for them with the coverage bound attached is more honest than showing two numbers and refusing to speak. The rail should ban unbounded ratios, not bounded ones.
- [P2] Add an explicit competitive-positioning paragraph to the architecture doc: no named contacts, no win-probability, no forecasts — and what compensates (reconciliation, entity resolution, honesty). Get a solicitor answer on whether published procurement-officer contact details on tender notices are within the PII gate. — Design partners will benchmark against Tussell/Stotles in the first meeting; the pitch needs a rehearsed answer for the three features it deliberately lacks rather than discovering the objection live.
- [P2] State the freshness envelope per report type on the cover (awards ~6 months lagged; live tenders current) and scope the sales pitch to market-mapping and renewal-planning use cases, not tender-chasing. — Prevents the first credibility incident: a customer finding an award in their own records that the report missed because of the upstream lag.

**Blind spots**
- No filled sample/mock report exists or is planned — the templates spec plumbing meticulously and never test whether the assembled output actually reads as worth paying for.
- No competitive teardown of what a Tussell/Stotles/Spend Network report actually contains, so the templates were designed against internal rails rather than against the buyer's reference point.
- No customer-discovery evidence: no bid consultant or SME has apparently seen these section lists; the personas' so-what lines are the author's guesses, not validated questions.
- Delivery format and lifecycle are unspecified: static PDF vs interactive HTML, whether a bought report gets a refresh, snapshot-dating, and versioning are all absent — customers will ask in the first meeting.
- No plan for the inevitable in-meeting question 'so should we bid?' — the analyst needs a scripted, rails-compatible answer (facts + questions framing), or partners will experience the no-verdict discipline as evasiveness.
- The templates never address report length or design; at current caveat density a supplier dossier runs long, and there is no target page count, visual hierarchy, or 'one page per section' constraint.
- Pricing is anchored to hours-saved and UK comparables but never to the free explorer: the docs don't articulate section-by-section what the paid dossier shows that the free site doesn't, which is the first objection a prospect will raise.


### Adversarial pre-mortem — "kill this business" — verdict: **proceed-with-changes** (medium confidence)

**Headline:** The kill case bounds the idea rather than kills it — reports-first is a cheap, self-terminating experiment — but two owner overrides (selling political-access co-occurrence, and shipping Iris-derived distress before solicitor clearance) import unbounded reputational/legal downside for negligible revenue, and the plan currently builds five engineering primitives before it has a single piece of demand evidence.

**Single biggest risk:** Not demand (that risk is cheap and self-terminating via Stage B) but asymmetric reputational contamination: the two owner overrides — paid political-access co-occurrence and pre-clearance Iris — attach an unbounded, unrecoverable downside to the free civic product, which the strategy itself identifies as the only durable asset and the entire top-of-funnel, in exchange for revenue that no pricing tier actually depends on. The single piece of evidence that decides the whole venture: an unprompted, non-discounted repeat purchase by a design partner within two quarters — if it happens, the niche is real and the kill case reduces to scope discipline; if it doesn't, everything past Stage B should be abandoned and the grant route pursued.


**Strengths**
- Reports-first sequencing is genuinely the cheapest-to-fail path: no accounts, no GDPR lift, no scheduler — a failed Stage B costs weeks of analyst time, not a build.
- §11 of the architecture doc is unusually honest self-critique (free data, small TAM, free official competition, 7% coverage, key-person risk) — the author already knows where the bodies are buried.
- The Stage A/B plan is ground-truthed to real symbols and line numbers, with stale-claim corrections — rare execution rigor; the never-sum/no-inference rails are enforceable-by-construction, not aspirational.
- Not forking the data layer (one pipeline, two front doors) is the correct architecture and avoids the classic spinout maintenance death.

**Weaknesses**
- [critical] Selling lobbying-overlap + ministerial-diary co-occurrence in the PAID product is a reputational kill vector that endangers the free product — the actual asset — for revenue that is immaterial to every pricing tier. — The guardrails (company-subject, raw counts, verbatim caveats) are invisible in a headline. The first aggrieved TD, competitor, or journalist writes 'civic transparency site sells ministers’ meeting data to companies bidding for state contracts' — and the design-partner list literally includes a journalist desk. The doc's own §13 says the free civic product is the top-of-funnel and credibility engine; this feature poisons the funnel at its source. No tier's willingness-to-pay depends on it; the risk is gratuitous. The architecture doc itself recommended free-only and was overridden.
- [critical] Iris-derived corporate distress is IN the paid product before the §14.2 solicitor confirmation — the doc calls it 'the weak link & highest legal risk' and then ships it to paying customers pending review. — Government copyright, 'personal use only' terms. 'Facts-defensible' is a theory, not clearance. If the solicitor says no AFTER design-partner reports containing distress facts have been sold, you have delivered arguably-infringing material commercially — far worse posture than never having sold it. The 'confirm-before-scaling' framing gets the gate backwards: the exposure starts at the first sale, not at scale.
- [major] The TAM is small AND the alert layer is already served: eTenders/TED offer free CPV email alerts, and commercial trackers (e.g. BiP/Tracker, Tenders Direct, TED-alert resellers) already sell Irish tender alerting. The doc cites only UK comparables (Tussell, Stotles) — in a market ~15x larger — as pricing anchors, with zero Irish competitive scan. — The SME tier (€600–1,200/yr) competes head-on with free official alerts. The genuinely differentiated layer (awards↔payments cross-source intelligence) appeals to a subset measured in dozens of consultancies and large suppliers. Realistic mature ARR is plausibly €50–150k after years of solo sales effort — a profitable niche only if the founder's time were free, which it is not.
- [major] The product's own coverage honesty guts its flagship deliverables: payments visibility ~7% of state spend, payments↔award linkage ~35%, CPV fill ~33% (national live snapshot 0/2,363), CRO match 'no_match' on 3,523 of ~10k suppliers, TED winner names 0% pre-2024. — The centrepiece of all three report templates is the awards-vs-payments reconciliation — built on the sparsest joins in the corpus. The mid-tier product (category market map, €4–7k) keys on CPV, the weakest field. The chosen design-partner archetype 'mid-size supplier who knows its own history' is precisely the customer who will immediately spot missing awards and conclude the product is incomplete. Rigor → honest gaps → unimpressive report → no repurchase is a coherent, likely failure mode the docs never confront.
- [major] Solo-founder commercial obligations the plan cannot currently satisfy: the Stage B Definition-of-Done requires a 'second-analyst spot-check' (there is no second analyst); data freshness depends on a refresh that gov.ie WAF blocks from datacenter IPs (i.e. runs from a home connection); the owner is away and seven decision gates block every primitive; and the free product carries a large open backlog competing for the same hours. — A paid product converts best-effort freshness into an implicit SLA. One person maintaining a bespoke Windows pipeline, writing €1.5–10k reports by hand, doing sales, and keeping the free site alive is three jobs; the docs scope none of the founder-hours unit economics (hours per report vs price).
- [major] No demand evidence anywhere — no named prospects, no customer conversations, no letters of intent — yet Stage A commits to building five trust-UI primitives (weeks of engineering) before the first sales conversation. — This quietly inverts the reports-first logic. A first report can be sold and hand-QA'd with the primitives replaced by manual checks; the primitives only pay off if reports sell. Building them first is the comfortable engineering path, not the validation path — the classic solo-technical-founder failure mode.
- [minor] No kill criteria or timebox for Stage B; success signal ('partner pays again unprompted') has no deadline, no threshold, no full-price requirement, and no defined stop action. — Without a pre-committed exit, a lukewarm Stage B (one discounted repurchase, polite feedback) will drift into Stage C by momentum — the exact scenario where the small-TAM and SLA costs land without offsetting revenue.
- [minor] The pass-through ToS is contractually neat but practically unenforceable: the data is CC-BY/PSI, the sui generis compilation right is thin, and a single motivated customer or competitor can replicate the method. — Pricing power rests entirely on hours-saved and trust, which is defensible (Tussell proves it over free UK data) but means churn-on-replication is a permanent ceiling, not a temporary one — the docs acknowledge the free-data problem but not the customer-copies-the-method variant.
- [minor] Opportunity cost is never evaluated: grants/institutional funding (civic-tech and journalism funders, EU programmes) could plausibly yield the same €50–100k/yr as the realistic ARR ceiling, without SLA, GDPR, insurance, or reputational exposure. — The docs compare architectures for the spinout but never compare the spinout against not doing it. For a solo founder whose scarcest resource is attention, 'respected free tool + grant funding' is a live alternative that gets zero analysis.

**Recommended changes**
- [P0] Pull lobbying-overlap and ministerial-diary co-occurrence OUT of the paid scope for Stage B entirely (revert to the doc's original free-only recommendation); revisit only after repeat-purchase evidence exists and with a written comms/response plan for the inevitable 'selling access data' story. — Asymmetric bet: the feature is load-bearing for no tier's revenue, and its failure mode (contaminating the free product's trust) is the one outcome the whole strategy cannot recover from. Stage B loses nothing by dropping it.
- [P0] Flip the Iris gate from 'confirm-before-scaling' to 'confirm-before-first-sale': no paid deliverable contains Iris-derived facts until the solicitor signs off in writing. Templates should stub the distress section the same way they stub PHASE-2-TODO sections. — Legal exposure begins at the first commercial delivery, not at scale; shipping known-unlicensed-source material to paying customers converts a theoretical risk into an actual one for zero validation benefit.
- [P0] Sell before building: attempt to close 2–3 design partners with a manually-QA'd report (no Stage A primitives) within a fixed window (e.g. 6 weeks). If you cannot book ~€3–5k of committed spend, do not build the primitives. — The primitives are only valuable if reports sell; demand evidence is the scarce input and it currently stands at zero. Manual caveat/attribution QA per the existing checklist substitutes fine for 3 reports.
- [P1] Pre-commit kill criteria in the plan doc: e.g. 'if fewer than 2 of 5 partners repurchase at NON-discounted price within 2 quarters of report #1, park the spinout and evaluate the grant route with equal rigor.' Add a founder-hours unit-economics sheet (hours per report vs price) to Stage B. — Prevents momentum-driven drift into the expensive stages (C–E) on ambiguous signal, and makes the 'is this worth a solo founder's hours' question answerable with data instead of hope.
- [P1] Run an actual Irish/ROI competitive scan (BiP/Tracker ROI coverage, Tenders Direct, TED alert resellers, local bid-consultancy in-house tooling) before pricing is quoted to any partner; stop anchoring on UK-only comparables. — The SME-alert tier may be dead-on-arrival against incumbents and free official alerts; better to discover that before positioning, and to concentrate the pitch on the genuinely unserved cross-source layer.
- [P1] Replace the impossible 'second-analyst spot-check' DoD gate with something a solo shop can actually satisfy (scripted reproduction of every headline figure from the query layer, diffed automatically), and address the freshness reality (WAF-bound, home-network refresh) in writing before any data-currency promise appears in a report or engagement letter. — A quality gate that cannot be executed will be silently skipped — worse than a weaker gate that is always run. Freshness promises a solo pipeline can't keep are the fastest route to a refund demand.
- [P2] Before Stage C, evaluate the grant/institutional-funding alternative in a one-page memo with the same rigor as §7's option table, and obtain professional-indemnity insurance plus a liability cap in the report engagement letter. — Reports customers use to inform bid decisions are a claim vector; and the opportunity-cost comparison is the single analysis the strategy docs omit entirely.

**Blind spots**
- Zero customer-discovery evidence: no named prospects, no interviews, no letters of intent — the plan's demand model is entirely hypothetical yet drives an engineering commitment (Stage A) first.
- No Irish competitive landscape analysis: existing ROI-covering tender-alert services and free official eTenders/TED alerts are never enumerated; only UK firms in a ~15x larger market are cited, and only as pricing anchors.
- Founder-hours unit economics absent: no estimate of hours per report, no accounting for the free product's maintenance backlog competing for the same time, and a DoD gate (second-analyst check) that a solo operation cannot execute.
- Opportunity cost never analysed: grants/philanthropic/EU funding for civic transparency work is a live alternative with a comparable revenue ceiling and none of the SLA/GDPR/reputational cost — it gets zero pages.
- Operational freshness reality unaddressed: the procurement refresh is blocked from datacenter IPs by the gov.ie WAF (per project memory), meaning commercial data currency depends on a home-connection scrape — incompatible with any implied SLA.
- Professional-indemnity/liability exposure of selling reports that inform bid decisions is not mentioned anywhere (a wrong figure in a €10k report is a claim vector).
- Legal-entity structure of the spinout (separate company vs sole trader, and how that interacts with the AGPL copyright holding and the free brand) is undiscussed.
- Source-revocation risk: no contingency for eTenders/OGP corpus terms changing or a publisher withdrawing payments data once it is being visibly commercialised — commercial reuse of PSI has historically triggered exactly this reaction from publishers.
- Conflict-of-interest optics of paying customers (suppliers, lobbyist-adjacent consultancies) appearing as subjects in the free site's accountability coverage — no editorial-independence policy exists.
- No comms/incident plan for the predictable 'transparency site monetises access data' story — the ethics section designs guardrails but never a public defence.
