Chapter 1: Background

1.1 Policy context

When introducing the bill that would become the Housing (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2014 ("the
2014 Act"), then Minister of State for Housing and Planning, Jan O'Sullivan, set out the rationale and
aims of HAP as follows:

          "We need a system of housing supports that is more effective, more efficient, more
          coherent and that better serves the needs of people. It has long been accepted that one
          of our key State housing supports, the rent supplement system, was ill-used and was
          becoming a much more long-term support than was ever intended. [...] The HAP is an
          opportunity to improve standards and levels of compliance, to remove employment traps,
          and to create a more equal and fair social housing system where the rights and
          opportunities available to tenants in different forms of social housing are vastly improved.
          [...] I am confident that it will improve the ability of housing authorities to plan and deliver
          their services in a coherent, flexible and responsive manner by bringing all long-term
          social housing services provided by the State together under the local authority system".1

Rent supplement is administered by the Department of Social Protection (DSP). It was designed as a
short-term support for private tenants who are struggling to meet their rent due to, for example,
temporary unemployment or reduced working hours. One of its conditions is that no one in the
household can be working more than 30 hours per week. The economic downturn, which started in
late 2007, led to a "surge in claims" for rent supplement and this surge continued, leading to
"concerns about the effect of financial disincentives to work created by its cliff-edge design".2 In
contrast, a person in receipt of HAP can be in full-time employment.

HAP was launched as a pilot scheme in three local authorities3 in September 2014 and gradually
implemented in others. The Programme for a Partnership Government published in 2016 reaffirmed
the role of HAP in "meet[ing] housing need" and undertook to "extend [its] roll-out by local
authorities".4 In line with this, that Government's action plan for housing and homelessness,
Rebuilding Ireland 2016-2021, committed to an "accelerated roll-out" of HAP to all local authorities
by early 2017. It also increased targets for the number of HAP tenancies to be established each year:
"HAP is a key delivery mechanism for the provision of social housing support under Rebuilding
Ireland, and aims to support just under 84,000 households over the lifetime of the plan (2016-
2021)"5, equating to "85% of new social housing tenancies".6

The programme for government "Our Shared Future," published in 2020, includes a commitment to
"[r]educe our reliance on the use of HAP for new social housing solutions, as the supply of social and

1.2 Legislative basis

The 2014 Act established HAP and provides the statutory basis for its operation. Section 35 defines
"housing assistance" as "the payment by a housing authority of rent for a dwelling to a landlord on
behalf of a qualified household", i.e. a household9 assessed as having a social housing need.10 This
means that before a household can make an application for HAP it must have first been considered
eligible for social housing support and added to the local authority's social housing list. A household
assessed as eligible for social housing is immediately eligible for HAP.

Section 39 of the 2014 Act states that a housing authority may provide HAP subject to the satisfying
of various requirements, including that the household "source the dwelling" and pay a "rent
contribution" to the housing authority; that the dwelling and landlord meet certain conditions set
out in the Act; and that the tenancy be "in good faith". The 2014 Act also amended existing social
housing legislation to add HAP to the range of social housing supports.11 As noted earlier, a
household availing of HAP is considered to have had its social housing need met and can be removed
from the standard social housing waiting list and related statistics.

    1.3. Oversight and coordination

The HAP scheme is implemented by local authorities, in their capacity as housing authorities, with
the responsibility for the delivery of social housing support. Typically, each local authority's housing
department has a HAP section. These sections are responsible for processing HAP applications and
acting as the primary point of contact for both tenants and landlords in the HAP scheme. The local
authorities are supported by the HAP Shared Services Centre (SSC), which handles the ongoing
financial operation of the scheme. This includes processing HAP transfers to landlords' accounts,
collecting rent contributions from HAP-supported tenants, and managing any rent contribution
arrears due to local authorities.

There are a number of other aspects to the operation of HAP, which are handled by other bodies

Chapter 2: Operation of HAP

2.1 Local authorities

Local authorities, in their function as housing authorities, have primary responsibility for
implementing the HAP scheme and are the point of contact for applicants. Once a HAP tenancy has
been established, the ongoing operational aspects (i.e., collection of differential rent and payment of
HAP) are handled by the SSC. Its role will be outlined in greater detail later in this chapter.

Before being approved and set up for HAP in respect of a particular property, a household must be
qualified for social housing in accordance with section 20 of the Housing (Miscellaneous Provisions)
Act 2009.18 Both the social housing assessment and subsequent processing of HAP applications are
carried out by local authorities' housing departments and in fact they cover much of the same
ground. Typically, however, the two functions are treated distinctly, with HAP applications handled
primarily by a HAP-specific section within the local authority. When we surveyed local authorities for
this investigation, the average number of staff working in HAP-specific sections was just over four
whole-time equivalents. Later discussions with local authorities revealed that staff working on HAP
often straddle other functions (such as the Rental Accommodation Scheme, RAS), and many sections
were carrying vacancies. Furthermore, in a number of cases, management-level staff had oversight
of multiple housing programmes.

A common theme that emerged during our engagement with local authorities was that the high
turnover of staff in HAP sections means that staff are often self-taught, developing the necessary
information and skills while on the job. According to one local authority staff member, formal,
centralised training was only ever provided when HAP was first rolled out. Since then, all of the
original members on the HAP team who received that training had moved on. Other HAP teams said
that the pace of staff turnover can mean that incoming staff do not have the opportunity to get
handover training from the outgoing person who is leaving the role. A number of HAP staff who
engaged with us expressed an interest in more structured and frequent training, be it online or in
person.

Staff in several local authorities commended the SSC for its online seminars and material and
ongoing ad hoc assistance in guiding staff through various processes and aspects of the system. They
said that SSC staff are very helpful and responsive to queries. However, some staff pointed out that
training on the HAP scheme itself should not be the responsibility of the SSC and expressed a wish
for similar training to be provided locally in each authority. Furthermore, the SSC handles HAP
transactions, but it is for local authorities to make substantive decisions on matters such as eligibility
for HAP, the correct HAP cap to be applied, whether to apply a discretionary increase to the rate
payable, etc.

Implementation of these aspects of the scheme is guided by circulars issued by DHLGH. Our
investigation has shown several differences of interpretation and application of circulars between
local authorities and, in some cases, significant deviations from the guidelines set out. These issues
are discussed in detail in later chapters but, in particular, includes less common aspects of the
scheme such as inter-authority HAP (where a household approved for social housing by one local
authority rents a property in the geographical area of a different local authority) and `section 43'

Finding:
Initially, DHLGH issued circulars to provide guidance on the implementation of HAP. Changes to the
scheme as it has evolved and grown continue to be guided by new circulars on issues such as inter-
authority HAP or discretionary increases to the HAP caps. While these circulars are issued by DHLGH
with the intention of standardising processes across the country, ultimately local authorities
interpret such guidelines or policies differently resulting in wide variations in the implementation of
HAP between authority areas. In some cases, local authorities appear to have deviated significantly
from circulars. There is no mechanism to ensure that local authorities are adhering to and
implementing circulars as intended.

Recommendation:
Although they lack the force of statute and regulations, circulars set out the manner in which the
relevant Department has determined legislation should be interpreted and implemented. Local
authorities must not deviate from the provisions of circulars, either intentionally or through
misinterpretation. DHLGH should engage with local authorities regularly to ensure that the scheme
is being administered as intended and in a fair and consistent manner. Given ongoing revisions to
the scheme, DHLGH should consolidate existing circulars into one comprehensive, living document,
to be updated on an ongoing basis and hosted on the existing Housing Manual website.

 Finding:
 Administrative issues, such as the ones identified across this report, arise from time to time which
 cause delays in processing applications and payments.

 Recommendation: Due to the number of administrative inefficiencies identified in this
 investigation, DHLGH should establish a HAP administration review group as a sub-group of the
 HAP Oversight Group, in order to identify practical solutions to administrative issues as they arise.
 The Ombudsman acknowledges that a HAP Processes Committee is already in place and suggests
 that this Committee is given the responsibility of reviewing and addressing the administrative
 issues highlighted in this report.

The role of the HAP Practitioners' Forum in terms of knowledge-sharing and consistency across local
authorities was outlined in the previous chapter. HAP staff often told us during this investigation
that, although aware of the HAP Practitioners' Forum, they were unclear about its structure, format
and purpose. We found that the distinction between the HAP Practitioners' Forum and Committee
was largely unknown. Several HAP staff confirmed to us that they did not know who the members of
the HAP Practitioners' Committee are or how they were selected. Nonetheless, the general opinion
was that the HAP Practitioners' Forum has a very important role to play in helping those working in

2.2 HAP Shared Services Centre

The delivery of HAP by local authorities is supported by the SSC which handles the financial
transactions involved in HAP on behalf of all local authorities.

The SSC is funded by the DHLGH and operates as a directorate of Limerick City and County Council. It
has a staff of approximately 70 and its operations are overseen by a HAP Governance Group chaired
by the Chief Executive of the Council.19 The SSC has no role in determining a household's eligibility
for HAP, the rent contribution to be paid by that household, or the application of a discretionary
increase to the HAP cap, which are all functions of the housing authorities.

HAP payments to landlords are managed centrally by the SSC. This service is provided out of Limerick
City and County Council. The role of the SSC is:

    · Set up and commencement of HAP tenancy.
    · Payment of monthly landlord rent.
    · Tax clearance checking.
    · Collection of tenant rent & arrears management.
    · Suspend or cease payments to landlord.
    · Monitoring, reviewing and maintaining HAP tenancies with interaction from local

         authorities.

    · Recoupment from the DHLGH including Pre-HAP costs.

Structurally, the SSC has two main Units ­ Operations Team and Debt Management Team, along
with a smaller, Finance and Compliance Team. The SSC manages the HAP software and database,
"Sugar", which hosts all of the information regarding the tenancies inputted by local authorities. The
SSC provide some training to local authority staff, and there seems to be a general acceptance this is
very useful and informative. However, interviewed staff indicated that training sessions and
refreshers could be provided as there is a constant turnover of staff across the authorities.

Once the local authority is satisfied that an application is valid, they can then proceed to enter the
information onto Sugar. In their discussions with this Office, local authorities highlighted a number
of concerns regarding the processes they currently employ. Primarily, these concerns relate to the
amendments process, which we will explore further now, and the debt management process, which
will be discussed later in this report.20

Local authorities cannot enter data onto the onto Sugar until it has been confirmed that all
necessary information is available (i.e. it cannot be partially entered when received). In our survey of
local authorities, they estimated that on average it took just over 2 weeks from receipt of application
to validation of information, and a further 2.4 weeks to have everything entered onto Sugar.

The SSC must validate the information provided by the local authority prior to initiating payments. It
informed this Office that, if needs be, it can send applications back and question a local authority's
directions21. The SSC has points of contact who liaise with the various local authorities around the
country, and generally, the sense from the local authority staff is that they are helpful and
responsive. Once the application is verified by the SSC, the tenancy is activated and payments begin.

2.2.1 Amendments
From the perspective of both the local authorities and the SSC, a significant portion of time appears
to be spent processing amendments. Even though the local authority created the original file on
Sugar, they do not have the permission or ability to make amendments to the data fields relating to
the tenancy. They must create the amendment, before having the amendment approved by the SSC.
This was highlighted by local authorities as one of the most significant administrative burdens for
their teams in relation to HAP.

Detailed process maps have been created, providing instructions for local authorities, and for staff of
the SSC, as to how to create and process each type of amendment. For example, if the local
authority makes and original entry of a telephone number on Sugar, they do not have the
permission to make a future change to that field themselves. The SSC provided information to this
Office that they processed 5,821 amendments in just one month, back in October 2022. These
amendments would have been initiated by the local authority first. Where an authority creates an
amendment, it must submit it to the SSC for approval. The SSC must then go through its own process
in order to approve or reject this change. So the simple change of telephone number, that has been
signalled by the tenant, must be processed by at least two people before it can be finalised, and this
may take a number of days.

Finding:
Currently, local authorities must submit any amendments to HAP records or applications for
approval by the SSC before the amendment can be made. The process is overly complex, causes a
disproportionate administrative burden for local authorities, and can lead to significant delays in
setting up or modifying HAP tenancies.
In a single sample month taken during this investigation, the SSC processed 5,821 amendments. It is
not clear what additional safeguard against fraud or error that the current amendment approval
process provides. The amendment process was one of the key administrative issues identified by
local authorities during our engagement.

Local authority staff should be able to edit/amend the details of HAP tenancies as needed, without

changes made and to provide necessary oversight.
2.2.2 Data accuracy
The SSC has the ability to create reports and make them available to local authorities. These reports
can have a wide variety of uses. Having this data centrally is excellent for analysis and policy making,
and there is no centralised source of data relating to other forms of social housing. The SSC shared
an anonymised report with this Office which provided a number of insights referred to in this
investigation.
In interrogating the data, it became apparent that there were some errors. While the number of
errors appeared low in comparison to the size of the dataset, it was evident that the errors identified
were in fact quite obvious. For example, of the 58,313 rows of data relating to active tenancies, 96
were recorded as having zero weekly income and 160 rows in total had less than 100 weekly
income. This would seem unlikely given the rates of social welfare payments. Other examples of data
errors relate to the amount of rent being paid. In some cases, where a household is in shared
accommodation, it appears that the full rent of the whole property is recorded, rather than the HAP
tenant's contribution. Inaccurate data, of any kind, will skew analysis which could have a detrimental
impact upon any resulting policies.
While data is held centrally by the SSC, data accuracy is the responsibility of the local authorities.
Regular interrogation of the data locally would help ensure their service users' data is kept as
accurately as possible and to identify if there are training requirements for staff members.
Generally, however, the data is reliable and useful. For example, the following graphic shows that
the vast number of households are in receipt of 0%, 20%, 35% or 50% discretionary increases, as
would be expected in line with the amounts referred to in various circulars.

2.3 Reviews and appeals

Since the introduction of this Office forty years ago, the right of individuals to review or appeal
decisions in respect of their public services has slowly begun to evolve. Over the years, as new

                                                                                                                        12
schemes have been introduced, review and appeals processes have also become more common
place.

In the case of HAP, Section 48 of the 2014 Act provides for a review process to be put in place under
Part 4 relating to "Housing Assistance". This section provides that an applicant can request the
housing authority to carry out a review of a decision under the Act i.e. an internal review within the
same housing authority. However, this section has not been commenced. When queried as to why,
the DHLGH said:

         "Appeals are currently being dealt with under a local authority's Customer Charter. Each
         local authority has a customer service charter and complaints procedure for applicants who
         may wish to make a complaint regarding the service they receive. Following that complaint,
         if they consider that they have been unfairly treated or are not satisfied with the Council's
         decision on their complaint, it is open to them to make a complaint to the Ombudsman."

In further discussions with DHLGH, we were advised that "there has not been demand from local
authorities to commence this section. Currently, HAP decisions may be appealed through local
authorities themselves with the option of taking the matter to the Ombudsman if not satisfied. This
approach has been working. "

Despite this, there are many decision points in the HAP process, that could result in a genuine need
for a review or an appeal, including decisions around:

    · A "good faith" tenancy;
    · date of validation;
    · differential rent;
    · revisions of differential rent;
    · section 43;

From the evidence we gathered, in many cases, the tenant is informed of the outcome without

3.2 Application form

Until recently, the HAP application was on paper but a new online system has been rolled out across
all local authorities as of March 2024.

The HAP application (paper or online) has always been divided into two parts: Part A which is
completed by the applicant, and Part B which is completed by the landlord. In Part A, the applicant
submits details such as household members, income, any relationship to the landlord and a
declaration to confirm the details are correct. Documentation, such as payslips, legal custody
documentation etc. are provided as proof. With the online system, any relevant documentation is
uploaded.

In Part B, the landlord submits their details, including bank details and details of the property, proof
of ownership, proof of tax compliance, compliance with RPZ rules and any other documentation that
might be required depending on the circumstances (for example, a declaration from a co-owner or
legal documentation relating to any probate issues).

Regarding the online process, there are some additional points to note:

    · The new system can be accessed either by registering with the HAP website or by logging in
         with a MyGovID account.

    · It has two-factor identification for security purposes.
    · Data is primarily entered by the applicant, reducing the amount of data entry for local

         authorities. However, the online system is not a linked to other local authority IT systems
         such as `iHouse'.
    · The online application is not linked to the HAP app, through which tenants can make
         payments on their account, nor does it create an "account" which can be updated by the
         tenant (e.g. with a new phone number or email address).

The online application system was rolled out to the whole country. As the system is in its infancy,
issues are still being resolved. The system is not currently compliant with EU accessibility standards,
however, it is understood that the SSC is currently working on this matter. A review of the online
system should be undertaken after a set period to evaluate its effectiveness and identify/resolve any
other ongoing issues. It is noted that paper based forms are still available for those who wish to use
them.

Although moving public services online is generally accepted as more efficient, we are mindful of a
number of issues specific to HAP applications, regardless of whether the application is online or not,
which will be discussed in the following sections.

When carrying out a social housing assessment, a housing authority requests information and
supporting documents from the applying household concerning its composition (number of
dependents, etc.) and income. Local authorities require qualified households to resubmit much of
this material when applying for HAP. However, under the relevant regulations a qualified household
seeking HAP is only required to provide information about the dwelling and the landlord.23

3.4 Proof of ownership

As noted above, landlords are required to submit proof of ownership of the property with part B of
the application form. This is not, however, a requirement under relevant legislation. The 2014 Act
requires landlords to submit a tax clearance certificate, but that Act does not impose any pre-
condition in relation to ownership of the property. Documents accepted as proof of ownership
include proof of payment of local property tax (LPT) for the property in question; proof of
registration of the tenancy with the RTB; the Land Registry folio; or a mortgage statement or
insurance policy in respect of the property.

NGO's highlighted delays in the processing of applications leading to the potential loss of tenancies,
or requiring the tenant to pay the full cost of the rent awaiting HAP approval.

All of the local authorities interviewed for this investigation brought up proof of ownership as an
issue that leads to delays in processing applications. Reasons cited for delays include:

    · The SSC sometimes refuses a proof that would have been accepted a couple of months
         earlier, which may be due to new/different staff.

    · The SSC can sometimes be over particular in its requirements.
    · Insurance renewal notices are not accepted unless there is a receipt.

The Irish Property Owners' Association (IPOA) flagged inconsistencies in relation to what proofs are
acceptable. For instance, a proof that might have been accepted for one HAP application is rejected
on another occasion, or a landlord is told at one stage that a proof will be accepted only to find on
submission that is rejected. There were also instances of landlord being told that a particular
document is necessary but on submission they were informed that it was not required.

One recurring issue is that proof of having paid local property tax (LPT) is listed as an acceptable
proof of ownership. However, the emailed LPT receipt, which many landlords submit as proof of
payment, is not accepted.

                                                                                                                        18
Local authorities expressed the view that proof of registration with the RTB is one of the most
convenient. However, proof of ownership of the property in question is not required to register a
tenancy with the RTB. As such, it is not reliable as proof of ownership.

It appears that the requirement for proof of ownership was devised to safeguard against HAP being
transferred to parties that are not entitled to receive it. However, we are of the view that the
current requirements are in some cases excessively onerous, and in other cases are ineffective, for
example in the case of RTB registration. Delays in relation to proof of ownership create an
unnecessary administrative burden on local authorities as well as serious risks of tenancies being lost
by applicants.

One option to address this issue would be to streamline the proofs of ownership that are accepted,
i.e. give fewer options. However, not all local authorities agreed that fewer proofs would be a good
idea, as the wide range currently accepted means that landlords should be able to provide a suitable
document readily.

Finding:
Our investigation revealed that there can be unnecessary delays verifying ownership of the property
when a landlord submits Part B of the HAP application form to the local authority. A number of
documents are accepted as proof of ownership, but there can be delays and often lengthy back and
forth correspondence if the document submitted does not meet the requirements of SSC. This can
result in delays to the commencement of tenancies, increased risk of inability to secure properties,
and delays to the payment of HAP itself. This was a prominent issue identified by local authorities we
visited. It was also raised by landlords and NGOs working on housing issues.

The 2014 Act requires landlords to submit a tax clearance certificate, but that Act does not impose
any pre-condition in relation to ownership of the property. However, the published guidance states
that proof of ownership of the property will be required and Part B of the application form includes
a declaration of ownership. It appears that the requirement of proof of ownership is intended to
mitigate the risk that payment will be made to a person who is not entitled to receive it. The
likelihood of this risk occurring is not clear and therefore, it is not apparent that the requirement of
proof of ownership is a proportionate response to this risk.

Recommendation:
The requirement to submit proof of ownership should be reviewed by the DHLGH. In addition to
considering whether the requirement is a necessary and proportionate measure, the review should
establish the difficulties caused to landlords, tenants and local authorities and propose measures to
address these difficulties.

3.5 Good faith tenancies

One of the issues raised regularly with this Office relates to section 39(2)(e) of the 2014 Act, which
requires that, in order to provide HAP, a local authority "shall be satisfied that the tenancy
concerned is or would be a tenancy in good faith". The HAP website provides the following guidance
about how a "good faith" tenancy should be confirmed:

         The applicant HAP tenant must have a tenancy which is agreed in good faith for
         the property the HAP payments will be made for. Where there is a family
         relationship between the tenant and the landlord, the local authority may seek
         evidence of a previous landlord and tenant relationship between the landlord
         and the HAP applicant. This evidence may include:

                                                                                                                        19
              · proof of rent payments
              · a lease or tenancy agreement for the property
              · registration of the tenancy with the Residential Tenancies Board (RTB)
              · It is for the applicant and landlord to prove that what is being proposed

                   is a bona fide tenancy. HAP cannot be paid where the tenant is seeking
                   to rent within the family home. 24

The Minister for Housing has responded to Parliamentary questions in a similar fashion (See
Appendix 1).

Complaints made to this Office demonstrate that, in practice, the requirements can vary from local
authority to local authority. In particular, we found that undue weight is often placed on the
existence of a family relationship. We are aware of several cases where a landlord placing a property
on the rental market offered the tenancy on a legitimate rental basis to a relative, but the local
authority would not approve it as a HAP tenancy because of the family relationship.

Case study
Background
Mark had been in receipt of HAP for five years. He applied to transfer his HAP to a new property in
May 2022 but his application was returned. The local authority required additional information to
prove that the tenancy was in good faith, because the property was owned by his sister and brother-
in-law. The following evidence was required where a property was owned by a relative of a tenant:

1) Proof of 12 months' consistent rental payments from the tenant's bank account into the
         landlord's account.

2) Copies of the tenancy agreements of current and previous tenant(s), where applicable.

3) Proof of registration of current and previous tenancies with the RTB, where applicable.

The local authority advised that Mark must pay the first 12 months' rent in full to his new landlords
(his sister and brother-in-law) in order to prove this was a tenancy in good faith, and reapply for HAP
after that.

Ombudsman's investigation
The local authority said these requirements applied to each HAP application where there is a
declared family relationship between tenant and landlord. The local authority confirmed that these
requirements would not apply if Mark found a property with a different landlord who was not a
relative, or if a HAP tenant unrelated to his sister and brother-in-law were to rent the property in
question. The refusal centred solely on the familial relationship between Mark and his prospective
landlords.

The Ombudsman noted that Mark had been availing of HAP for a number of years and remained
eligible for the payment, and that there was no way to provide proof of 12 months' rent payments,
as the property had not been rented out previously.

Outcome
The guidance on "good faith" tenancies leaves scope for what evidence may be required, as each
application should be considered on a case-by-case basis and a level of flexibility is required. A lack
of proof of previous rent does not mean that the new tenancy was not in good faith; simply that it

was impossible to meet the specific requirements of that local authority. The Ombudsman
recommended that the local authority reconsider Mark's application, given that there was a
reasonable explanation as to why proof of 12 months' rental payments could not be provided.

Other local authorities are not as restrictive as in the case above. For example, one local authority
requires the following:

    · If it is an existing tenancy, evidence of rent currently being paid;
    · copy of a tenancy agreement;
    · proof of registration of the tenancy with the RTB.

Another considers the tenancy genuine if any of the following apply:

    · The landlord and tenant have a 12-month rental history, or rent supplement has been
         awarded previously for the tenancy.

    · The landlord has let the property for 12 months to a different tenant and can provide
         evidence.

    · The tenant has been residing there but the property has changed ownership to new owner
         to whom the tenant is related.

We outlined the details of Mark's case for three local authorities, to gauge whether they would
consider it a "good faith" tenancy. All three said that in the circumstances described, they would
have allowed the tenancy. In our view, this, along with the fact that there are different criteria
across local authorities, indicates that there is variation among local authorities regarding what is
considered a "good faith" tenancy. This leaves space for inequity across the country, potentially
preventing genuine tenants in certain areas from accessing properties in an already difficult market.
In our view, the overriding consideration should be whether a tenancy has been established simply
to avail of a state payment, in circumstances where there would otherwise be no landlord-tenant
rental relationship. Examples seen by this Office include:

    · A person was living in a property owned by a family member for a considerable time without
         paying any rent. The person then applied for social housing, was approved, and applied for
         HAP for the house where he was already living. The local authority deemed this not to be "in
         good faith", as the property-owner would not have sought any rent from the person if HAP
         was not available.

    · In another case, a person had been living in a property owned by a family member for
         several years without paying rent. The person applied for social housing and was approved.
         The same relative built a new property for this person to move to, and she applied for HAP
         to rent it. The local authority was of the view that this was not a tenancy "in good faith" as
         the evidence was that the property-owner had never taken rent from that person before she
         was eligible for HAP.

                                                                                                                        21
Finding:

Under the legislation governing HAP, a local authority must be satisfied that the tenancy concerned
is or would be in good faith ­ meaning it is or would be a genuine tenancy with rent payable
regardless of the HAP scheme. The HAP website and HAP application form list several pieces of
evidence that local authorities may consider when determining whether a tenancy is genuine, but
local authorities ultimately have broad discretion in this regard. Our investigation has found that the
way local authorities apply the guidance on supporting documents, does not always account for
each case being different and nuanced, with the result that some genuine tenancies are unable to
commence with HAP. In some cases, local authorities place undue weight on family relationships
even where all other evidence suggests that the tenancy is legitimate. This has resulted in situations
where HAP has been refused where all evidence suggests a tenancy is legitimate, and others where
HAP has been approved where it should not have been.

Recommendation:

DHLGH should issue guidance clarifying that the purpose of the good faith aspect of the legislation is
to identify applications where the only purpose of setting up a tenancy is to avail of HAP. Guidance
should permit local authorities to look at each tenancy on its own merits, guided by proofs that are
relevant and attainable. The aim should be to strike the right balance between ensuring legitimate
tenancies can proceed (including those where there is a family relationship), while at the same time
excluding tenancies established solely to take advantage of a substantial State payment.

3.6 Validation date and processing times

Although a person is eligible for HAP once their social housing support application has been
approved, HAP is only payable in respect of a particular tenancy from the "validation date" of the
application for HAP for that specific property.

The "validation date" is not the date a person submits the application, or the date that application is
approved. The validation date is the date that the local authority is satisfied that HAP is payable,
once the application is subsequently approved. The SSC told our Office that it understood that all
local authorities validate applications in the same manner, taking the validation date as the date that
all information, including both parts of the application and all supporting documents, is correctly
completed and submitted to the local authority.

For example, if a tenant submits Part A on 1 January, but the landlord does not submit Part B until 15
January, the validation date will be 15 January. If Part B is not deemed to be complete due to a
rejected proof of ownership, the validation date will be pushed further out. If the local authority is
not satisfied that the tenant has submitted a complete Part A, the validation date will be delayed.

The significance of the validation date is that, once a HAP application has been approved, the local
authority will only backdate HAP to either a) the validation date or b) the date up to which the
tenant covered the whole rent, if they were paying rent to the landlord while waiting for their HAP
application to be processed. An applicant who is already in a tenancy (either long-term, or recently
entered in anticipation of being approved for HAP) must therefore cover the whole rent up to the
validation date, even if delays in validating the application are due to the landlord's actions.

Our investigation indicates that what counts as a validation date varies from local authority to local
authority. Although many do not validate the application until both parts and all documentation has
been received and found acceptable for processing, others will validate an application as soon as
either part has been received, and regardless of whether the supporting documentation is all

                                                                                                                        22
present and correct yet. This means that when HAP is eventually approved, it can be backdated to
the earliest possible date and any delays with the supporting paperwork do not adversely affect the
applicant. Based on our investigation, we are of the view that backdating HAP to a point as close as
possible to the point of eligibility is the fairer approach.

Given that eligibility for HAP is established when a social housing support application is approved, in
our view there is no rationale, why it should be backdated only to the date that all supporting
documents have been submitted, or even to the date of application for HAP. Although certain
information about the landlord, property and tenancy must be verified, the entitlement to HAP
already exists. Once the local authority is satisfied that all of the documentation is in order, HAP
should be backdated to the start of the tenancy (where that post-dates eligibility for social
housing/HAP). Where there is a pre-existing, longer term rental during which the household has also
been eligible for social housing support, it would seem reasonable to limit this to three months'
back-dating. It must also be noted that once HAP is approved, the tenant has to pay the differential
rent accrued and may find themselves in danger of entering the arrears process through no fault of
their own. Such arrears, whether arising from backdated HAP or general delays, should not be
recouped via a lump sum or any other avenue that might place undue hardship upon the applicant.

The scenario of a tenant paying the full rent beyond the validation date, while awaiting HAP, is a
separate issue, which can arise because HAP is paid in arrears. Landlords who wait for HAP to come
through have expressed frustration with delays in processing and approval because, although the full
amount is eventually paid, there can be a significant period with no rent coming in.25

Processing/approving applications after validation can take significant time. Our survey of local
authorities found that it takes on average 2.2 weeks from initial receipt of an application to
validation, and a further 2.4 weeks before it is processed on Sugar. However, our survey also showed
that there can be delays of up to 10 weeks. The online system may reduce these timelines given that
it removes a significant data-entry burden from local authorities.

NGOs and members of the public contacting our office have highlighted cases where a landlord was
not satisfied to wait until a HAP application was processed and approved, and tenants have come
under pressure to pay the full rent upfront. Given the lead-in times and payment in arrears, the
landlord's perspective is understandable, particularly given that there is no guarantee that HAP will
eventually be approved. For many tenants, however, this means having to borrow money to cover
the rent and sustain the tenancy, or can even lead to the loss of a tenancy.

The SSC can only backdate payments to landlords and not to tenants, as there is no mechanism to
process payments to tenants. This means that where a landlord insists that a tenant pay the full rent
in advance, the tenant will not be able to recoup the funds from the SSC even if they are entitled to
the payment during the period. In effect, tenants who are pressed to pay the rent in full while
waiting for HAP approval are penalised for having done so and can be left significantly out of pocket.
One local authority told us that, as a way around this, it will authorise a payment to the landlord
with the understanding that it will be refunded to the tenant. In our view, HAP tenants should not be
left out of pocket due to processing times and it is not adequate to have to rely on the goodwill of
the landlord to reimburse the tenant. In a small number of cases, local authorities have applied a
credit to the tenants' differential rent account. A means of making payment to tenants should be
incorporated into the HAP financial/IT system.

Case study
Background
Maria submitted a HAP application form to a council on 17 October 2019 and moved into the
property on 1 November 2019. Maria had to pay rent from the date she moved in to the property.
Maria was notified on 22 November 2019 that her application had been found valid 2 days earlier.
On 20 December 2019, the application was assigned to a member of staff to process. During
processing, there were a number of issues which created further delays and the application was
finally approved on 13 January 2020. Awaiting approval, Maria had paid rent up to 31 December.
The HAP payment was backdated to the 31 December, not to the date she moved into the property.

Ombudsman's Investigation
The investigation of this case found that through no fault of her own, Maria's application was not
fully processed until the 13 January 2020. This resulted in Maria having to pay the rent in full
between the 17 October to the 31 December. While the SSC stated that it would agree to backdate
payments to landlords where no rent had been paid, they will not process a backdated payment
where the tenant has paid the landlord upfront.

Outcome
It was this Office's view that the application should have been deemed valid from 17 October 2019,
as the delay was not her fault. Following discussions, the local authority agreed to work with the SSC
to find a way to refund Maria.

Finding:
Delays in the validation of HAP applications by local authorities are having significant impacts on
prospective tenants. The validation date of HAP applications is important because it is the date
which dictates from when payment of HAP can commence. During the processing period for a HAP
application, a tenant may have to pay rent to the landlord in order to retain the property. It follows
that when an application is validated, the local authority in question may only pay HAP from that
date onwards. This does not account for any rent paid to the landlord prior to the awarding of HAP.
In short, a tenant could have to pay a number of months' rent unassisted due to the administrative
delays by the local authority. This is unfair as, at this point in the application process, the tenant has
already been deemed eligible for social housing supports.
Recommendation:
Once a HAP application is approved, a person's entitlement to HAP should be considered to
commence, or have commenced, from the date that the household is found eligible for social
housing support. Where a household has been approved for social housing support and has yet to
find a property, the validation date should be the starting date of their tenancy, and payments
should be backdated to that point. Where a `back-payment' to the tenant is required, a payment
covering a maximum of three months should be made directly to the tenant or a credit should be
applied to their differential rent account.

3.7 Inter-authority HAP

Typically, a household receiving a social housing support is housed in the area overseen by the local
authority that made the housing assessment. For instance, someone who wishes to be allocated
social housing in Offaly must apply to and be assessed by Offaly County Council. Exceptions exist
where more than one authority oversees one county, e.g. Galway City Council and Galway County

                                                                                                                        24
Council, or the four authorities in Dublin. In these cases, the social housing support application form
provides the option of selecting areas of choice under all housing authorities in the county.

HAP is unique as a social housing support in that it allows a household to find accommodation in any
county and to apply for social housing support ­ i.e. HAP ­ provided by the county where their social
housing assessment was made (their `originating authority'). Circulars concerning inter-authority
HAP refer to the intended flexibility and say that it allows for "better utilisation of all private rental
stock for social housing purposes" and "remov[es] geographical barriers to employment". According
to anonymised data received from the SSC, there are just under 2,400 households living in a HAP
supported tenancy outside their `originating authority'.

Circular 15/2017 sets out a protocol for inter-authority HAP. Under the rules set out in the circular,
the originating authority continues to deal with the household and the application, including setting
differential rent and including the household on its transfer list (if requested). However, the HAP
caps that apply are those in effect in the area where the property is located.

Initially, inter-authority HAP was facilitated only between areas where the social housing eligibility
income bands were the same or between authorities that already operated shared areas of choice
(e.g. Cork City and County). This was to prevent an anomaly arising whereby a person earning above
the income level for a particular housing authority area could avail of HAP in that area if assessed in
an area with a higher threshold. Circular 10/2019 revised the approach so that a household with an
income below the threshold for the intended rental area can now avail of inter-authority HAP in that
area, even if the general threshold in that household's originating area is higher.

Circular 10/2019 reaffirms the roles of each local authority in the operation of inter-authority HAP:
"The original local authority takes responsibility for the tenant (original local authority differential
rent, original local authority transfer list) the new local authority takes responsibility for the property
(property inspection, new local authority rent limits and advice on market rent). The original local
authority should consult with the new local authority in relation to the market rent for the relevant
area, particularly when discretion is being applied". The circulars advise that "[t]he movement of
HAP applicants between local authority areas should be facilitated wherever possible".

Although according to the circulars, the role of the destination local authority is limited to providing
guidance to the originating local authority, on reasonable market rents and the relevant HAP caps, in
practice, there appear to be different approaches being taken. Many destination local authorities
confirmed that they do not see that they have any role in incoming inter-authority HAP beyond
providing information on current market rents at the request of the originating authority and
handling the inspection of the property. Others, however, advised that they would not "facilitate"
incoming inter-authority HAP in certain circumstances. For instance, one local authority advised that
it would "say no" to an originating local authority if it considered the proposed rent "extortionate".
In another case, one destination local authority advised an originating authority by letter that it was
"not in a position to facilitate" a particular application on the basis that a large number of
households on its own social housing list were also seeking accommodation with HAP in the town in
question. A further local authority advised that in its experience certain authorities are "strict" about
inter-authority HAP and frequently "refuse". Several local authorities advised that they do not object
to other authorities' "refusals" of inter-authority HAP and will not approve an application where the
destination authority has raised such objections.

Circular 10/2019 doesn't include any formal grounds on which a destination local authority may
prevent an originating authority from approving HAP for a property in the jurisdiction of another
local authority.

Case study
 Background
 Cathal's local authority assessed him as eligible for social housing support. He could not find a
 property to rent with HAP in the area but found one in another county. However, the destination
 local authority refused. The landlord of the property contacted the destination local authority to
 query the statutory basis for its refusal. The local authority told him that it was not in a position to
 support the application given high demand for the area in question on its social housing list, as
 well as an extremely limited supply of private rental properties in that area. The landlord again
 queried the statutory basis for this. The local authority did not respond directly on that point but
 instead made reference to legislation setting out social housing income bands. It maintained that
 households assessed by a local authority in the highest band may only avail of HAP in areas within
 the same band. (Cathal's local authority was in the highest band and the destination was in the
 lowest). The local authority went on to say that the originating authority must seek approval from
 the destination authority in such cases and that on this basis, and in light of high demand in the
 area, it had refused.

 Ombudsman's Investigation
 The Ombudsman highlighted to the local authority that there is no basis in the inter-authority HAP
 guidelines for a destination authority to refuse to facilitate an application on the grounds of high
 demand in the area. The Ombudsman also noted that it is possible for a household from a higher
 band local authority to avail of HAP in a lower band area as long as the household's income is
 below the lower threshold, and that there is no requirement in the circulars that the originating
 authority seek approval in these cases.

 Outcome
 The originating local authority made a new decision based on the Ombudsman's intervention. The
 tenancy was commenced on foot of this new decision.

Finding:
Inter-authority HAP is where a local authority (originating authority) approves a HAP application for
a tenancy to rent in another local authority's geographic area (destination authority). There are a
number of circulars that outline a standard protocol for the roles of each local authority in respect of
inter-authority HAP. Under the current circulars, the role of the destination authority is to provide
information to the originating authority in respect of rents in the area, and to carry out property
inspections. However, DHLGH has informed us that it was always intended that there would be
scope for the destination authority to refuse an inter-authority HAP application in limited
circumstances. This is not outlined in the circulars.
Recommendation:
The guidance should be clarified to confirm that while acceptance of an inter-authority HAP
application should be the default position, it can be refused by the receiving authority in limited
circumstances. DHLGH should be told of refusals on a regular basis to ensure that any pattern of
refusals or over-use of this function is identified.

Chapter 4: Homeless HAP

When a person is homeless, or at risk of becoming homeless they may avail of Homeless HAP, also
known as the Place Finder Service. Homeless HAP provides applicants with a deposit and allows for
up to two months' rent to be paid in advance to the landlord26. In order to qualify for the payment, a
local authority must be satisfied that the applicant is homeless or at risk of homelessness i.e. has no
other potential options for accommodation, and requires the extra supports to secure a property. It
is not a separate scheme to standard HAP, but rather additional supports that can be used. In the
Dublin area, Homeless HAP is operated by the Dublin Regional Homeless Executive (DRHE) on behalf
of the four local authorities. The DRHE handles cases where the lead authority has already carried
out a homeless assessment of the applicant27.

Homeless HAP was made available to each of the 31 local authorities from the 19 January 2018,
following initial trials of the scheme in Dublin with the DRHE from 2015, and in Cork City Council
from 201728. The service was introduced to assist homeless households find accommodation where
the high demand and low levels of supply make the rental market very challenging and where "the
provision of such service is justified".29

Due to the ongoing housing crisis, households who contact our Office are frequently eligible for
Homeless HAP, but are unable to locate a property due to high rental costs and low supply. This can
lead to increased longer-term pressure on homeless supports such as emergency accommodation.
The following section outlines how Homeless HAP was established and how it is implemented by
local authorities and the DRHE.

4.1 The application process and initial stage

To access Homeless HAP an applicant must present to a local authority as homeless, or provide the
local authority with evidence of an impending risk of homelessness, such as a notice to quit their
current tenancy. Once a tenancy is in place, it is essentially the same as a standard HAP tenancy,
except that the rent is paid in advance (i.e. for the coming month), instead of in arrears (i.e. for the
previous month). The DRHE may also apply a discretionary increase in the HAP cap of up to 50%,
rather than the usual 35%30 (See Chapter 5.4 for further information on discretionary increases).

As of May 2023, there were 13,363 households in receipt of Homeless HAP nationally31. As those
who are deemed eligible for Homeless HAP might not have located a property and submitted an
application yet, they would not be on the SSC's system. There is no centralised figure available for
the number of people who have been deemed eligible for Homeless HAP.

During the course of this investigation it emerged that while the additional supports offered by local
authorities under Homeless HAP are the same across local authorities, ­ i.e. the deposit, the rent in
advance, and a discretionary increase in the HAP cap ­ the way in which these supports were
administered varies significantly between local authorities. The differences often depend on
whether the local authority has a member of staff dedicated to solely handling Homeless HAP cases,
often referred to as a Place Finder. There are three main ways in which the processing stage of the
service is operated.

4.2 Role of the Place Finder

As noted above, Homeless HAP is often coordinated within a local authority by a Place Finder. While
the initial purpose of Homeless HAP was primarily providing access to deposit and advance rental
payments, an option was given to local authorities to enhance the service by assigning staff to
provide assistance in the following:

    · seeking out potential properties suitable for households currently identified as homeless;
    · liaising with specific households to establish their specific needs;
    · establishing relationships with local property agents and landlords; and,
    · supporting those homeless households to exit emergency accommodation and secure a

         tenancy.32
Staff assigned to this role are generally referred to as Place Finders. The role of the Place Finder
varies between local authorities. As of December 2022, 26 of 31 local authorities had, or had access
to, a Place Finder with recruitment for the role ongoing in a number of local authorities33. The Place
Finder is generally a member of either the Homeless Section or the HAP Section in a local authority.

Local authorities can apply to the DHLGH to obtain exchequer funding towards a dedicated member
of staff for the Place Finder role. Local authorities looking for this funding are required to submit a
proposal to DHLGH for consideration, including information about the targets which they are
expected to achieve by appointing the dedicated resource, such as exits from emergency
accommodation into new HAP tenancies.

One of the local authorities without a Place Finder noted to this Office that it had previously
provided the additional Place Finder service through a service level agreement with an NGO34.

Excluding the Dublin area, 13 of the 22 local authorities which reported having a Place Finder only
had one person in the role. None had more than two Place Finders35. Many of the Place Finders
which this Office spoke to were new to their role, with high rates of staff turnover reported. As there
is no guarantee of a handover period for the role, and due to the limited guidance and training from
the Department, many of the Place Finders are required to re-establish the role once they are
appointed. The level of day-to-day support they receive from HAP teams can vary depending on
what section they are assigned to (i.e. the HAP section or the Homeless section). In one local
authority we spoke to the Place Finder was located in a different town to the standard HAP section.
It is important to recognise that these members of staff are tasked with the difficult job of
supporting people through an extremely vulnerable time in their lives. In order to ensure that the
role of the Place Finder operates effectively, supports and guidance must be given to Place Finders.

4.2.1 Locating a property
Locating a property remains a challenge for HAP applicants, as there is currently limited supply in the
rental market across the country 36. Tenants eligible for Homeless HAP may source their own
accommodation, but due to the supply they may have difficulty doing so. The Simon report "Locked
Out of the Market Study December 2024" documented that only 46 properties were available within
the standard or discretionary HAP limits across its 16 study areas, or 4% of the total properties
studied.37 One of the supports which can be provided by the Place Finder is help locating a rental
property.38 When asked about the role of the Place Finder, most local authorities told our Office that
part of the role was to "find and source" private rental properties for homeless households.
Although, notably one local authority said that "the onus is on the individual/applicant to source
their own private rental accommodation".

Place Finders told our Office that to locate a property they often use rental websites such as daft.ie
or rent.ie, and consider the properties available together with the composition of the households
that are found eligible for Homeless HAP. Some Place Finders noted that they have built good
working relationships with landlords and agents to ensure that HAP is seen as a reliable form of
payment. This is with a view to encouraging those landlords to contact the Place Finder if a new
property becomes available.

In the DRHE the HAP Place Finder Fee was introduced as an incentive for landlords to offer
properties exclusively to the shared service for Homeless HAP applicants. This is a 500 fee which is
offered for exclusively providing a property to the DRHE's Place Finder (i.e. the property is not
advertised in other places, such as on letting websites). In order to encourage landlords/agents to
provide additional properties, an additional 500 per property can be provided for the fourth, and
any subsequent properties secured in this way. The landlord/agent is required to invoice the DRHE
for each payment. The fee can be paid once per landlord per property. The decision was taken to
provide this fee due to the extent of the homelessness, the lack of housing supply, and "the
comparatively higher cost of accommodating homeless households in emergency
accommodation39".

However, it is unclear how much of an incentive the fee actually is. The DRHE noted that even with
the fee, and the 50% discretionary increase to the cap, it can be difficult to locate properties. It
stated that properties are often on the border of being within maximum of the increase cap, and can
require negotiation to get a reduction in price. In these cases, sustainability can be a problem as
households can be priced out if there is a single rent increase.

Deposit and Rent in Advance
The provision of deposits and rent in advance are the major financial supports that differentiate
Homeless HAP from standard HAP. These supports were made available to all local authorities in
2018 when Homeless HAP was rolled out nationwide40. The provision of these measures recognised
that applicants facing homelessness may require additional supports to help them access the private
rental market, and that deposits were already often being sought from the Department of Social
Protection through use of the Exceptional Needs Payment. The new measure allowed local
authorities to recoup the cost of the deposit and advance rent from the DHLGH, via the SSC.

4.4 Place Finder network

In order to ensure there was a clear understanding of the role of the Place Finder, and clear
communication between DHLGH and the Place Finders, a Place Finder Network was established in

when a DHLGH circular proposed to expand the membership to include a representative from each
of the regional homeless lead authorities. This was done in order to make the group become more
representative of the Place Finders, and to enable more in-depth consideration of issues facing
Homeless HAP and the Place Finder service46. The role of the representative was to represent all
local authorities, and to liaise as required with local authorities, in their respective regions.

Along with the regional representatives, the network was to include a member from both the HAP
policy unit and the homeless policy unit in the DHLGH, the DRHE, and the Housing Agency, which

develop, among other things, the best policy practises for roll-out across all local authority areas.

From the discussions held with local authorities during this investigation, the success of these groups
are mixed. One Place Finder said that they had had a number of meetings with the Place Finder
Network in the last year. Another Place Finder informed this Office of some proactive steps which
their regional group was making to standardise the role. They said that the group met quarterly and
was drafting a best practice manual for Place Finders in the region. The Place Finder noted that some
of their recent innovations in how they operate the role came from this group, such as looking to
contact the landlord of a HAP tenant who was allocated a local authority tenancy to ensure the
property was retained for Homeless HAP.

However, one Place Finder told this Office that they were not aware of the Place Finder Network
having met since 2018. They said that that they were aware that there were regional networks, but
they were not in one. However, they noted that they had ongoing informal contact with other Place
Finders in the region. It also remains unclear the level of interaction the local authorities who do not
have a specific person employed as a Place Finder have with the network.

4.5 Leaving International Protection Accommodation

As noted above, Homeless HAP also looks to support applicants who are at risk of becoming
homeless. Included in this are those who are housed by the International Protection
Accommodation Service (IPAS) and have been granted permission to remain in Ireland, and are thus
due to leave their IPAS accommodation. DHLGH has issued guidance that, once found eligible for
social housing support/HAP, these applicants should receive access to Homeless HAP supports to
assist in the transition to other accommodation.

The rationale for this is that IPAS is only obliged to provide accommodation to those awaiting
consideration of their case, not those who have already been granted "leave to remain". As such,
those who have received stability in terms of living in the country, may be asked to leave IPAS
accommodation without a property available to them. IPAS noted that it sometimes has to move
households into emergency accommodation if they don't obtain social housing or HAP tenancies.

The decision to make the additional supports of Homeless HAP available to those eligible for social
housing was seen as a positive solution by DHLGH. This is was due to HAP being immediately
available for those eligible and not requiring the person to have been on the social housing waiting
list for a lengthy duration48.

An Irish Residence Permit is needed to apply for the social housing assessment. In discussion with
this Office IPAS noted that while some local authorities will accept an application pending the
submission of the permit, others will wait for the permit before accepting the application. However,
it noted that this difference in approach was not currently an issue due to the lack of properties
available nationally.

Applicants are then required to wait for up to twelve weeks for their social housing assessment to be
completed before they can apply for HAP. IPAS noted that these delays can be an issue, as people
will sometimes have a property lined up through community networks, but this can be lost if the
wait is too long.

As outlined in the "Information paper on supporting people with status/leave to remain" those
leaving IPAS accommodation do not need to have a proven link to the local area to apply for Social

4.6 Other affected groups

The feature of HAP that sets it apart from other forms of social housing support is that the applicant
is required to source their own accommodation on the private rental market. The general difficulties
of securing private rented accommodation have been well documented elsewhere, especially when
it comes to people looking for rental properties within HAP limits.50 As well as issues of supply and
cost, households seeking to rent accommodation with HAP can face the additional barrier of
reluctance on the part landlords who do not want to accept them as tenants because of their
reliance on HAP. This is despite legislation against housing discrimination being introduced, under
which households that have been rejected as tenants because they are availing of HAP can bring
proceedings against the landlord to the Workplace Relations Commission.51 The recognition of these
difficulties are part of the rationale behind the additional supports offered under Homeless HAP.

There are other affected groups that have not been discussed in this chapter that can experience
difficulties securing private rental accommodation, to whom extra supports are not always available.
Members of ethnic minorities can face discrimination when trying to rent in the private market, with
this discrimination hidden behind seemingly otherwise legitimate requests. NGOs have informed this
Office that references are often requested from Travellers or people leaving IPAS accommodation.
The NGOs feel that these requests are disingenuous and can be used as a means by which to refuse
them as tenants.

People with additional housing requirements for medical or other health reasons can face
disadvantages in the private rental market, further reducing their options in an already limited pool.
For instance, a person with mobility issues may need to live in a ground-floor property, to have
accommodation with certain adaptations, or to live particularly close to public transport.

The problems of a lack of supply, high private rents, and discrimination are not strictly an aspect of
the administration of HAP. However, shifting the State's responsibility for securing properties for
social housing onto the individual is particularly onerous for people who face extra hurdles when it
comes to finding an appropriate home on the private rental market.

Recognising this, some local authorities already implement extra supports within the existing
parameters of HAP. Six local authorities informed our Office that they provide separate Traveller
specific support. One local authority outlined that it has additional staffing resources working in this

5.1 Background

The HAP scheme involves agreements between the landlord and tenant, the tenant and the local
authority and the local authority and the landlord. As part of this, the tenant agrees to pay the
landlord any rent above the HAP cap and also agrees to pay differential rent to the local authority.
The following explains the different elements of the cost of rent:

    1. Landlord rent ­ the rent charged by a landlord. The HAP payment covers some or all of this
         amount.

    2. Differential rent ­ the rent charged by the local authority for the delivery of social housing
         supports which is relative to the income of the household.

    3. Top-up payment by the tenant ­ the difference between the rental amount due and the
         total payment from the local authority which is paid by the tenant.

Local authorities are responsible for the following payments:

    4. HAP cap ­ the contribution to the rent paid by HAP to the landlord. It is determined by the
         family type and the location of the household nationally. The current HAP amounts are set
         out in Appendix 2.

    5. HAP discretionary increase ­ an amount allowable by the DHLGH for an increased payment
         to landlords on the HAP scheme. Under the current guidelines, local authorities may pay up
         to an additional 35% of the HAP cap for the household as set out in Circular 29/2022.

This chapter will look at the costs involved in more detail, along with an analysis of the implications
and practical outcomes.

As mentioned earlier, once an applicant is deemed eligible for social housing support, they are
provided with a HAP pack which does not always include a breakdown of the costs of HAP. In our
discussions with the Housing NGOs, they advised that while some local authorities provide a
provisional estimate of the cost to the tenant under HAP, often applicants have to search for a
tenancy without knowing the costs and contributions involved.

In terms of clarity, it would be useful if potential applicants were informed of what might happen in
the event of a tenancy. One local authority provides a letter which includes an example as follows:

     "To illustrate how the scheme works by example:
     Alan is a single person who is on the Housing List and his income is 203 per week. He has
     sourced accommodation and entered into a private tenancy agreement with the landlord
     for a two bedroom flat, for 400 per month. He is deemed eligible for a Housing Assistance
     Payment. The rent contribution limit that is set for XXX County Council to pay a landlord for
     a single person is 360. Therefore, the Local authority will pay directly to Alan's landlord on
     the last Wednesday of each month 360 and Alan will have to pay the balance of 40
     directly to the landlord.

     Alan will also be liable to make a rent contribution to the Local authority. Based on his
     income of 203 per week, the rent contribution is calculated at 27.50 per week. This
     amount will be deducted each week, either by setting up a standing order from bank
     account or by getting a Household Budget Deduction* set up (*if social welfare payment is
     made by An Post)."

However, similar examples are not always provided by other local authorities.

 Finding:
 Applicants are not always given clear information about the rent contribution a the tenant will pay
 a local authority; the amount of HAP the local authority will pay towards rent; the potential
 availability of a discretionary increase to the HAP cap; or the shortfall that a tenant may have to

5.3.2 Legislative basis for differential rent in HAP and revision of the differential rent system
The legislative basis for charging differential rent to HAP tenants is unclear. Under the 2014 Act, a
household being supported by HAP must pay a "rent contribution to the housing authority".64 The
legislation provides that this is to be paid "in accordance with section 44" of the Act, which provides
in turn that the rent will be determined "in accordance with section 31" of the 2009 Act. Section 31
sets out the requirement for housing authorities to publish a differential rent scheme in line with
Ministerial regulations. However, neither section 44 of the 2014 Act nor section 31 of the 2009 Act
has been commenced.

The DHLGH has confirmed that the legal basis for charging differential rent to social housing tenants
rests on section 58 of the Housing Act 1966. This section provides that local authorities may charge a
rent to tenants in dwellings owned by the authority and also that, "as respects any [...] services
provided under this Act, the authority may make such charge, whether by way of rent or otherwise,
as they shall think fit".65 Given that the 2014 Act defined HAP as a social housing support, it is
therefore a housing `service' for which local authorities charge differential rent.

Work has been ongoing in the DHLGH over a number of years in relation to the development of a
national differential rent scheme. Circular 35/201466 from the Department of the Environment,
Community and Local Government, set the revised schedule for commencement of rents under
section 31 of the 2009 Act as 1 July 2015. That circular makes it clear the intention that a revised
national framework for differential rents would be in place to coincide with the implementation of
HAP.

Parliamentary Questions (Appendix 5) have been answered on several occasions between 2017 and
2019 which reference work being done on a review of the rent schemes, with a view to developing

5.3.4 Hardship clauses
Each local authority's differential rent scheme is obliged to include a hardship clause, to reduce the
amount of differential rent payable to a local authority where a household is in financial difficulty.71
Having reviewed the various schemes, it appears that two of them, Waterford City and County
Council, and Wexford County Council, include HAP-specific hardship clauses. These clauses are
almost identical and essentially say that where the maximum discretion is being applied (both refer
to 20% as the maximum discretion as they were adopted prior to Circular 29/2022) and the cost of
differential rent, plus any additional rent payments to the landlord, exceed 30% of income, then a
reduced differential rent will be considered. Wexford County Council specifies that the minimum
rent will be 20.

Clare County Council also has a reference to HAP in its differential rent scheme which will be
discussed further in Section 5.4.

Five of the differential rent schemes refer to the Money Advice and Budgeting Service (MABS): Three
local authorities (Louth, Cavan, Mayo) indicate that persons finding themselves in financial difficulty
or financial hardship should consider approaching MABS for advice. One (Galway County Council)
advises that tenants must "first seek the help of the Money Advice and Budgeting Service and the
Department of Social Protection", while Tipperary County Council advises that "all hardship cases
must initially be referred through MABS...". MABS has indicated anyone in need of their support
should be given the information necessary to get that assistance, and in this regard signposting of
their service is always valuable. However, the service that it provides is quite invasive in terms of an
individual's financial and personal details, and it should not be a requirement of a government
service that an individual should have to submit to that level of scrutiny before they can access it.

In speaking to MABS they have expressed concern at the level of difficulty that they have in relation
to accessing hardship clauses for people in HAP. One MABS Money Adviser told us that she had
requested hardship rent from one local authority for approximately 10 HAP tenants over the course
of 2-3 years without success, despite submitting a financial statement and detailed explanation
letters about their situations and the hardship they were in. In contrast, the same Money Adviser
has had over 30 cases of hardship being applied in "standard" social housing cases, without
recollection of any being refused. She also advised that other colleagues in MABS have had the same
experience. NGOs informed us that even where the hardship rate is approved, it is only for a limited
period, and as a result, applicants have to apply again six months later.

In one complaint to our Office, a tenant was refused the standard hardship clause on the basis of
there being a HAP hardship clause available in the local authority's differential rent scheme.
However, the HAP hardship clause was only relevant where the total outgoings on rent were in
excess of 30% of weekly income. In this case, while the amount owed was less than 30%, it did not
take account of the fact that the tenant had significant additional outgoings due to a disability. This
factor was only recognised following intervention and extensive correspondence from this Office.

to ensure that Joan was below 30% of income ratio, and why it had not replied to MABS' request to

set out our view that there was greater flexibility in the circular than was being applied.

Outcome
On foot of discussions with our Office, the local authority accepted that 30.2% was over the 30% of
income set out in the circular and applied the maximum discretionary increase to the HAP cap of
35%. The local authority, however, did not accept that a hardship rate of differential rent should
apply. Joan is now paying circa 24% of her income in rent.

5.4 HAP caps, discretion, and sustainability
5.4.1 HAP caps and the full cost of rent

The Economic Assessment of the Proposed Housing Assistance Payment says that "[i]t is intended
under HAP that a local authority will make a full rental payment directly to the landlord. HAP tenants
would then be required to make a rent contribution to the local authority". 72 Elsewhere, it states
that "under HAP... the local authority will pay the landlord the full rent on behalf of the tenant". 73

An explanatory memorandum prepared to accompany the Housing (Miscellaneous Provisions) Bill
2014, sets out that "the Housing Authority will pay the full rent (`housing assistance') for the
accommodation direct to the landlord on behalf of the household, subject to a maximum rent limit
applicable under the scheme". This "full rent" is also referenced in Circular 40/2014 (Appendix 1),
the purpose of which was to explain the various sections of the Act to local authorities.

It is clear, therefore, that the original intention of HAP was to cover the full cost of rent to a
maximum rent limit applicable under the scheme. As further referenced below, evidence of direct
payments to landlords was seen as a reason to make a discretionary increase in any given case74. The
maximum rent limits referred to were set out in various circulars during the roll-out of the scheme,
but Circular 48/2016 finalising the roll-out published the final HAP caps nationwide (see Appendix 2)
which are still in place to this day.

As the cost of rent continues to rise, the availability of rental properties which come within the

Recommendation
DHLGH should review the effectiveness of discretionary increases to the HAP caps. In the first

authorities in the application of discretionary increases. Caps should also be revised on a regular
basis to reflect the rental market.

5.4.2 Households to which the standard HAP caps do not apply (`Section 43' households)
The standard HAP limits do not apply to households above certain sizes or households of certain
types or compositions. Under section 43(b) of the 2014 Act, the Minister may determine the
maximum amount of HAP payable in these cases. When a local authority receives an application for
HAP from a household that does not fit into the standard HAP framework, it should ask the
Department to determine the appropriate level of HAP.

An explanatory memorandum provided by the Department provides a number of examples of
scenarios where this might be relevant:

    · Household is in shared accommodation.
    · Households consisting of an adult or couple with four or more children.
    · Adult dependent has a medical condition and parent/guardian is in receipt of carer's benefit

         (medical dependency).
    · Adult dependent who continues onto further education (education dependency)
    · Adult has a disability and requires a live-in carer.
    · Atypical households ­ cases where large family groups choose to be on the one housing

         application.

Where a local authority receives a section 43 determination from the Department, it may apply the
same amount of HAP should a household of the same size/type/composition apply again thereafter.

Data from the SSC in May 2023 showed that there were 1,756 households around the country
whose HAP rate had been determined based on section 43. However, when looking at this data, it
became apparent that there were a number of anomalies. For example:

    · One county has no section 43 cases recorded at all.
    · One case, recorded as Single 3+ with a HAP cap appropriate to Single/Couple +3, (630) is

         apparently receiving a payment of 20 discretion and their payment is 650 towards a rent
         of 900. This does not appear to make sense as a Section 43.

Furthermore, in our meetings, one local authority appeared to understand section 43 requests as
only relating to households with four children (to the exclusion of households with more than four
children or any of the other examples listed above).

5.4.3 Discretionary increases to HAP
Even before the roll-out was finalised, it had been recognised that in certain circumstances, the
specified HAP caps may not be sufficient to allow tenants to find appropriate accommodation.

Circular 31/2016 allowed for a 20% discretionary increase to the caps. The guidance for this increase
called for local authorities to have regard to existing rents in the area and any evidence of payments
from the tenant directly to the landlord. This changed however, with Circular 29/2022, which raised
the discretionary increase up to a maximum 35% (excluding homeless HAP in Dublin region, where
an increase of up to 50% may be applied to the HAP cap).

This was conditional on the household spending no more than 35% of income on rent, or 30% in the
case of households with solely social protection income. When we asked DHLGH about this change
in position, it said that these income thresholds were "set by the Department of Public Expenditure
and Reform as a condition of sanction for the additional exchequer expenditure needed to fund the
increased HAP discretion introduced in July 2022". DHLGH also supported these thresholds by
reference to the following extract from a 2016 report from the National Competitiveness Council:

       "A common methodology to assess the affordability of housing is to calculate the
       ratio of housing costs to income (gross, net, residual, etc.), and compare this to a
       predetermined level that indicates affordability. The most common ratio for
       affordability is 30 per cent [...] The main methodological issue arising from this
       approach is the essentially arbitrary nature of the chosen threshold. The choice of 30
       per cent is not based on any economic analysis and can in fact be traced historically
       back to its use in rent control legislation in the US during the 1960s and 1980s. This
       led to the 30 per cent figure becoming the de facto threshold for lending criteria by
       federal housing enterprises, and is now a commonly cited figure in studies using the
       price-income methodology internationally. The 30 per cent figure has also been cited
       in the Irish housing market as a reference point for researchers' analysis, while a 35
       per cent figure has also been used to assess affordability by the Housing Agency in
       2015."

Our investigation found that the interpretation of "no more than" seems to vary, with some local
authorities using it as a target and some as a limit. For example, one awards the full 35% for any new
application, even if that brings the proportion of income spent on rent well under the 30/35%
threshold, on the basis that rents in the locality are so much higher than they were when the caps
were first set out. Other local authorities are inclined to be more frugal when it comes to providing

                                                                                                                        51
discretionary increases, applying just enough of an increase to the HAP cap to ensure that the
household is paying 30/35% of income on rent.

In addition, Circular 29/2022 refers to "monthly household income" but does not specify whether a
household's income should be calculated using the Household Means Policy or the relevant
differential rent scheme. At least two cases came to light during our investigation, where a local
authority included carer's allowance (a payment disregarded in the Household Means Policy but
often included in differential rent scheme) in the calculation of the rent-to-income ratio. This meant
that the proportion of income spent on rent appeared lower than it would have been if the carer's
allowance had been discounted, meaning these households were unlikely to be awarded a
discretionary increase to the HAP cap. At the same time, this can be useful for some tenants when it
comes to the local authority's assessment of whether the rent on a property is sustainable. One local
authority informed us that some applicants have asked that carer's allowance be included because,
the higher the household's income, the more chance there is that the rent will be under the
sustainability threshold. Although this may be desirable from the applicant's perspective in the short
term, we are not satisfied that including Carer's Allowance in this calculation is a solution to the
longer term issue of the rising cost of rent. We asked DHLGH its view on the inclusion of allowances
excluded from the Social Housing Means Policy in the calculation of income for the purposes of
Circular 29/2022. It said: "The application of discretion is a matter for the individual local authority,
however local authority staff are reminded to ensure that a consistent and measured approach is
taken in applying the discretionary flexibility provided."

Differential rent schemes are for each local authority to determine. In contrast, the discretionary
increase is a matter for DHLGH. In specifying 30% and 35% as the upper limits of income to be spent
on rent, DHLGH should for consistency clarify how "income" is to be determined. This should also be
considered in any revised guidance on the implementation of the 30/35% income thresholds.

A further point of divergence between local authorities' application of discretionary increases arises
in respect of Rent Pressure Zones (RPZ), where landlords may increase the rent by no more than 2%
per annum. Local authorities and NGOs told us that this has now become a standardised, expected
increase. However, there is no coinciding increase in HAP caps or discretionary increases to
accommodate this. During our investigation, some local authorities confirmed that they hold back
some of the discretion to allow for annual increases. One local authority informed an applicant that
"the reason we don't always award full discretion is in case the tenants get a rent increase and we
would then not be in a position to offer any further assistance".

As we have seen earlier in the chapter, rents have increased significantly since the introduction of
HAP. The SSC figures demonstrate that 66% of households in receipt of HAP receive some level of
discretionary increase to the applicable HAP cap. When two-thirds of HAP households receive some
level of discretionary increase, it is clear that use of this discretion has become the norm. This
routine use of discretionary increases, with wide divergences between and within local authorities,
can lead to an inequitable outcome not only for households in different counties but those under
the same local authority.

5.6 What is a fair rent?

From the earlier sections, we can see that HAP tenants pay both differential rent and, in most cases,
top-up payments directly to landlords due to the rising cost of rent. Despite the fact that two-thirds
of households in the HAP scheme receive some form of increase to the HAP cap at their local
authority's discretion, top-ups to landlords are being paid by the same proportion of tenants. As
discussed in connection with discretionary HAP increases, the Department of Housing and DPER
have settled on 30/35% of income as an acceptable and sustainable amount for households in the
HAP scheme to spend on rent, between their differential rent and any landlord top-up.

It is interesting to note that Circular 35/2014, referred to in Section 5.3.2, set out bands for a
proposed national differential rent framework. It proposed a payment of:

    · 30 for an adult aged 26 or over on the first 14,000 of income (c. 11%),
    · 16%-20% on the next 5,000 (i.e. 14,001 - 19,000)
    · 21%-24% on the next 5,000 (i.e. 19,001 - 24,000)
    · 25% on the next 5,000 (i.e. 24,001 - 29,000)
    · 22% on any income over 29,000.

As referenced in 5.3, some local authorities already use income bands to calculate differential rent.
Some also disregard amounts or certain sources of income, or charge lower percentages on part, to
arrive at what they consider a fair rent for their tenants to pay. For example, Meath County Council's
differential rent scheme calculates rent at 18% at the lower end (28 from income of 152) and 22%
at the higher end (82.25 from income of 369), excluding any deductions applicable.77

The Department's proposals in 2014 support this progressive approach to rent for tenants in
standard social housing. The Department's guideline of 30/35% as an acceptable proportion of
income to spend on rent in a market where properties cannot be sourced within the HAP caps is
completely at odds with its policy of graduated differential rent. Using these maxima applied to HAP,
low-income households may have no alternative but to pay larger portions of their income directly
to landlords, or risk losing out on housing.

77 https://www.meath.ie/system/files/media/file-uploads/2021-10/Differential%20Rent%20Scheme%202021.pdf, accessed 12/9/2023

                                                                                                                        56
Finding:
According to data provided by the HAP Shared Services Centre in 2023, around 60% of HAP tenants
also make additional payments directly to their landlords. HAP tenants, like their counterparts in
standard social housing, pay differential rent. Unlike their counterparts, they do not have access to
the same benefits. These benefits include access to hardship clauses and flexible, tailored arrears
repayment plans, designed to help protect tenants in times of financial difficulty, and the ability to
raise maintenance issues directly with the local authority or approved housing body.
Even though legislatively HAP tenants are considered to be socially housed, local authorities
generally do not apply hardship clauses to HAP tenancies nor is there permanent access to
equivalent repayment plans for arrears. This puts HAP tenants at risk of having their HAP payments
suspended should they be unable to pay off arrears. This unfairness is compounded by the fact that
they do not have the security of tenure that their counterparts in other forms of social housing have.
The cumulative effect of these inequities poses a risk, in some cases, of HAP tenants falling into a
poverty trap. Although DHLGH has introduced a "landlord guarantee" to ensure continuity of
payment of HAP in the event of a tenant falling into arrears, this does not address the aspects of the
HAP scheme that contribute to a household's financial difficulties in the first place.
Recommendation:
We understand that DHLGH is working to create a more equitable national rent framework which is
an opportunity to recognise the unique implications for HAP tenants of differential rent schemes and
we support the direction of travel in this area.
In the short term, DHLGH should re-emphasise to local authorities that, as per its own national
guidance on differential rents and as HAP tenants are considered to be socially housed, they should
have access to hardship clauses. The application of such clauses to HAP tenants in financial difficulty

6.1 HAP transfer list

A household that has been allocated social housing is removed from the relevant local authority's
housing list because it is deemed that their housing need has been met. The same principle applies
to households in the HAP scheme, given that the legislation establishing HAP defines it as a form of
social housing. This means that once a household has entered a private tenancy supported by HAP, it
is taken off the housing list.

Several TDs objected to this aspect of the scheme when the 2014 Act (then a bill) was debated in the
Dáil, raising concerns that people on a local authority's housing list have a legitimate expectation
that their wait will result in a long-term home provided directly by the local authority, and that HAP
cannot match this concept of social housing.

Speaking on the bill in the Seanad in July 2014, Jan O'Sullivan, then Minister of State for Housing and
Planning, addressed these concerns by stating that households in HAP would be able to access the
relevant local authority's transfer list. The transfer list, as ordinarily operated by local authorities,
allows tenants in standard social housing to apply for a transfer to another property under the same
housing authority. Minister O'Sullivan advised that, for households supported by HAP,

         "[t]he transfer list will reflect the specific priority or previous position the household
         had on the previous waiting list within the authority area in which they are resident.
         The principle will be that the reasonable expectations of households should be
         preserved. They will, therefore, be placed on a transfer list with no less favourable
         terms than if they had remained on the main housing waiting list". 78

These remarks were formalised as a ministerial direction issued in various circulars, requiring that
each housing authority a) maintain a transfer list to which households in the HAP scheme can be
added and b) set aside a proportion of its dwellings for allocation to households on that HAP transfer
list.79

6.2 HAP transfer list and the housing list

The HAP Tenants' Information Booklet advises that "Once in receipt of HAP, you can apply to

6.3 Grounds for transfer from HAP to standard social housing

In standard social housing, in order to apply for a transfer, an applicant must have been in their
current accommodation for at least two years, and satisfy the relevant grounds for seeking a
transfer. Acceptable grounds generally include that the current property is overcrowded; that a
household wishes to move to a smaller property (downsizing or "rightsizing"); that there are medical
grounds for moving to more suitable accommodation; that anti-social behaviour justifies moving; or
that other exceptional circumstances apply.

HAP tenants are currently asked within two weeks of the HAP tenancy commencing to confirm if
they wish to be considered for a move to a more permanent form of social housing by completion of
a HAP transfer form. A Ministerial Direction issued on the matter says that "All HAP tenants who
choose to do so may apply to be placed on this transfer list" and that they should "...be placed on
the transfer list with no less favourable terms than if they had remained on the waiting list". There is
no requirement for grounds for the transfer, such as those above, and there is no minimum
timeframe required to have been in a HAP tenancy.

Despite this, several allocation schemes indicate that households in HAP tenancies on the private
rental market are required to fulfil transfer criteria that should apply only to households looking to
transfer within the standard social housing stock. For instance, Westmeath County Council's
allocation scheme includes HAP-supported households in its list of households to whom transfer
conditions apply. It says that the Council "may consider applications for transfer" if there is
overcrowding or "under-utilised" accommodation; current accommodation does not meet the
household's requirements in respect of "physical, sensory, mental health or intellectual
impairment"; or there are other "exceptional medical or compassionate reasons". Mayo County
Council lists similar requirements for social housing transfers and, as noted above, includes HAP
tenants in this general transfer policy. Several allocation schemes include a general section on social
housing transfers and do not clarify that HAP-supported households in the transfer system are not
subject to these general criteria.

6.4 Reassessment of eligibility for social housing support

Local authorities may reassess households on their housing list to ensure that they still qualify for
social housing support. This can happen at any point while the household is on the social housing
waiting list. For instance, local authorities typically reassess a number of households for the
purposes of the Summary of Social Housing Assessments. A reassessment is also carried out upon
allocation of social housing, just before the household is confirmed to move into the property.

However, local authorities are not required to assess a household that is currently in receipt of some
form of social housing for eligibility for social housing, even if moving to another type of social
housing.100 Any relevant change in household circumstances ­ for instance, if its income has risen ­
will be taken into account in calculating the differential rent due but will not exclude the household
from social housing. This means that a household moving from one council-owned property to
another will not risk losing social housing altogether even if its income has risen since it was first
allocated housing. Similarly, under the current legislation, the local authority is not required to
reassess a household in the HAP scheme, as this is technically simply a transfer within the social
housing system.

We have confirmed that a number of local authorities apply this approach to HAP. For instance,
Carlow County Council and Wexford County Council both specify in their allocation schemes that
their consideration of HAP-supported households for allocation of standard social housing "will not
entail any review of eligibility."101. Louth County Council told us that households in the HAP scheme
are not reassessed for social housing eligibility. In respect of its periodic reassessment of housing
needs, Dublin City Council confirms on its website that "[t]ransfer applicants (DCC/HAP/RAS tenants)
are not included in this assessment".102

However, many local authorities told us that they do reassess the social housing eligibility of
households supported by HAP. For instance, one council said in response to our survey that
households in HAP transferring to standard social housing "are assessed as normal, no different[ly]
than applicants on the social housing list". Another told us that the same procedure is applied to
households allocated a council-owned property whether they are moving from the general housing
list or from HAP, i.e. that the allocations team carries out a reassessment to establish eligibility for
social housing and the household may be found no longer to qualify if its income is now over the
relevant threshold.103

More significantly, reassessment by some local authorities leads to inconsistencies in the
experiences of HAP across the country. A household in receipt of HAP support from one local
authority could lose social housing support altogether if its income goes above the relevant
threshold, whereas the equivalent household in another local authority area would not. We also
found that some local authorities' HAP sections were themselves unclear as to whether the
authority reassesses HAP households' eligibility for social housing at any point.

6.5 Impact of listing HAP tenants on the main housing list

In its recent report, the Housing Commission recommended that HAP be "reset" as a "short- to
medium-term support... for households eligible for social housing", who "must remain on the
housing list, except where their income levels increase above the eligibility criteria for social
housing". The report acknowledges that this would require the amendment of legislation "to remove
provisions that define [HAP] as `social housing support' and... regard recipients' long-term social
housing need as having been met if they receive these allowances". The report also notes that this
would allow local authorities to "review continued eligibility for HAP" on an annual basis and
recommends that it "should be withdrawn if the claimant's income exceeds the eligibility criteria".

treatment of HAP tenants as regards allocations of standard social housing.

Under current housing legislation, HAP is defined as a form of social housing support. This means
that on entering HAP, a household is considered to be socially housed and is therefore removed
from the social housing list. However, a large majority of households in HAP tenancies aim to
secure long-term, standard social housing. These households have already expressed that aim,
and been found eligible, through their social housing assessment. When the legislation

formalised through Ministerial Direction. HAP tenants who want to remain eligible for allocation
of standard social housing, and to retain and continue to build waiting time towards this, must opt
in to the transfer list within two weeks of HAP approval.

However, our investigation has found that there is in general a lack of transparency about how
the HAP transfer system operates and what impact moving to the transfer list will have on a
household's chances of securing standard social housing.

At the same time, HAP tenants arguably have an unfair advantage over households who do not
take HAP and stay on the main waiting list. Households in social housing (including HAP) are not
reassessed for eligibility ­ only their income is reassessed periodically to determine the
differential rent they should pay. This means that, unlike households coming off the main housing
list, HAP tenants do not have their eligibility for social housing reassessed at the point of being
allocated standard social housing as they are simply transferring between different forms of social
housing.

Recommendation:
The legislation defining HAP tenants as socially housed currently prevents their inclusion on the
main housing list. In this regard, we note the recommendations of the Housing Commission in its
2024 report. These propose that the legislation be amended to remove provisions that define HAP
as `social housing support' and that households supported by HAP be included on the social
housing waiting list without any negative affect on their priority, which we support.

In the interim, DHLGH should consider the policy intention of the Ministerial Direction in relation
to transfers to more permanent forms of social housing, and the availability of data to inform it if,
operationally, this directive is being administered in a manner that is equitable across the country.
Pending this review, the transfer list and main housing list should operationally be treated as one
list, with each household retaining the relative priority it would enjoy on a single list. Inclusion on
the list should be on an opt-out rather than opt-in basis, essentially meaning HAP households
automatically retain their status as awaiting standard social housing. This would avoid any
inadvertent loss of a household's status in the social housing allocation system, and reduce the
administrative burden of extra paperwork for both local authorities and the households
concerned.

Households accepting HAP support should also be given clear information about how the transfer
list operates, the distinction between it and the main housing list, and the impact receiving HAP
would have on a social housing application generally.

7.1 Landlord-tenant relationship

A household living in standard social housing is a tenant of the local authority or approved housing
body (AHB) and can raise any issues with the local authority directly. The tenant in that scenario will
benefit from certain policies and protections, as well as a degree of security of tenure. As we have
noted elsewhere, HAP-supported households are private tenants. Despite paying an equivalent
differential rent to the local authority for the provision of housing services, households in the HAP
scheme do not benefit from the same status or services as their counterparts in standard social
housing.106

Instead, the HAP household's private residential lease with its landlord is governed by the
Residential Tenancies Act 2004.107 Under this Act, all private landlords and tenants have certain
rights and are subject to certain obligations. For landlords, the latter include requirements in respect
of repairs, deposits, notice before entering the property, etc. If a HAP-supported household turns to
its local authority regarding difficulties with the property (save for those related to minimum
standards) or difficulties related to the landlord, the advice is that the matter is a private dispute in
which the housing authority has no role. Households in such situations are advised to pursue a
resolution directly with the landlord and, if that fails, to raise a complaint with the Residential
Tenancies Board.

    7.2 Property standards and inspections

Under the 2014 Act, each local authority must inspect properties that are rented supported by HAP,
to ensure that they comply with statutory standards for rental accommodation.109 The local
authority must have inspected and found the property compliant within 12 months before HAP was
sought, or an inspection must take place within eight months of the tenant applying for HAP on the
property.110 The standards are set out in the Housing (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1992, and relate
to the structural condition of the property, sanitary facilities, heating facilities, ventilation, and fire
safety, among others.111 The 1992 Act provides that a local authority can issue an improvement
notice directing the landlord to remedy a contravention by a certain deadline, and a prohibition
notice if the landlord fails to comply. A property under a prohibition notice cannot be rented until
the breaches of standards have been addressed.112

HAP sections we consulted pointed out that the inspection of properties is not a matter for them,
but for the local authority's inspections unit.113 The level of communication between the two
sections varies between local authorities. In one local authority, a member of the HAP section was
also part of the inspections section. Staff in one local authority's HAP section said that where they
have local knowledge that standards might be poor in a particular property, or where a landlord is
known to them in that respect, they would encourage the inspections section to prioritise the
inspection and any follow-up. However, the most common response among local authorities was
that there is little role for the HAP section once a property has been flagged for inspection on Sugar.

In 2017 Sugar was updated to allow it to record the details of property inspections. A circular about
the update requested that local authorities capture details of the inspection on the updated system
"in order to demonstrate that all local authorities are complying with their legislative obligations
under the 2014 Act".114 This circular also provided a manual on how to enter the inspection details
on Sugar. However, there are still inconsistencies between local authorities in the recording of
inspection details, with one local authority telling us that it was unaware that it was to update the
system if a prohibition notice had been issued on a property. The DRHE noted that it is the
responsibility of the local authority where the property is located to update inspection details on
Sugar. If that local authority does not fill in the fields correctly, the lead local authority for the
tenancy won't know if there is an issue with the property standards.

Despite the general disconnect between HAP sections and inspection, staff in local authorities' HAP
sections voiced a variety of views on property standards. Some said that in their experience issues
with standards in HAP properties tend to be relatively minor and are remedied quickly. However,
one noted that some landlords view properties in the HAP scheme as more expensive to maintain

7.3 Deposits

Like all private tenancies, landlords in the HAP scheme require a deposit in order to secure the
tenancy in the first place and so that the landlord has an element of security regarding damage to
their property. Social housing tenancies however, do not come with this requirement ­ except in the
case of HAP.

Approved social housing applicants, who generally have no other real option other than to avail of
HAP, are required to find the deposit ­ generally a month's rent ­ to secure a tenancy. Often this
requires borrowing from family or friends, or in some cases going to the Department of Social
Protection for assistance via the Community Welfare Officer (CWO), placing an additional
administrative burden on that Department. The exception to this is Homeless HAP, in which case the
deposit is paid by the local authority, but the onus remains on the tenant to get the deposit back
from the landlord, which can be difficult.

Catherine, the subject of the case study referred to earlier in this chapter, took her case to the RTB,
as she was advised by the local authority. The RTB found in Catherine's favour and awarded her the
return of the deposit, along with damages of over 4,000. Despite this award, Catherine still had not
received the return of the deposit months later. The local authority did not assist with a deposit for a
new tenancy as it said she was only entitled to one deposit and there were no circumstances in
which a second deposit could be provided. We have since been informed, however, that in
exceptional circumstances, other local authorities have provided second deposits where necessary.

7.4 Arrears and debt-management process

 Like other social housing tenants, HAP tenants in financial difficulty can fall into arrears if they fail to
 keep up with their differential rent. An unexpected bill or an illness can make a big difference to a
 household's ability to pay. The consequence of failing to pay off differential rent can be the loss of
 social housing. The 2014 Act provides that a household will not be eligible or will cease to be eligible
 for social housing support (including HAP) if in the preceding three years "the household... was in
 arrears of rent, rent contributions, charges, fees or any other moneys due and owing to a housing

 120 Page 48, section 24

authority or an approved body for an accumulated period of 12 weeks or more" and has not entered
or complied with "rescheduling arrangements". 121

For standard social housing tenants, "rescheduling arrangements" can be flexible and tailored on a
case-by-case basis. Dublin City Council's website advises its tenants who are having difficulties to
"[c]ontact us immediately" and the Council "will help you agree to a fair and reasonable repayment
plan which includes the weekly rent charge plus an additional sum to reduce the arrears". Galway
City Council advises the following: "If you are experiencing difficulties in paying your rent or if your
account has fallen into arrears we recommend that you contact an Arrears Controller in the Finance
Department of Galway City Council as soon as possible... The Rent Arrears Staff will work with you to
agree an arrangement to help you pay your rent arrears". Similarly, Cavan County Council advises
tenants to "contact the Rents Section immediately, and you can make an arrangement to pay off any
arrears on a weekly basis over a reasonable period". As well as flexible repayment plans, standard
social housing tenants can also avail of the local authority's hardship clause, under which differential
rent is reduced for a period.

As outlined elsewhere, the hardship clause is rarely used for HAP tenants. In addition, "rescheduling
arrangements" for clearing differential rent arrears in HAP are much more stringent than those that
apply to differential rent arrears accrued in standard social housing, despite the fact that the
majority of HAP tenants face the double burden of paying differential rent as well as top-ups to their
landlords.

Until May 2023, the HAP arrears process operated as follows:

    · Level 0: Tenancy is fully up to date with differential rent payments.
    · Level 1: First notification letter, issued to the tenant only. This is issued when the tenancy

         falls into arrears equivalent to 3 weeks' differential rent. The tenant has four weeks to clear
         the arrears (and the ongoing differential rent accruing over the same period).
    · Level 2: Second notification letter, issued to both the tenant and landlord. This is a warning
         letter prior to the suspension of HAP. This letter is sent when the tenant has accumulated
         additional arrears or has not cleared the arrears in full over the four-week period since the
         first notification letter issued.
    · Level 3: Suspension notification letter, issued to the tenant, landlord and local authority.
         This letter is sent when the tenant has continued to accumulate additional arrears, or has
         not cleared their arrears in full over the four-week period since the second notification letter
         issued. This letter notifies all parties that the payment of HAP will be suspended immediately
         unless the arrears are cleared.

    · Level 4: Pre-cessation notification letter, issued to the tenant and landlord. At this stage, the
         payment of HAP to the landlord has already been suspended. The letter is a final warning
         giving one week to clear all arrears or the tenancy will be removed from the HAP scheme
         ("cessation"). However, the final decision to cease a tenancy rests with local authorities, and
         many indicated in discussions with us that they exercise some discretion about the final
         decision to terminate. Based on our discussions with local authorities, their approaches vary.

they do not have access to the same benefits. These benefits include access to hardship clauses and
flexible, tailored arrears repayment plans, designed to help protect tenants in times of financial
difficulty, and the ability to raise maintenance issues directly with the local authority or approved
housing body. However, even though legislatively HAP tenants are considered to be socially housed,
local authorities generally do not apply hardship clauses to HAP tenancies nor is there permanent
access to equivalent repayment plans for arrears. This puts HAP tenants at risk of having their HAP
payments suspended should they be unable to pay off arrears. This unfairness is compounded by
the fact that they do not have the security of tenure that their counterparts in other forms of social
housing have. The cumulative effect of these inequities poses a risk, in some cases, of HAP tenants
falling into a poverty trap.

contribute to a household's financial difficulties in the first place.

an opportunity to recognise the unique implications for HAP tenants of differential rent schemes and
we support the direction of travel in this area.

guidance on differential rents, as HAP tenants are considered to be socially housed, they should
have access to hardship clauses. The application of such clauses to HAP tenants in financial difficulty
should be actioned as a matter of urgency. Furthermore, differential rent could be reduced on a
more long-term basis where a household is paying a top-up payment to the landlord due to the
shortfall between HAP and the actual rent.

DHLGH should revise the operation of HAP with regard to falling into arrears on differential rents.

7.5 Ending or moving between HAP tenancies

HAP tenancies, like all private rental agreements, may end for a range of reasons and due to a break
by either party to the agreement. However, as the Parliamentary Budget Office has pointed out,
"while HAP tenancies are afforded the same protections as other households in the private rental
sector, by not being direct tenants of a Local authority or AHB they do not have a continuous right to
have their housing need met in the result of their tenancy ceasing", resulting in a "precarity of
tenure". Where a HAP tenancy ends for whatever reason, the household is back in the position of
having to find accommodation and go through the process again, in a way that does not typically
apply to social housing. Where there is a gap between the end of one tenancy and the approval of
another, the household might find that it is without the support of HAP for a period. Twenty-four
local authorities said in response to a question on our survey that they prioritise the processing of
`HAP-to-HAP' applications, which can help to ensure continuity of support for a household in HAP.124

Aside from any break in HAP support, however, the ending of a tenancy can have other significant
consequences for a HAP-supported household, in terms of its ongoing entitlement to social housing
support. Even though HAP is based on private rental agreements, section 46 of the 2014 Act
("Change of dwelling") seeks to discourage tenants from leaving a HAP tenancy by providing that a
HAP-supported household "shall cease to be eligible for social housing support" for one year if it
moves from its accommodation within the first two years and looks for HAP in respect of another
property.

This does not apply if the tenancy is ended because the property or landlord do not meet the
requirements set out elsewhere in the Act, or if the tenancy ends due to its term having expired or
on foot of a "no-fault" notice to quit. A housing authority can also disregard the two-year
requirement if it is satisfied that the move is necessitated by overcrowding; access to employment
or educational opportunities elsewhere; exceptional circumstances such as fire or flood; or "medical
or compassionate reasons".

In principle, the two-year restriction mirrors similar provisions governing tenants of standard social
housing, who are not eligible to request a transfer to other social housing within two years of
allocation. As outlined in this chapter, however, HAP-supported tenants may have a range of reasons
for ending a tenancy that do not arise for people in standard social housing. For instance, social
housing tenants do not have to navigate landlord-tenant disputes. Catherine, whose case was
outlined above, was initially told by her local authority that leaving her tenancy would disqualify her
from HAP for two years ­ although it later agreed that she should look for accommodation
elsewhere. Standard social housing tenants do not have to make extra out-of-pocket payments to a
landlord that can mean having to give up a tenancy altogether if they become unaffordable.

There is a varied approach to how different local authorities observe the two-year rule, although all
were clear that they only permit a move if the household is not in arrears in terms of differential
rent. One local authority said that it sees the two-year period as a guideline: If a HAP-supported

Written answers

                              Minister for Housing, Local Government and Heritage

The Housing Assistance Payment (HAP) Scheme is deemed to be a social housing
support under the Housing (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2014. As a long term housing
support, an assessment of housing need must be completed in order for a household to
qualify for HAP. Any household with an identified housing need is eligible for HAP.
Under HAP, households at risk of homelessness may be eligible for additional supports.
To qualify for specific additional supports available to homeless households, a
household must have been determined by the relevant local authority to be homeless
within the meaning of section 2 of the Housing Act 1988. The operation of local
homeless services, including the Place Finder Service, is a matter for each local
authority.
In order for housing assistance to be provided under the Act the housing authority must
be satisfied that the tenancy concerned is or would be a tenancy in good faith. Where
there is a family relationship between the tenant and the landlord, the housing
authority may seek evidence of a previous landlord and tenant relationship between a
landlord and the HAP applicant. This evidence may include:
- Proof of rent payments;
- A lease or tenancy agreement for the property; and
- Registration of the tenancy with the Residential Tenancies Board.
HAP support is available to tenants in rent a room scheme arrangements, however, the
rental of a room to a civil partner, son or daughter is excluded from this scheme. HAP
cannot be paid where the tenant is seeking to rent within the family home.
In order for a housing authority to be satisfied that a tenancy is or would be in good
faith, the onus is on the applicant and landlord to prove that what is being proposed is a
bona fide tenancy. Ultimately, it is the responsibility of the housing authority to make a
decision in each individual case.

71. To ask the Minister for Housing, Planning, Community and Local Government if carer's
allowance is assessed as income when local authorities are determining rents to be charged
under the differential rent scheme. [8828/17]

       Simon Coveney (Cork South Central, Fine Gael)

Link to this: Individually | In context | Oireachtas source

The making and amending of rent schemes is the responsibility of local authorities as an
integral part of their housing management functions, subject to broad principles laid down by
my Department in Circular letter HRT 3/2002 of 6 March 2002. It is a matter for local
authorities, when assessing individual households, to consider whether rents payable are in
accordance with the authority's rent scheme. Therefore, decisions on whether or not to
disregard either a proportion of income or particular sources of income for the purposes of
calculating rents are matters for each individual local authority to consider in accordance with
their own rent scheme.

Section 31 of the Housing (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2009 allows the Minister to make
regulations in respect of various matters to be included in a rent scheme. These can include
the sources of household income that may be assessed for the purpose of determining rent
levels. Considerable work has been carried out by my Department in developing a draft
framework for a harmonisation of the approach to be taken by local authorities in regard to
various aspects of rent schemes. This includes providing for a common set of income
disregards to be applied by all housing authorities in determining a household's assessable
income for rent calculation purposes.

These proposals are now being examined further in the light of the broader commitment
given in the Rebuilding Ireland Action Plan for Housing and Homelessness, to review the
disparate systems of differential rents for social housing in place across local authorities. The
overall aim is to ensure that housing supports are fair and sustainable, prioritise those on
lowest incomes and avoid creating social welfare traps that may prevent people from either
returning to work or to the private housing market, while also recognising the fact that a
range of approaches to differential rents have become established in different local authority
areas. This review is scheduled to be completed before the end of Quarter 2 2017.

Minister for Housing, Planning and Local Government

The Housing Assistance Payment (HAP) scheme is a flexible and immediate housing
support that is available to all eligible households throughout the State. At the end of
Quarter 1 2019, there were just over 45,900 households having their housing needs met
via HAP.

HAP tenants are required to sign a rent contribution agreement to pay a weekly rental
contribution to the local authority, in line with the local authority's differential rent
scheme for social housing tenants more generally. As set out in the rent contribution
agreement, this weekly rental contribution must be paid by them so that they remain
eligible for the HAP scheme.

The right of local authorities to set and collect rents on their dwellings is laid down in
section 58 of the Housing Act 1966. The making or amending of such schemes is an
executive function and is subject to broad principles laid down by my Department
including that;

- the rent payable should be related to income and a smaller proportion of income
should be required from low income households;

- provision should be included for the acceptance of a lower rent than that required
under the terms of the scheme in exceptional cases where payment of the normal rent
would give rise to hardship; and

- appropriate local factors should be taken into account including the costs of the
maintenance and management of the stock of rented dwellings and the adequacy of the
rental income to meet such costs.

Each local authority has its own separate differential rent scheme or schemes in
operation. As rent is calculated using each local authority's own formula, the schemes
do vary in a number of ways from each other. This is particularly the case when it comes
to what may or may not be considered as reckonable/assessable income for rent
purposes e.g., disability allowance and carer's allowance.

Section 31 of the Housing (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2009 provides for the Minister
to introduce a national rents framework for social housing tenants. Considerable work
has been carried out by my Department in developing a draft of such a framework,
which has as its main aim the harmonisation of local authority rents, to ensure that rent
schemes are fair and sustainable, prioritise those on lowest incomes and avoid creating
social welfare traps that may prevent people from either returning to work or to the
private housing market. The general over-arching principle that rents should be related
to incomes will be maintained.

This work is now being examined further in the light of the broader commitment given
in the Rebuilding Ireland Action Plan for Housing and Homelessness, to review the

I expect that the review will be completed in the near future, at which point I will
consider what proposals might be brought to Government as part of a wider social
housing reform package of measures, that would continue to ensure that housing
support goes to those who need it most within a system that is responsive to people's
changing needs and circumstances. I hope to finalise this matter by the end of Q3 2019.
