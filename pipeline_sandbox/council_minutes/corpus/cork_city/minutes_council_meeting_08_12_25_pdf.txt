6.1 
ARTS OFFICE – FUNDING APPLICATIONS 
 
 
In response to the following question submitted by Comhairleoir P. Horgan, a written 
reply was circulated as outlined below:- 
 
 
To ask the Chief Executive the number of applications for funding received by the arts 
office under the heading of Comedy for 2023, 2024 and 2025 year to date and if she 
will make measures available to ensure that Comedy is recognised  as a legitimate art 
form for the purposes of grants administered by this local authority arts committees 
into the future despite the Phraseology contained in the Arts Act 2003. 
 
 
REPLY 
 
No applications for funding were received by the arts office under the explicit heading 
of Comedy in 2023, 2024 or 2025.  
 
One Bursary application by an artist identifying as a comedian was received in 2025, 
this was labelled by the applicant under the artform headings of: Film & Video; Other; 
Traditional Arts; and Multidisciplinary. 
 
Comedic artworks under the heading of other artforms such as theatre have been 
applied for and funded in that period, such as the comedy play, Taboo by Dylan 
Kennedy, which was funded in 2023 under the Arts Project Scheme. 
 
The Everyman Theatre and Cork Opera House are funded by Cork City Council 
annually and both offer a considerable programme of comedy as an intrinsic part of 
their public offer. 
 
We are guided by the Arts Act (2003) in terms of the definition of arts, which is broad 
in definition: 
 
“arts” means any creative or interpretative expression (whether traditional or 
contemporary) in whatever form, and includes, in particular, visual arts, theatre, 
literature, music, dance, opera, film, circus and architecture, and includes any 
medium when used for those purposes; 
 
When making an application for Cork City Arts Funding, applicants are invited to 
select all that apply from the following list: 
 
a) Architecture 
 
 
 
 
 
b) Arts and disability 
c) Circus 
d) Community arts 

e) Dance 
f) Digital arts 
g) Festival 
h) Film and video 
i) Literature 
j) Multidisciplinary 
k) Music 
l) Opera 
m) Theatre 
n) Traditional arts 
o) Visual arts 
p) Youth arts 
q) Other 
 
So long as the application meets the criteria of the awards and the project / 
organisation is a not-for-profit, all applications are assessed. Comedy as a distinct 
label can currently be included under ‘Other’, and we can include it explicitly in the 
list of art forms in future years. 
 
Rebecca Loughry, 
Director of Services, 
Corporate, Community & Cultural Affairs.

To ask the Chief Executive to address whether Cork City Council is, or has recently 
been, in the process of selling land it owns in Mahon, and to address whether any such 
sale of land may prioritise the local people of Mahon? 
 
 
REPLY 
 
Cork City Council is not currently engaged in the sale of land in the city suburb of 
Mahon. 
 
As part of the governance process involved in the sale of any City Council owned

6.3 
EMERGENCY ACCOMMODATION RULES 
 
 
In response to the following question submitted by Comhairleoir P. Dineen, a written 
reply was circulated as outlined below:- 
 
 
I write to ask the CEO if there is a pathway for families and or individuals who avail 
of emergency accommodation to take some approved time out for short periods of 
time from their emergency accommodation to attend family events, visit family (ie. 
Christmas etc.) if in another jurisdiction or any other event held by family members 
and friends.   
 
 
REPLY 
 
All residents in Emergency Accommodation are automatically permitted to 2 nights 
per month leave from their accommodation.  They are required to notify the 
Accommodation Placement Office APS should they wish to avail of this provision. If 
residents require additional nights away from the emergency accommodation 
provided to them this is examined on a case-by-case basis. In relation to the 24th, 25th 
and 26th of December, the APS office has approved absences for this period should 
residents wish to stay elsewhere but they are still required to notify the APS office of 
such absences in advance.  
 
Nicky Carroll,  
Acting Director of Services, 
Housing Directorate.

6.7 
RIVER USE STUDY 
 
 
In response to the following question submitted by Comhairleoir C. O’Connor, a 
written reply was circulated as outlined below:- 
 
 
To ask the Chief Executive for an update on the river-use study, including the 
proposed Cork Lido, specifically: 
 
1. The status and expected timeline for issuing the brief and tender. 
 
2. The planned stakeholder and public consultation process associated with the study. 
 
 
REPLY 
 
The brief has been developed and a specialist consultant team appointed to undertake 
a River Use Study for the River Lee.  
 

The study area will extend from the City Centre to Tivoli and Blackrock Castle. It will 
include an assessment of swim access and the potential for a LIDO with cognisance 
of potential demand, local conditions (e.g. tide/flow, water quality), scale, 
accessibility and ancillary requirements. 
 
The project will take approximately 6 months to complete with initial engagement 
with key stakeholders is planned before Christmas. The engagement plan will be 
undertaken with existing commercial and recreational river users (to include Cork 
LIDO Group, sailing/ rowing clubs, and Water Transport Providers) via 1 to1 
engagement and workshops (online & in-person). 
 
The final output will consolidate all findings, analyses, and stakeholder inputs into a 
comprehensive River Use Study Report, providing clear recommendations aligned 
with the Cork Docklands Framework Plan.  
 
Niall Ó Donnabháin,  
Director of Services,  
Planning & Integrated Development Directorate.

6.9 
LANDS AT LISHEENS 
 
 
In response to the following question submitted by Comhairleoir J. Lynch, a written 
reply was circulated as outlined below:- 
 
 
To ask the CE why lands acquired by Cork City Council at Lisheens, Ovens via 
Compulsory Purchase Order in May 2024 - for the purposes of developing a new 
shared education campus to accommodate Le Chéile secondary school and Gaelscoil 
an Chaisleáin - remain in Council ownership; why the land has not yet been transferred 
to the Department of Education; what engagement there has been with the Department 
of Education to advance the design, planning and construction phases of the project; 
and when it is anticipated the school campus will be operational. 

 
 
REPLY 
 
The land, the subject of the query, remains in City Council ownership awaiting 
completion of the formal vesting process which will subsequently enable 
advancement of the formal disposal process to the Department of Education and 
Youth. 
 
The design of the intended education campus and facilities thereon, rests solely with 
the Department of Education and Youth to advance.  Cork City Council does not 
contribute to the design stage, beyond pre-planning consultation and ultimately 
performing its role as the planning authority. 
 
The Department of Education and Youth would be the appropriate government 
department to contact to provide response to the details sought. 
 
Niall Ó Donnabháin,  
Director of Services,  
Planning & Integrated Development Directorate.

8.2.3 
INCREASE THE MEMBERSHIP OF COUNCIL 
 
 
An Chomhairle considered and approved the report of the Director of Services, 
Corporate, Community & Cultural Affairs, dated 30th October 2025 on the following 
motion:-  
 
‘That Cork City Council seek from the Government legislative change that would 
increase the membership of this Council to better reflect increased population, added 
land area and greater responsibilities brought about by the 2019 city boundary 
extension.’ 
 
(Proposer: Cllr. D. Boyle 25/526) 
 
 
The report of the Director of Services stated that the responsibility for determining the 
number of councillors per Local Authority area rests with the Minister for Local 
Government.  
 
The Programme for Government 2025 provided for the establishment of the Local 
Democracy Taskforce for the reform and strengthening of local government in 
Ireland. The Local Democracy Taskforce was indeed established in June 2024, and it 
is currently progressing with its work schedule with the aim of delivering 
recommendations to Government in the first half of 2026. In relation to councillor 
numbers, the Taskforce has been asked to: - 
“examine how the current number of councillors is determined and explore proposals 
for a framework for amendments including taking into consideration population 
growth since the 2014 reforms and the consequent need for an increase in the numbers 
of councillors to ensure appropriate representation of citizens.” 
 

Taking an evidence informed approach, the Taskforce will finalise a programme of 
recommendations for the reform and strengthening of local government in Ireland, 
which will include recommendations in relation to the framework for determining 
councillor numbers; these will be considered by Government following reporting by 
the Taskforce. Elected members are represented on the task force through the AILG 
and Cork City Council agreed to include the Taskforce as a standing item at CPG 
whilst the taskforce is continuing its work. It is recommended that CPG consider the 
recommendations of the taskforce and any proposal to increase the number of 
Councillors is predicated on those recommendations.

12.3.4 
GENDER EQUALITY AND INCLUSION IN CORK CITY FIRE SERVICE 
 

 
An Chomhairle considered and approved the report of the Director of Services, 
Emergency Management & Climate Action, and the Director of Services, People & 
Organisation Development, dated 29th August 2025 on the following motion:- 
 
 
‘That Cork City Council: 
 
1. Acknowledges the under-representation of women in the Cork City Fire Service, 
with only 2 female firefighters out of a workforce of 140, and recognises that  we 
can explore efforts in achieving gender equality. 
 
2. Notes the recommendations of the Crowe Report (June 2022), in particular 
Recommendation 17, which calls for Cork City Council to: 
 
o 
Review recruitment processes to increase gender and ethnic diversity; 
 
o 
Ensure equal opportunity for all candidates; 
 
o 
Modernise operational practices and contemporary thinking to support 
inclusion. 
 
3. Mandates Cork City Council to take the following actions. 
 
a. 
Establish a Gender Equality Action Plan specific to the Fire and 
Building  Control Department, with measurable targets, published timelines, 
and transparent monitoring. 
 
b. Undertake a full audit of fire service infrastructure, including facilities, 
equipment, and uniforms — noting that firefighting protective clothing 
already adheres to the highest national and international safety standards — 
with particular attention to day-to-day uniforms and ancillary equipment, to 
ensure they are comfortable, fit-for-purpose, and inclusive for all personnel, 
in line with best practice across comparable emergency and civil services. 
 
c. 
Develop and publish a robust Diversity & Inclusion (D&I) policy for all 
emergency services, clearly outlining a commitment to equitable recruitment, 
promotion, and working conditions. 
 
4. Calls on the Chief Executive to report to Council on the implementation progress 
of the above actions, and to engage external equality consultants if necessary.’ 
 
The report of the Directors of Services stated that Cork City Council has undertaken 
many initiatives in order to promote diversity in the Cork City Fire Service, including: 
 
• 
Increased use of social media platforms to promote a career in the Fire Service to 
a diverse audience of job seekers. 
• 
The use of promotional videos which were broadcast on social media platforms 
and which were specifically designed to encourage applications for past fire 
service recruitment campaigns from a diverse backgrounds, including specifically 
females. 
 

Links to sample recruitment information: 
• Cork City Fire Brigade launches new recruitment drive 
• JoinOurTeam. In 1995 Irene Wallace joined ccfb. A station officer in 
Ballyvolane today, this is her story. #BeTheDifference 
• Cork City Fire Brigade Recruitment 
• WATCH: Training to be a firefighter; Cork City Fire Brigade recruit new 
members for first time in eight years  
• Cork city to hire 20 firefighters in new recruitment drive – The Irish Times 
 
The numbers of female Fire Fighters within the Fire Service is dependent on many 
factors (many of which are outside of the control of Cork City Council) and include: 
• Number of applicants – which remain low despite the initiatives undertaken by 
Cork City Council (see above) 
• Successful progression through each phase of the competitive recruitment 
process 
• The attractiveness of such a physical role with unsociable hours in an economy 
with extensive employment opportunities across many different disciplines and 
work areas 
• The requirement to meet all of the qualifications for the role and achieve 
minimum standards in the required testing process. 
 
Cork City Council is an equal opportunities employer and takes our responsibilities in 
this area very seriously.  Under no circumstances are there any artificial barriers put 
in place to negatively impact on the recruitment of female firefighters.  A career as a 
firefighter makes exceptionally high physical demands in respect of certain operations 
involving firefighting and rescue duties. Employees of the fire service must be capable 
of performing these tasks at all times, as failure in the performance of such tasks could 
have fatal consequences for either the firefighter, their colleagues or members of the 
public. It is also a matter of fact that the roles and functions of a firefighter are not 
defined on gender grounds and that all firefighters are required to carry out the same 
duties.  Cork City Council is required to: 
• Protect the firefighter from harm/risk of harm (by protecting the individual's 
health and complying with its health and safety obligations) 
• Protect the firefighter’s colleagues and/or members of the public from harm/risk 
of harm 
• Ensure that Firefighter is sufficiently fit to and capable of carrying out their 
duties 
• Maintain an efficient and effective fire service, including mutual assistance to 
other emergency services when requested 
 
Modern firefighting PPE and equipment is ergonomically designed around all genders 
and is not specific to one gender.  Fire Service infrastructure, facilities, equipment, 
and uniforms are also gender appropriate, inclusive and safe for all personnel.   
 
Cork City Council’s has a Dignity at Work Policy in place and our People Strategy 
contains specific strategies and objectives in relation to Diversity & Inclusion.  
Additionally, The National Directorate for Fire & Emergency management (NDFEM) 
have recently carried out research with all Fire authorities on the topic of diversity in 

the Fire Services. It is expected that this research will be used to produce guidance 
documentation that will be issued to fire authorities.

ACCOMMODATION CENTRES FOR INTERNATIONAL PROTECTION 
 
 
An Chomhairle considered and approved the report of the Director of Services, 
Corporate, Community & Cultural Affairs, dated 11th September 2025 on the 
following motion:- 
 
 
‘That Cork City Council recognises the eviction of families, including those with 
children going to school, from Designated Accommodation Centres for International 
Protection in Cork, and supports, including by practical measures, their continued 
accommodation, at least until alternative accommodation is available.’  
 
(Proposer: Cllr. H. Kamegni 25/241) 
 
The report of the Director of Services stated that Cork City Council through the 
Community Team facilitate and have representation at the Community Response 
Forum. This platform allows for discussion on various aspects of integration in Cork 
City including challenges faced by this client group.  However, responsibilities 
relating to accommodation are with the Department of Justice, who appraise all 
accommodation settings based on criteria outlined by the International Protection 
Procurement Service (IPPS).  Predominately, these criteria are drawn from the 
International Protection Accommodation Service (IPAS) National Standards. When 
assessing accommodation, there is a minimum bed space requirement per person. 
 
All offers are subject to full compliance with requisite statutory legal 
regulations.   HIQA has now assumed responsibility for monitoring the quality of 
IPAS centres and all inspection reports are published on their website.

HGV RESTRICTION FOR LOWER KENT ROAD 
 
 
An Chomhairle considered and noted the report of the A/Director of Services, Local 
Area Development & Operations, dated 20th November 2025 on the following 
motion:-

Vehicles (HGVs) and that signage be put in place to reflect such a restriction.’ 
 
(Proposer: Cllr. D. Boyle 25/359) 
 
The report of the Director of Services stated that any proposed ban of heavy vehicles 
cannot be considered as a solution for one street only but a city-wide approach and

17.1.2 
LOWER KENT ROAD 
 

An Chomhairle considered and noted the report of the A/Director of Services, Local 
Area Development & Operations, dated 20th November 2025 on the following 
motion:- 
 
‘That this Council engage with the residents of Lower Kent Road, Ballyphehane who 
have witnessed increased levels of traffic using the road due to many factors in the 
area including changes to the junction at Curragh Road. The proposed developments 
at the former Vita Cortex and CMP sites will no doubt exacerbate the situation and a 
traffic management plan needs to be put in place. This must include signage and traffic 
calming measures.’ 
 
(Proposer: Cllr. F. Kerins 25/367) 
 
The report of the Director of Services stated that traffic calming ramps have been in 
place on Lower Kent Road for a number of years. Two out of the three ramps were 
replaced in 2024 due to deterioration. Ramp ahead signs and a Children at Play sign 
are also in place.  
 
There is a National Speed Limit Review currently being rolled out. The first stage of 
this was the reduction of the default speed limit on Rural Local Roads from 80 km/hr 
to 60 km/hr on the 7th February of this year.  
 
The second stage of the National Speed Limit Review will look at reducing speed 
limits in Urban areas from 50 km/hr to 30 km/hr, however we are still awaiting 
guidance from the Department in relation to this. Lower Kent Road can be considered 
for a reduced speed limit as part of this review.  
 
The October 2025 update on request for traffic management plan during and after 
completion of development stated that during the construction phase of any 
development, where the use of any public road is required, the contractor shall submit 
a construction traffic management plan outlining any temporary traffic management 
plans required for the development. This is immediately prior to commencement of 
the developments.  
 
In relation to traffic management for completed developments, the Cork Metropolitan 
Area Transport Strategy is the overall transport plan for the Cork Metropolitan Area. 
CMATS represents a coordinated land use and transport strategy for the Cork 
Metropolitan Area to cover the period up to 2040. One of the objectives of CMATS 
is to increase uptake of active travel and sustainable modes of transport through the 
provision of infrastructure which will have the result of reducing the number of private 
vehicles in the city in the long term. BusConnects forms part of the strategy which 
looks at the overall city in terms of the enhancement of bus services. As part of 
BusConnects, Kinsale Road forms part of the Cork City South East Sustainable 
Transport Corridors Scheme where major improvements to the route and bus services 
will be rolled out to minimise reliance on the private car in this area.  
 
In relation to the review of urban speed limits, the Department of Transport issued 
guidance on the 16th October 2025 with regard to the implementation of 30 km/hr 
speed limits in urban areas with a view to having the changes implemented by the 31st 
March 2027.  

 
The updated response in relation to the request for a defined area plan for the area in 
the vicinity of Lower Kent Road stated that Kent Road is in an established residential 
suburb developed around the middle of the last century. While there are a number of 
sites being developed in the area, this is true for many parts of the city. The City 
Development Plan provides a land-use planning framework for this area and there is 
no real benefit in preparing an additional planning-led area plan for this area. In the 
longer term, the wider surrounding area has regeneration potential and the Cork City 
Development Plan makes reference to a potential future strategy for the area.  
 
The particular matters raised by Members in the motions in relation to HGVs and 
traffic management fall under the wider transport strategy CMATS for the Cork 
Metropolitan Area. As outlined previously, the Cork Metropolitan Area Transport 
Strategy is the overall transport plan for the Cork Metropolitan Area. CMATS 
represents a coordinated land use and transport strategy for the Cork Metropolitan 
Area to cover the period up to 2040. One of the objectives of CMATS is to increase 
uptake of active travel and sustainable modes of transport through the provision of 
infrastructure which will have the result of reducing the number of private vehicles in 
the city in the long term. BusConnects forms part of the strategy which looks at the 
overall city in terms of the enhancement of bus services. As part of BusConnects, 
Kinsale Road forms part of the Cork City South East Sustainable Transport Corridors 
Scheme where major improvements to the route and bus services will be rolled out to 
minimise reliance on the private car in this area. 
 
 
An Chomhairle further agreed to refer the motion to the Transport & Mobility 
Strategic Policy Committee for further consideration.
