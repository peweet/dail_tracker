  Lord Mayor's Business 
 
The Lord Mayor sought agreement of Council to suspend standing order to allow 
extension of Lord Mayors Business beyond 10 minutes.  This motion was unanimously 
agreed.  
 

 
 
In Memoriam 
The Lord Mayor reflected on the difficult events the city has faced in recent weeks, 
noting how deeply such tragedies affect communities. He expressed condolences to 
the family and friends of Grace Lynch from Finglas, who died on 25 January after 
being struck by a scrambler bike. While welcoming the government’s announcement 
of plans to ban scramblers in public places, he stressed that Dubliners expect real, 
effective action, not just statements, especially given the ongoing issues seen across 
the City. 
 
He also addressed the tragic incident on North Earl Street, where a bus entered a 
pedestrian area, injuring several people and resulting in the death of Frank Daly from 
East Wall. The Lord Mayor offered sympathies to Franks family and those recovering 
in hospital. While the investigation is ongoing, he emphasised the responsibility of 
Councillors and the Executive to consider further measures, protections and 
interventions to improve pedestrian safety. 
He acknowledged the ongoing work by Chief Executive Richard Shakespeare and the 
Deputy Garda Commissioner on public safety and security in pedestrian zones with 
particular focus on prioritising locations where hostile vehicle mitigation measures can 
be installed.  He also welcomed the fact that all new public realm projects are now 
being designed with these protective measures in mind. A minute’s silence was 
observed. 
 
Severe Weather 
The Lord Mayor acknowledged the great work across all departments in DCC and in 
particular to Dennis Keeley, ACE, and his team dealing with the impact of the recent 
severe weather warning.  
 
Thank you 
The Lord Mayor wished to publicly thank Freeman of Dublin Jim Gavin and 
acknowledged the contribution he has made in the NEIC as he steps down from his 
role as chair of the North East Inner City Initiative. 
 
Ethics 
The Lord Mayor also advised Councillors that their Ethics Declarations are due for 
submission by the 28th February 2026. 
 
Conferral of the Honorary Freedom of the City of Dublin

Emergency Motion No. 1 in the name of Councillor Gavin Pepper 
I note the recent fatal incident in Finglas involving a scrambler bike, which tragically 
resulted in the death of a young girl, and the devastating impact this has had on her 
family and the wider community. This incident highlights the serious and ongoing 
danger posed by the illegal use of scramblers and off-road bikes in residential areas, 
green spaces, and on public roads, placing pedestrians and other road users at 
significant risk. 
  
I further note that I previously submitted a question to the Northwest Area Committee 
meeting of 15th October 2024 looking for a designated motocross / dirt track facility, 
which was not supported at that time. I know that this will not stop illegal use of 
scramblers on roads, but it might reduce the risk. For example, the one that was built 
in east wall.   
 
This fatal incident highlights the urgency of an issue that continues to pose an 
immediate risk to public safety. 
  
I believe that urgent action is required to help reduce serious injury or loss of life, and 
that a combination of enforcement, parental responsibility, and safe alternatives must 
be examined. I also recognise growing public concern that parents or guardians who 
allow children to use scrambler bikes illegally are contributing to a serious public 
safety risk.

Emergency Motion No. 6 in the name of Councillor Hazel De Nortúin and 
Councillor Conor Reddy.  
Dublin City Council recognises that tenants of Approved Housing Bodies (AHBs) who 
have been allocated housing through Dublin City Council’s housing list are 
experiencing serious and ongoing housing maintenance issues, including severe 
damp and mould, which often go unresolved. 
 
These conditions have significant and well-evidenced adverse impacts on tenants’ 
health and wellbeing. Mould exposure, in particular, has been robustly linked to 
respiratory illness, poor mental health outcomes including depression, and cognitive 
impacts, disproportionately affecting children, older people, and those with existing 
health conditions. 
 
Dublin City Council further recognises that it has a statutory responsibility to ensure 
that housing standards and regulatory requirements are upheld for all tenants housed 
through its housing list, regardless of whether properties are directly managed by the 
Council or by an Approved Housing Body. 
 
Council Notes that existing housing standards and tenant protections, are not currently 
being applied consistently where management responsibility rests with an AHB. This 
has resulted in tenants being left without effective or timely responses to serious 
maintenance issues, including damp and mould, water ingress, heating faults, and 
structural problems. 
Council Calls for an immediate audit of social housing properties allocated via Dublin 
City Council’s housing list but managed by Approved Housing Bodies, with the audit 
to: 
• 
Assess compliance with basic housing standards; 
• 
Identify systemic maintenance failures, particularly those relating to damp, 
mould, and other tenant health hazards; 
• 
Review response times, communication practices, and accountability 
mechanisms currently used by AHBs in addressing tenant maintenance 
requests; and 
• 
Produce a report with clear findings and recommendations, to be presented to 
the Housing and Social Inclusion SPC no later than 12 weeks from the passing 
of this motion. 
 
Council Directs that the findings and recommendations of this audit be referred without 
delay to the Housing and Social Inclusion SPC with instruction to establish a 
dedicated sub-committee comprising Councillors, officials from relevant Council 
departments (including Housing Maintenance and Tenancy Services), and tenant 
representatives, 
to 
oversee 
and 
support 
implementation 
of 
the 
audit’s 
recommendations. 
This sub-committee shall report to the SPC on progress made in ensuring compliance 
by AHBs with required maintenance standards and timely response obligations. 
 
Council reaffirms that we will utilise all available regulatory tools and contractual levers 
to enforce minimum housing standards in AHB-managed properties, including, where 
necessary, the use of compliance notices, financial penalties, or the reassignment of 
management responsibility in cases of persistent failure to meet obligations. 
 
We reaffirm our commitment to sustainable tenancies and housing quality as set out in 
the Dublin City Development Plan 2022–2028, including policies promoting high-
quality housing and residential amenity.  
 
 

 
Lord Mayors Decision 
The Lord Mayor agreed to take this motion without debate and will write to each of the 
CEO's of the Approved Housing Bodies.  He also agreed to refer this motion to the 
Housing and Social Inclusion SPC.

CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
No. 41 Parnell Square is a protected Georgian building of historic significance, 
including its association with the Irish National Foresters and the former Foresters Hall 
to the rear, which was constructed in 1912 and has since been demolished. 
Stabilisation works have been carried out to prevent further deterioration, ensuring the 
structure is secured while longer‑term plans are being advanced. 
 
Dublin City Council is examining the potential for the restoration and reuse of the 
building through a conservation‑led approach. This includes consideration of future 
uses that would bring the property back into productive use, including the potential for 
cost‑rental or key worker accommodation, in line with the Urban Redevelopment 
Working Group – Rejuvenation Framework and Report. 
 
Work is ongoing to determine the appropriate pathway for the restoration and reuse of 
the building. Further updates will be provided to elected members as this work 
progresses.

CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
Dublin City currently has 25 Architectural Conservation Areas and further ACAs will be 
considered for designation over this development plan period. Fifteen ACAs have been 
identified for prioritisation following careful consideration of the aspects required to 
identify areas for protection under an ACA.  An ACA is generally defined as a place, 
area, group of structures or townscape that is of special architectural, historical, 
archaeological, artistic, cultural, scientific, technical, social interest or value, or 
contributes to the appreciation of protected structures.

Conservation Area zoning within the estate currently. 
The process for bringing forward an area for ACA designation requires considerable 
resources.  Surveying & analysis, consultation & then the formal procedures for 
bringing through a variation to the City Development Plan all require resources.  As 
resources permit, ACA’s are being designated and while the CIE Estate ACA is on a 
priority list, I cannot give a timeline as to when these works will commence.

Q.33  COUNCILLOR PUNAM RANE 
To ask the Chief Executive to outline, based on current tenancy data, the number of 
local authority tenants and Housing Assistance Payment (HAP) tenants who will be 
subject to rent increases arising from the proposed amendments to the Differential 
Rent Scheme; to provide details of the average and maximum projected rent increase 
for affected households; to indicate how these impacts are distributed across 
household composition and income bands; and to confirm whether an equality 
screening and/or poverty and social impact assessment has been undertaken in 
accordance with Section 42 of the Irish Human Rights and Equality Commission Act 
2014 (Public Sector Equality and Human Rights Duty), and to summarise the key 
findings of any such assessment. 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
Dublin City Council currently has 26,645 Social Housing tenants who are charged rent 
under the Differential Rent Scheme. The differential rent scheme is an income-based 
assessment and affects tenants in different ways depending on their earnings, rents 
will vary from home to home.  
 
Today the average rent charge in Dublin City Council is €83 per week or €360 per 
month. This equates to 12% of net income. The average increase of household rent 
across all tenancies will be c. €22 per week and the average rent will amount to €108 
per week or €470 per month.  
 
The lowest rent that will be charged to the lowest income households would be €35.82 
a week or €155.22 per month. Impacts are distributed in line with scheme rules/ 
household make up and income levels. The overall changes to the 2026 Differential 
Rent Scheme are as follows: 
 
• 
The first €55 of your weekly income is not assessed. This is an increase from 
€32 under the previous scheme. 
• 
Primary Earners (i.e. the highest earner in the household) will now pay a 3% 
increase to 18% of their net weekly income, after the deduction of the 
unassessed amount of €55. 
• 
Other adults in the household can be charged up to €40 per week (previously 
€21). 
• 
If you have children under 18 years of age, you will receive a Child Deduction 
Allowance of €3 per child, per week. This is an increase on the €1 received 
under the previous scheme. 
• 
For self employed tenants, if no income details are given, an income will be 
assumed at €700 per week.  
 
The changes outlined above will come into effect from Monday, 6th April 2026. Letters 
containing the new rent charge and an information leaflet outlining how rents are 
calculated will issue to all tenants before the end of February 2026. 
 
Local authorities right to set and collect rents on their dwellings is laid down 
in Section 58(3) and Section (3A) of the Housing Act 1966 Revised Acts.  The making 

or amending of a differential rent scheme is an executive function. It is subject to 
broad principles laid down by the Department, which states that rent levels should 
reflect tenants’ ability to pay.   
The basis for differential rent is that charges are calculated based on a proportion of 
tenant's incomes, with adjustments for dependents and other household 
circumstances, each local authority sets their own rents in their own Rent Scheme. 
The Rent Review Working Group was formed consisting of 8 Councillors from across 
both SPC’s. A total of 6 workshop meetings were held from May to November 2025. 
The key objectives for the work of the rent review group were that any proposed 
changes would be:   
• 
Fair and equitable in particular for vulnerable tenancies    
• 
Proportionate   
• 
Simple to implement, administer and collect   
• 
Generate income for the Council to spend on upgrading our existing homes   
 
Key supports for tenants include additional deductions for children, increases in the 
unassessed levels to support lower paid and hardship clause for those in need. A 
Hardship Clause has also been included to support tenants experiencing exceptional 
circumstances which may be affecting their ability to pay their weekly rent charge.  
Under the Hardship Clause, the circumstances of the whole household will be 
considered as part of the Hardship Application process. All Hardship Applications will 
be reviewed on a case-by-case basis and will only be considered where there are 
exceptional circumstances. Any arrangements agreed under the Hardship Clause are 
a one-off arrangement and once the agreed term is completed the tenant must 
resume full payment of their weekly rent charge and/or rent arrears repayment 
arrangements.  
The final scheme has been approved by Assistant Chief Executive Order for 
commencement on the 6th April 2026. Additional Rental income has been adopted in 
the 2026 Budget.

Q.56  COUNCILLOR NIAL RING 
In light of the recent update on the Dublin District Heating Project which included 
details of  the search for commercial advisory services ( a commercial advisor) to assist 
in creating a detailed business case and a pricing strategy for the supply of district 
heating to Dubliners, can the Chief Executive provide an explanation for the delays in 

the overall project, given that the Dublin Waste to Energy facility has been operational 
for several years. 
 
Specifically, could the Chief Executive provide: 
A. A specific commencement date for the physical laying of the pipeline network. 
 
B. The projected date for the first home or business to receive heat. 
 
C. A breakdown of the 'pricing strategy' goals to ensure that residents, particularly 
those in the immediate vicinity of the facility who have endured its visual and 
operational impact, will receive a tangible 'community gain' in the form of reduced 
energy costs. 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
The Dublin District Heating Project (DDHP) team appointed a Technical Advisor in 
December 2023 to assist with the design, planning, procurement and construction of 
the project. A preliminary design for the network has now been completed and 
following completion of a number of surveys and confirmation of grant funding from 
central government, the team intend to commence a Part 8 Procedure in 2026.  
 
The key barriers to progress of the District Heating (DH) project to date have been 
access to grant funding and the absence of a legal and regulatory environment that 
would encourage customer connections and bring the market certainty required to 
allow investment in the emerging district heating sector.  
 
In order to mitigate some of these risks, an interim phase for the project has been 
developed, whereby the network will be operated by a contractor on behalf of DCC in 
the early years, while a procurement of a joint venture (JV) private partner to develop 
and expand the network will be progressed later. Delivering the project in this manner 
will “prove the concept” to the market, avoid loss of potential customers and establish 
the reliability and performance of the heat source in advance of the JV procurement. 
Furthermore, as large scale district heating is new to Ireland, the project will benefit 
from the additional time to develop a legal and regulatory environment with a clear 
policy framework and appropriate supports. 
The capital cost of the DH project will be 100% funded by local and central government 
in the early years while an investment in the project by a future private partner will be 
included later in order that it is appropriately incentivised to develop and expand the 
network. It should be noted that it is expected that the network will operate at a loss in 
the early years until enough customers connect to meet the Operation & Maintenance 
costs, debt repayment and cost of heat.  
The advice to the Project Team is that high density, high (heat) demand customers 
should be targeted in the early years in order to mitigate costs and establish the project. 
It is not envisaged that individual residential households will be connected for some 
time and not until the retailing & customer management part of the business has been 
developed. 
The DH company will be completely separate business to the Dublin Waste to Energy 
Company which has a significant Community Gain Fund in place. The main objectives 
of the DDHP are to contribute to Ireland’s climate and energy targets and to enhance 
the security of Dublin’s energy supply. It is not expected that the DH company will be 
in a position to repay the capital investment or make a profit for a significant period of 
time.  
In the context of the above, the responses to the specific questions are as follows:  
A. It is hoped to commence construction of the network and heat offtake works at the 
Dublin Waste to Energy facility in 2027, depending on a successful Part 8 
procedure to deliver the network and the securing of funding for the project. 

B. Assuming no delays, the earliest connection date for residential and business 
premises in the North Docklands and Irish Glass Bottle site will be in Q3 2028. 
C. The pricing strategy in the early years will be based on ensuring a viable DH 
business that can meet its debt and contractual obligations. This will be developed 
over the coming months but it is not envisaged that it will be possible to connect 
individual residential households for a considerable time or to offer preferential 
rates for the reasons outlined above.

Q.100  COUNCILLOR CIERAN PERRY 
 
To ask the Chief Executive to explain why Dublin City Council officials have decided 
not to use facial images and has restricted evidential CCTV footage to vehicles only, 
in relation to the North Inner City CCTV trial attempting to tackle the illegal dumping 
crisis in the area? 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
As one of the first Local Authorities in the State to operate CCTV fully in line with the 
new CCTV Codes of Practice, Dublin City Council made the decision to be cautious 
during the initial deployment to ensure every aspect of the scheme exceeded the 
required compliance standards while the technology, procedures, and oversight 
arrangements settled in. 
 
For future schemes, DCC will review whether the scope should be expanded to include 
facial images where necessary and proportionate. 
 
To avoid confusion, using facial images simply means reviewing standard footage 
where a person’s face may be visible. This is distinct from facial recognition 
technology, which is not permitted. Facial recognition involves automated biometric 
matching of a person’s face against a database, whereas standard facial footage does 
not involve any automated processing and is reviewed manually by authorised 
personnel only. 
 
It is encouraging that the deterrent effect of the CCTV has been strong. Early 
indications show a dramatic decrease in incidents at the monitored sites, meaning the 
scheme is achieving its primary objective of preventing illegal dumping before it occurs.

Q.134  COUNCILLOR FELJIN JOSE 
To ask the Chief Executive to explain the process and timelines for the opening 
public roads and footpaths by utility companies including: 
 
1. Why a temporary surface is required initially 
2. What minimum standard is required for the temporary surface 
3. The timeline for full reinstatement 4. Penalties for failure to reinstate within this 
time 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
1 Why a temporary surface is required initially 
A temporary reinstatement forms the first stage of a two-stage reinstatement process 
and is required where unbound granular backfill materials have been used within the 
trench. In practice, once the underlying works such as a joint bay repair or a water 
service intervention are completed, the utility provider may return to complete the 
permanent reinstatement, with a asphalt contractor to install the bound layers and 
final surfacing to the required standard. 
The purpose of the temporary surface is: 
1. Allow continuation of incomplete infrastructure works 
The temporary surface allows the service provider to return to complete the 
underground works such as a water leak, ESB joint bay or install manhole cover and 
frame. 
2. To seal and protect the backfilled area 
The temporary surface must effectively seal and maintain the backfill, preventing 
water ingress and other environmental impacts while the trench undergoes the 
necessary period of settlement. 
3. To ensure safety and serviceability for road users 
It must provide a safe, trafficable surface for vehicles, cyclists, and pedestrians, 

minimising risks associated with unevenness, potholing, loose materials, or slipping 
hazards during the interim period. 
This staged approach reflects the fact that permanent reinstatement typically requires 
different expertise, equipment and materials, particularly in the placement of bound 
layers and in achieving the required levels of compaction and surface tolerances as 
prescribed in Chapters 6 and 7 of the Guidelines for Managing Openings in Public 
Roads. 
2 What minimum standard is required for the temporary surface 
The temporary surface shall be designed to last for a minimum of six months and 
must seal and maintain the area of backfill and provide a safe and serviceable 
surface for vehicles and other road users. Delay set macadam is not permitted as 
part of temporary reinstatement. 
Temporary reinstatement must follow the construction details in the Standard 
Drawings (e.g., SD1, SD3, SD10) of the Guidelines for Managing Opening in Public 
Roads, which set minimum requirements for: 
• 
surface level tolerance, 
• 
compaction, 
• 
edge condition, 
• 
protection of adjacent pavement. 
3 The timeline for full reinstatement 
There is no single national timeline for full permanent reinstatement as roadworks 
may vary between size and complexity. The Guidelines for Managing Opening in 
Public Roads stipulate that a works complete notification must be submitted to Dublin 
City Council 90 days after the completion of the works. 
  
4 Penalties for failure to reinstate within this time 
Dublin City Council continues to experience a substantial operational burden arising 
from non-compliant roadworks. This burden includes the need for repeated inspections 
of unfinished reinstatements, the investigation of public complaints, and the revisiting 
of sites to address snags—all in accordance with the Guidelines for Managing 
Openings in Public Roads (Department of Transport, Tourism and Sport, 2017). 
In October 2023, I set out a strategy aimed at strengthening compliance with 
roadworks requirements, recognising that improved oversight is essential to protect 
the public from hazardous street works, minimise disruption to road users, and 
maintain the structural integrity of the City’s road network. At present, the only 
legislative mechanism providing for penalties is Section 101D(7)(a) of the Road 
Traffic Act 1961, which renders a person guilty of an offence for contravening a 
direction issued by the Road Authority, with liability to a court-imposed fine or, at the 
discretion of the court, imprisonment. I examined this avenue and on advice from our 
Law Dept. it was cost prohibitive and has never been utilised by the Road Authority in 
Ireland. 
As part of this review, I examined the feasibility of introducing a Fixed Penalty Charge 
to discharge criminal liability in cases where roadworks are not completed within the 
directed timeframe. However, following advice from external counsel, it is clear that 
such a measure would require new or amended primary legislation, and that the 
Guidelines for Managing Openings in Public Roads would also need to be reviewed to 
support such an approach. 
In the interim, Dublin City Council has proposed the introduction of Additional 
Inspection Charges for non-compliant roadworks. This proposal has been submitted 
to the Transport Infrastructure Network Group under the City and County Managers 
Association (CCMA) for sectoral consideration. Once these charges have been agreed 
by the CCMA, it is intended that Dublin City Council will introduce a four-month 
maximum duration for the majority of roadworks to be fully reinstated, accompanied by 
a mandatory notification of completion to the Council. Additional Inspection Charges 

will apply to specific categories of non-compliance, including the failure to complete 
reinstatement within the required period. 
It is my view that these Additional Inspection Charges will ultimately form the basis for 
a future system of Fixed Penalty Charges, designed to provide a practical and 
proportionate alternative to prosecution for non-compliant roadworks and to strengthen 
overall compliance with reinstatement standards across Dublin City. It would be greatly 
appreciated if our Elected Members would support engagement with the Department 
of Transport regarding the need for new or amended primary legislation, and the 
parallel review of the Guidelines for Managing Openings in Public Roads to ensure 
they remain fit for purpose.

Q.148  COUNCILLOR GAVIN PEPPER 
To ask the Chief Executive When a tenant passes away, what is the formal procedure 
followed by the local authority from the time of death to the securing of the property? 
Why are council properties being boarded up within as little as one week of a tenant’s 
death, in some cases before Dublin City Council has received an official death 
certificate? 
What verification process is used to confirm a tenant’s death prior to boarding up a 
property, and who authorises this action in the absence of a death certificate? 
Which individual or department within the council is responsible for signing off on the 
decision to board up a property following a tenant’s death? 
Why was the boarding up of these properties described as an “operational measure”, 
and under what policy or operational guidelines is this classification made? 
What consideration is given to next of kin or family members, particularly regarding 
access to the property, personal belongings, or time to make arrangements? 
Is this practice subject to oversight or review, and if so, how does the council ensure it 
is being applied consistently, lawfully, and sensitivity? 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
Any tenancy agreement between Dublin City Council and the sole tenant of a property 
is the contract between DCC and the sole tenant and in the event of the death of the 
sole tenant the contract ends.   
 
After the death of a tenant cases are handled on an individual basis with empathy and 
having regard to the individual circumstances surrounding each case following due 
diligence.   
 
This may involve securing the property to protect the persons belongings or to protect 
the property and/or to prevent any unauthorised occupation. 
The timeframe in securing a property depends on the specific circumstances of each 
case.        
 
In all cases Dublin City Council take reasonable steps to secure properties having 
undertaken due diligence to ascertain the dwellings were unoccupied. Dublin City 
Council must manage all vacant properties to ensure reallocation in a timely manner 
in relation to the need and unprecedent levels of demand.  In terms of communication, 
in some cases securing a property may be urgent. The Area Office Housing Team offer 
support and assistance to all families to discuss individual circumstances, following the 
loss of a family member, we endeavour to support family members during difficult 
times.   
 
As outlined above all cases are dealt with on an individual basis by the Area office and 
Dublin City Council engages with families of deceased persons in an empathetic 
manner to ensure access to personal belongings and to make any necessary personal 
arrangements. 
 
Q.149  COUNCILLOR GAVIN PEPPER 
To ask the Chief Executive what plans are in place to protect tenants who are 
receiving HAP or RAS payments, tenants in private accommodation who are on the 
housing list due to get evicted from private accommodation with the new laws being 
implemented in March? Has DCC got the capacity to house these families if needed. 
 
CHIEF EXECUTIVE’S REPLY: 
When a HAP tenant has been served a notice of termination, the tenant can source an 
alternative property and reapply for HAP support.  The tenant can be assessed for 

Homeless HAP if they have not sourced a suitable, affordable property within 12 weeks 
of the end date on the notice. 
Regarding RAS, Dublin City Council pays rent to landlords directly. Once a Notice to 
Quit (NTQ) is served to a tenant under RAS, Dublin City Council are obligated to 
rehouse the tenant as a priority in order to prevent homelessness.  
This process will be the same regardless of the new rules being introduced in March. 
However, if a NTQ is issued to the tenant as a direct result of a breach of their tenancy 
agreement, then they will not be rehoused.
